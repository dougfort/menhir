# Daily Life at Venn Court — Summer 2026

---

## Overview

The six weeks between Ned's arrival in mid-July and the start of the school term in early September establish a routine that suits both of them — shaped by Edmund's existing habits and adjusted, quietly and without announcement, to accommodate a nine year old boy. Neither of them plans it. It simply arrives.

---

## Morning

Edmund is up early, as always — by seven at the latest, often earlier. He makes tea, drinks the first cup standing at the kitchen window looking out at the yard and the orchard beyond. In the first days of Ned's stay, Edmund goes to the stone alone in the early morning before Ned is awake, as he always has.

Ned sleeps later than Edmund but not extravagantly — he is not a boy who lies in bed. He appears in the kitchen between half past seven and eight, slightly tousled, and sits at the table without requiring conversation, which Edmund finds immediately comfortable.

**Breakfast** is the meal Edmund is best at. Toast from good bread — Mrs. Perrett's, collected on Edmund's morning walks into Somerton. Eggs, scrambled or poached as a rule, occasionally fried once Edmund notices this is what Ned seems to prefer. Fruit from the kitchen garden when the season offers it — raspberries in quantity through July, then blackberries from the hedges in August. Tea for Edmund, strong. For Ned, tea made weaker with more milk, adjusted without comment, and apple juice — a good Somerset variety, purchased from the Saturday market.

After breakfast Edmund washes up — Ned dries, without being asked, from the second morning onward, which Edmund notes — and they go their separate ways.

---

## Edmund's Working Morning

Edmund's study hours are approximately nine until one. He works in the library, at his desk in the bay window overlooking the orchard — the manuscript open, the old books within reach, the sounds of the house and the grounds coming in through the window if it is warm enough to open.

He works on his current novel — a story set in the Quantock Hills, a family who find an arrangement of stones beneath their garden — moving it forward in the unhurried way of a man who has been doing this for twenty years and knows that sentences come when they are ready and not before.

He also reads for research, annotates, occasionally corresponds — a small but devoted readership sends letters, which he answers carefully and without revealing anything about himself.

Ned's activities during these hours are his own. Edmund does not organise them or check on them, which Ned finds immediately remarkable and then, gradually, exactly right.

---

## Ned's Morning — Possibilities

On fine days Ned is usually on his bicycle by half past nine. The lanes around Venn Court suit a boy alone in summer — quiet, hedged, the occasional tractor but nothing threatening.

**Somerton** is the primary destination. Two to three miles, twenty to thirty minutes at Ned's pace. He knows the market square now, the baker's where Mrs. Perrett will occasionally produce something from beneath the counter without being asked, the hardware shop where Derek nods at him as if he has always been there.

**The library** at Cox's Yard becomes a regular stop. A community-run library with a children's section, computers with reasonable WiFi — better than Venn Court's — and a librarian named Miss Pardew who is somewhere in her fifties, has very good taste in children's fiction, and recognises a serious reader when she sees one. She and Ned develop their own quiet relationship over the summer, largely conducted through book recommendations passed across the desk without much discussion.

**The local children** — found first in the market square, circling on bicycles. A gradual process of absorption, as it always is when a new child arrives in a small town in summer. Within a week Ned knows their names. Within two he is one of them for the purposes of a summer afternoon, without quite becoming one of them in any deeper sense. They know he is staying at Venn Court. This is a source of interest.

**The stone** — Ned has noticed it from the grounds but has not yet visited. The local children have views about it. He listens to these without comment.

On rainy days Ned stays at Venn Court and reads in his room, or sits in the kitchen with a book while Edmund works. The kitchen range makes even rainy days comfortable. He occasionally wanders into the library and looks at the shelves — not touching unless invited, which Edmund notices and, after a few days, resolves by simply saying: you may read anything you find here.

---

## Lunch

The lightest meal. Edmund emerges from the library at one, or slightly after, and assembles rather than cooks. Bread and good local cheddar, something from the kitchen garden — tomatoes, cucumber, whatever is ready — cold meat occasionally. In warm weather this is eaten outside at the yard table, in the shade of the woodshed wall.

Edmund would forget lunch entirely if left to himself. With Ned around it becomes a proper pause in the day. They eat without much conversation, which is not unfriendly — it is simply the midday quiet of two people who are both, in their different ways, comfortable in their own heads.

On cooler days there is soup — made from whatever the kitchen garden and the butcher in Somerton between them offer, left on the range through the morning, improving steadily.

---

## Afternoon

Edmund's afternoons are variable. Some days he returns to the library for further work or reading. Some days he attends to the estate — the woodshed, the kitchen garden, minor repairs in the stable block. He moves through these practical tasks at his usual unhurried pace, taking mild pleasure in work done with the hands.

Occasionally he walks — into Somerton, or around the estate's outer fields, or across the meadow. He does not visit the stone every day. Perhaps three or four times a week.

Ned's afternoons follow his mornings — town if the morning was spent at Venn Court, the grounds or reading if the morning was spent in Somerton. He explores the estate gradually and methodically, which is the way he does most things: the stable block, the kitchen garden, the pond in the orchard, the ha-ha. The meadow.

He has not yet gone beyond the orchard.

---

## Tea

Mid-afternoon, between three and four. Edmund makes tea without asking and it simply appears, which Ned comes to rely on without quite noticing he has done so.

There is cake occasionally — a fruit cake that Edmund produces from a tin, made rarely but capably, improving over several weeks. Something from Mrs. Perrett's if Edmund has been to Somerton. Always biscuits — digestives, reliable and unpretentious.

This is often the day's first real conversation. Ned arriving back from wherever he has been — bicycle helmet on the table, summer colour on his face — and Edmund pouring tea and asking one careful question. Ned answering accurately and without elaboration, then asking something in return. The ratio of questions shifting, gradually, over the weeks.

---

## Dinner

Edmund cooks properly in the evenings. Not elaborately, but with genuine attention and good ingredients. He cooks seasonally — what the kitchen garden, the Saturday market, and the butcher in Somerton between them offer.

**Reliable summer dinners:**

- *Roast chicken* — a Somerset farm bird, roasted slowly. Bones made into stock the next day. Edmund is the kind of man who makes stock.
- *Lamb shoulder* — Mendip Hills lamb, slow-roasted all afternoon. The smell fills the kitchen from two o'clock onward and gradually draws Ned away from whatever he is doing.
- *Fish pie* — a proper one, with mashed potato top. Edmund drives occasionally to a fishmonger in Taunton.
- *Pasta* — Edmund's concession to the modern world. A good tomato sauce, properly made, nothing from a jar. On the first occasion Edmund produces a large dish of parmesan alongside it, he discovers that a nine year old boy left unsupervised with a block of parmesan is not a conservative user. He says nothing. He simply grates more.
- *Somerset cheese and bread* — on warm evenings, dinner is sometimes simply this. Edmund sees nothing wrong with cheese for dinner and Ned quickly agrees.
- *Vegetables from the kitchen garden* — beans, courgettes, the last of the peas. Edmund treats these seriously, which surprises Ned, who has mostly encountered vegetables as an afterthought.

A pudding occasionally — something simple, an apple or blackberry crumble from the orchard fruit, or good vanilla ice cream from the Saturday market. Edmund does not make a habit of puddings but finds himself making them more often than he expected.

---

## Evening

Dinner is cleared — Edmund washes, Ned dries, established from the second night as a natural arrangement — and the evening opens out.

Edmund reads in the kitchen or the library. Ned reads in his room or the kitchen, depending on where he has ended up. There is occasionally the small television in the side room, though neither of them is much drawn to it.

In the long Somerset summer evenings the light stays until nearly ten. There is time, some evenings, to sit outside in the yard. Edmund with his tea, Ned with his apple juice. Not talking particularly. The swallows going over. The quality of light on the old stone of the house.

Edmund goes to the stone sometimes in the evening, on his own, before it gets dark. Ned has noticed this. He has not yet asked about it.

---

## Bedtime

Ned's bedtime is nominally nine o'clock, which is not rigidly enforced. Edmund calls it from the kitchen without great ceremony and Ned appears.

The reading ritual — established on the first night — has become fixed without either of them deciding it. Edmund brings the chair from the desk to beside the bed. They pass *The Little Prince* back and forth, a page or two each, until Ned's reading slows and his eyes close.

Edmund sits for a while after. Then turns off the lamp and goes along the tilted corridor.

He finds he sleeps better since Ned arrived, which he has not examined.

---

## What Has Changed Since Ned Arrived

Edmund's cooking is more plentiful. The kitchen is warmer in a way that has nothing to do with the range. The house makes its usual sounds at night — the tick and settle of old stone, the occasional creak from the Victorian wing — but they mean something slightly different now, or nothing, which is not quite the same thing.

The stone is still there at the end of the meadow, as it has always been.

Ned has not yet gone to it. But he will.
