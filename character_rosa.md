# Character Sheet — Rosa

---

## Basic Facts

- **Full name:** Rosa — surname to be decided. Something Somerset, possibly. Ferrers, Tanner, Burrough, Helyar — old county names. Or something more recent, reflecting mixed heritage.
- **Age:** Nine, turning ten in the spring — a few months after Ned's October birthday
- **Year:** Year 5, Somerton primary school. Has been at this school since Reception.
- **Family:** Somerton family, several generations on her father's side. Father runs something local and practical — a farm, a building business, something rooted in the county. Mother perhaps more recently arrived, giving Rosa a slight mixture of deeply local and not-quite-local. One older sibling, probably — a brother in secondary school who ignores her with the magnificent indifference of a fourteen-year-old, which she finds more amusing than upsetting.
- **Home:** In Somerton itself, or just outside. Close enough to school to walk. She knows every short cut.

---

## Appearance

- Dark hair, almost always slightly escaping whatever it has been put into. By mid-morning it is doing something independent.
- Brown eyes, quick and bright. Misses very little.
- Moves fast — not restless, just efficient. She has decided where she is going and is going there.
- Slightly taller than Ned, which she has not noticed and he has.
- Hands usually with something in them or gesturing. She talks with her whole body.
- School uniform worn with the mild dishevelment of someone who put it on correctly and then lived in it hard. Her socks are always regulation.

---

## Character

Rosa is a live wire. She has no obvious brake pedal — she has an idea and pursues it with complete conviction, says the thing she is thinking immediately, asks the question nobody else will ask, and decides something is interesting and announces it. None of this is malicious or reckless. She simply does not have the inhibitory circuitry that makes most people pause and ask *is this appropriate*. She has always been secure enough not to monitor herself.

She is not loud exactly. She is *present* — occupying the space she is in fully and without apology. This is the quality Ned notices first and finds most startling, having spent his whole life making himself smaller.

She is generous. When she is interested in something she wants everyone to be interested in it. When she likes someone she tells them useful things without being asked. When she thinks something is wrong she says so, directly, and then the matter is closed and she moves on.

She is not always right. She is occasionally spectacularly wrong and recovers from this with remarkable speed, usually by finding the next interesting thing.

---

## Enthusiasms

Rosa's enthusiasms are passionate, specific, and rotate every few months. She becomes an expert on whatever has currently seized her imagination and deploys this expertise freely.

**Current obsession (autumn/winter of the story): Starling murmurations.**

The Somerset Levels are among the best places in England to watch starling murmurations — hundreds of thousands of birds moving as a single organism over the flat wetlands, one of the great wildlife spectacles of the natural world. Rosa's father has been taking her to watch them since she was small. This autumn she has decided to understand them properly.

She knows:
- The behaviour is called a murmuration (obviously)
- No single bird is in charge — the coordination emerges from each bird following simple rules about its nearest neighbours
- Predators — peregrines, usually — trigger the most dramatic formations
- The best viewing spots on the Levels and when to be there
- That they are impossible to predict and that is part of the point

She does not know (yet, but is working on it):
- Why the rules each bird follows produce such apparently intelligent group behaviour
- Whether this is related to anything else in nature
- Why it feels the way it feels to watch one

That last question is the one Ned will ask, and it will stop her briefly, because she has felt it but not thought to ask it.

**Previous enthusiasms have included:** a period of intense interest in the Romans in Somerset (connected to a school trip to Bath), a brief but thorough investigation of why Cheddar cheese is called Cheddar cheese, and an attempt to identify every species of lichen in the school playground which was abandoned after three weeks not because she lost interest but because there were more species than she had expected and the field guide was expensive.

**Future enthusiasms will probably include:** whatever Ned is interested in, partly. This is new for Rosa — usually her enthusiasms are her own. Having someone who asks the right questions changes what she pays attention to.

## Rosa at School

She does well enough because she is bright, but she is not Ned-careful. She loses things — not important things, usually, but the permission slip she definitely put in her bag and the pencil case that migrated to the bottom of her rucksack. She occasionally forgets homework, which she feels briefly terrible about and then moves on from.

Mr Ashby has a complicated relationship with her. She is one of his most interesting students — her written work is vivid and confident, her contributions to class discussion are energetic and often right, and she asks questions that make him think. She is also the student most likely to make him close his eyes briefly and breathe through his nose. He is fond of her in the way teachers are fond of students who make their days less predictable.

Her best subjects are: science (she wants to know how things work), geography (she knows this landscape), English (she has things to say and says them).

Her more difficult subjects are: anything requiring sustained quiet patience. She can manage it but it costs her.

---

## Rosa and Ned

Rosa decided they were friends sometime in the second week of term, following the compass conversation on the first day. She announced this decision to herself and acted on it. Ned noticed this happening and did not object, which is as close to reciprocation as Ned manages at this stage.

What Rosa makes of Ned: he is quiet but not dull. He knows things but doesn't show off about them. He actually listens — remembers what she said three weeks ago and connects it to something new. He asks the question one step further than the obvious question. She has not had a friend who pays this quality of attention before and she finds it, without analysing it, valuable.

What Ned makes of Rosa: she is startling. She takes up space without apparently noticing that space is something you could take up less of. She is loud in ways he finds himself, privately, finding funny rather than alarming. In her company it is harder to be carefully self-contained — she makes so much noise that he doesn't have to manage how much of himself he shows. He finds this, after a while, restful in a way he couldn't explain.

The dynamic: Rosa announces, Ned observes. Rosa charges at things, Ned approaches them carefully. Rosa says the first thing in her head, Ned says the considered thing after a pause. Rosa is occasionally wrong with great confidence, Ned is cautiously right. Neither of them is trying to change the other, which is why the friendship works.

---

## What Rosa Doesn't Know About Ned

Rosa knows Ned lives outside Somerton with an older relative — she has the vague impression of something slightly unusual about the arrangement but hasn't asked directly, partly because Ned doesn't invite the question and partly because Rosa is focused outward rather than inward and other people's domestic situations don't naturally catch her attention.

She does not know about the stone, the incidents in previous houses, Ned's psychic sensitivity, or the specific quality of Venn Court. She knows Ned has a brass compass that doesn't always point north and that he gave an interesting answer when she asked about it.

---

## Rosa at Venn Court

When Rosa eventually comes to Venn Court — the birthday party in October is the most likely occasion — she will encounter the house, Edmund, the kitchen garden, and at some point, through the window or on a walk, the stone at the end of the meadow.

**Rosa and Edmund:** She will ask him something direct and slightly startling within ninety seconds. Edmund will answer properly, which will surprise her, because adults usually don't. They will get along well — very different people who share a quality of genuine interest in things. Edmund will find her refreshing in the way that people who have chosen solitude sometimes find a particular kind of noise restoring rather than draining.

**Rosa and the stone:** She will see it from the kitchen window or across the orchard and say *what's that* with the directness of someone who sees something interesting and simply asks. This is the question Ned has never asked quite straightforwardly, the question Edmund has been sitting with for thirty years. Rosa will ask it because she wants to know.

What happens after that question is part of the story's third movement.

---

## Rosa's Role in the Story

Rosa is not a secondary character who exists to illuminate the main characters, though she does this. She is a person in her own right, with her own enthusiasms and her own arc — the arc of a child who has always been confident and outward-facing discovering, through friendship with Ned, that some things are worth the slow attention she usually reserves for murmurations and nothing else.

She also does something structural: she connects the two worlds — Ned's school life and Venn Court — in a way nothing else does. Until Rosa visits, those worlds have been separate. The stone has been private. When Rosa stands in the kitchen at Venn Court and asks *what's that out there*, the private becomes, for the first time, slightly less private. This is a significant threshold.

She should not steal the show. But she should liven it up considerably.

---

## Notes on Voice

Rosa speaks quickly and in bursts. She doesn't always finish sentences before starting new ones. She uses emphasis freely — *brilliant, massive, actually, literally* (in the colloquial not literal sense), *dead good, proper, honestly*. She laughs easily and loudly.

Her questions are direct: *why does it do that / what happens if / have you ever / did you know.* She does not ask questions rhetorically — she wants the answer.

When she is excited about something, which is often, she talks faster. When she is thinking seriously about something she goes unexpectedly quiet for a moment — the quality of attention she brings to murmurations, which she learned from watching her father watch birds. Then she says the thing she has been thinking, which is usually worth the wait.
