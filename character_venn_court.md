# Character Sheet — Venn Court

---

## Basic Facts

- **Name:** Venn Court
- **Location:** Somerset, England, approximately two to three miles outside Somerton, south of the Mendip Hills
- **Size:** Forty to fifty acres
- **Character:** Old money, slightly reduced circumstances. Comfortable rather than grand.
- **Status:** The estate is old enough that locals think of it as belonging to itself. Edmund Kelway is the current version of the Kelway who lives there.

---

## The House

A farmhouse that grew into something larger over several centuries, then slowly contracted again as rooms were closed off. Built of local limestone — the same stone as the menhir — gone the colour of old honey.

### Structure
- **Medieval core:** The oldest part, now the kitchen and the room above it
- **Georgian front:** Gives the house its face and a certain worn dignity
- **Victorian wing:** On the north side, partly closed off — too expensive to heat, the rooms given over to silence and storage

### Key Rooms
- **The kitchen:** The heart of the house. Flagstone floor worn smooth by generations of feet. A wood-burning range that is always on, always warm. A table that has been there longer than anyone can trace. The back door opens onto the yard and is almost never locked.
- **The library:** The former dining room, walls entirely given over to books — floor to ceiling, slightly jumbled, a working library rather than a display. A mix of genuinely old volumes and worn paperbacks. Edmund's writing desk sits in a bay window looking out toward the orchard.
- **Edmund's bedroom:** Above the kitchen, warmest in the house. Low ceiling with a beam requiring respect. A window overlooking the yard and woodshed. Plain old furniture that has always been there.
- **Ned's bedroom:** In the Georgian part of the house. Higher ceilings, larger windows, more light. Looks out toward the orchard. On a clear day, from a certain angle, the top of the stone is just visible above the treeline. Edmund chose this room deliberately, without entirely admitting it to himself.

---

## Modern Conveniences

- **Electricity:** Yes — wired in the 1930s or 40s, partially updated since. Sockets in slightly odd places in the older rooms.
- **Heat:** Wood-burning kitchen range supplementing oil-fired central heating kept at a low thermostat. Fireplaces in the main rooms used in winter. The closed Victorian wing is unheated.
- **Hot water:** Reliable but slow — an old system that takes its time.
- **Internet:** Yes, but rural Somerset broadband. Adequate rather than fast. Edmund doesn't mind. Ned will find it frustrating.
- **Mobile signal:** Patchy at the house. Worse in the meadow. Near the stone, unreliable in ways that may or may not be coincidental.
- **Television:** One small set, kept in a side room, rarely watched.
- **What it lacks:** A dishwasher, a microwave, anything that arrived after roughly 1995 and wasn't strictly necessary.

---

## Outbuildings

- **Stable block:** Hasn't seen horses in decades. Used for storage — old tools, timber, a workbench where Edmund does occasional repairs. Dry, smelling of old wood and stone.
- **Woodshed:** Attached to the back of the house. Well stocked. Edmund takes this seriously — the range depends on it.
- **Small stone outbuilding:** Near the kitchen garden wall. Too small for animals, too solid for a simple store. Of uncertain original purpose. Possibly older than the current house.

---

## The Grounds

### Immediate Gardens
- **Walled kitchen garden:** South-facing. Productive despite neglect. Espaliered pears still fruit along the old brick inner wall. Raspberries that have gone their own way entirely.
- **Ha-ha:** A sunken boundary wall separating the immediate gardens from the meadow — a Georgian addition to keep livestock out while preserving the view. Crossed by a set of worn stone steps.

### The Orchard
Old enough to be gnarled and characterful. Some trees still produce well. Others have become more interesting as sculpture than as fruit trees. A **pond** sits in one corner — silted and ancient, ringed with rushes. Newts certainly. Perhaps something older.

### The Meadow
Unimproved grassland — the kind that takes centuries to develop. Full of plants that have no business surviving in modern agricultural England. Slopes gently downward toward the boundary. Has not been ploughed in living memory.

### The Approach
The lane to the house is unpaved for its last quarter mile, lined with ancient hedgerow. Requires care in winter. Beautiful in summer.

---

## The Stone

A single menhir at the far edge of the meadow, half screened by a blackthorn hedge that has grown up around it over centuries.

- **Height:** Fifteen to eighteen feet
- **Material:** Local limestone, gone dark with age and weather, patched with orange and grey lichen
- Leans very slightly — not enough to worry about, just enough to suggest it has opinions about vertical
- Has stood so long that the field has built up around its base — no one knows how deep it goes
- The surface is not smooth: slight concavities, marks that might be weathering and might be something else
- Seen from the house it is partly hidden by the orchard and the rise of the meadow — the top appears first on approach, then more as you descend
- The blackthorn around its base is ancient and nearly impenetrable — one gap exists that may once have been deliberate

---

## Community Relationships

- **Jim Turley:** A neighboring farmer who leases several of the outer fields for grazing. Three generations of his family have farmed around Venn Court. He and Edmund have a relationship of comfortable minimal conversation.
- **Somerton:** Where Edmund buys most of what he needs. A particular relationship with the independent hardware shop.
- **Local regard:** Venn Court is old enough that locals think of it as belonging to itself rather than to Edmund personally. There is residual deference to the estate without much deference to Edmund. He is liked, considered mildly eccentric, and left largely alone.

---

## The Stone and Local Memory

Local people know about the stone. It has accumulated a thin layer of folklore — not dramatic, just persistent. Older residents remember being told not to touch it as children, without anyone explaining why. There is a vague local sense that the stone is Venn Court's business and not a matter for outsiders. Nobody talks about it much. Nobody has ever suggested moving it either.
