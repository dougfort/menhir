# Wells, Somerset — Research Notes for Future Story Development

*Compiled during development of the Venn Court novel. Wells appears briefly in the adoption scene — Edmund and Ned attend the celebration hearing there in February. But Wells has the qualities of a future story setting and these notes are kept for that purpose.*

---

## The City Itself

Wells is a cathedral city on the southern edge of the Mendip Hills, 21 miles south-east of Weston-super-Mare, 22 miles south-west of Bath and 23 miles south of Bristol. Although the population recorded in the 2021 census was only 11,145, Wells has had city status since medieval times because of the presence of Wells Cathedral. Often described as England's smallest city.

Thinking of Wells as a city is misleading — this beautiful home of the Bishops of Bath and Wells has much more of a rural town or very large village about it. The easy confidence of somewhere that has been itself for a very long time.

Wells takes its name from three holy wells dedicated to Saint Andrew that lie within the grounds of the Bishop's Palace and cathedral and supply water to the marketplace. A small Roman settlement surrounded them, which grew in importance and size under the Anglo-Saxons when King Ine of Wessex founded a minster church there in 704.

The holy wells themselves — still visible in the grounds of the Bishop's Palace — have been sacred since before Christianity. Stone Age flints and fragments of Roman pottery have been found near the wells, and archaeological excavations revealed the remains of an intact late Roman mausoleum, probably Christian. The site has been a place of significance for thousands of years. The water still rises from the same springs.

## Wells Cathedral

The Cathedral is mostly a product of the 12th to 14th centuries and embodies the very finest Early Gothic architecture. The west front has an amazing collection of statuary set into over 300 individual niches.

The highlight of a visit to Wells is the magnificent cathedral — replete with intricate Gothic carvings, a unique scissors vault to brace the building against shifting medieval foundations, and a marvellous chapter house. Look for the staircase that rises up to the chapter house; its worn steps undulate like rolling waves.

The Wells clock still has its original medieval face, which depicts the universe with the earth at its centre. It was probably in place by the end of the 14th century. When the clock strikes the hour, knights exchange blows above the clock face and the Quarter Jack counts the quarter hours with his heels. One of the oldest working mechanical clocks in existence.

The cathedral receives over 300,000 visitors per year. Daily services continue as they have for eight centuries.

## Vicars' Close

To the north of the cathedral and connected to it by the Chain Gate is Vicars' Close, a street planned in the 14th century and claimed to be the oldest purely residential street in Europe, with all but one of its original buildings intact.

Vicars' Close was designed to provide accommodation for the Vicars Choral, who sing the daily services, and this is still the case today. This centuries-old tradition is unique and a much valued part of the continuing life of Wells Cathedral.

People have been living in these houses and singing in that cathedral for seven hundred years. The street is not a museum — it is inhabited, continuously, as it has always been.

## The Bishop's Palace

Construction began around 1210 by Bishop Jocelin of Wells but principally dates from 1230. The chapel and great hall were built between 1275 and 1292 for Bishop Robert Burnell. Stone bosses where the supporting ribs meet on the ceiling are covered with representations of oak leaves and the Green Man.

In the 14th century, Bishop Ralph of Shrewsbury had an uneasy relationship with the citizens of Wells, partly because of his imposition of taxes, and surrounded his palace with 5 metres of crenellated walls, a moat and a drawbridge. The medieval power struggle between bishop and city is written into the landscape — the moat that still surrounds the palace was dug because of a dispute.

The mute swans on the moat have been trained to ring bells by pulling strings, to beg for food. There is a tradition with the swans which is believed to date back to the 1850s, when a Bishop's daughter taught the swans to ring the bell for food. The swans still ring the bell for food, however to stop them doing it at random times throughout the day, the rope hanging from the bell is tucked into the window until the time for feeding.

Swans trained to ring a bell for food. A detail so Somerset it almost can't be real.

The wells which gave the city its name are in the gardens of the Bishop's Palace. They have been a place of worship for millennia, long before Christianity came to the area. There was a Roman mausoleum on the site, and a 7th-century church built by King Ine.

The water still rises from the same springs. You can see it.

## Penniless Porch

Tucked away in the extreme corner of the market square, a gateway leads to the cathedral. This decorative, covered gateway is known as Penniless Porch — here in the Middle Ages, beggars would congregate, protected from the worst of the weather, and beg alms of people passing to and from the cathedral.

The Penniless Porch and the Bishop's Eye are the two gateways from the market square into the cathedral precincts — one for the poor, one for the bishop. Built at the same time, in the same style, by the same hands, for different worlds.

## The Wells and Mendip Museum

Located on Cathedral Green, next to Vicars' Close. The museum offers fascinating displays bringing to life the natural history and heritage of Wells and the Mendip region. Children can explore Stone Age tools and find out about the excavations at Wookey Hole.

The bones of the Witch of Wookey are kept here — the skeleton of an Iron Age woman found in the caves in 1912, with a dagger, a knife, and a stalagmite ball. She was made into a legend by people who needed an explanation for a calcified figure in the cave.

## Wookey Hole

One mile west of Wells, in a ravine on the southern flank of the Mendip Hills. Worth knowing in detail for any story set in this landscape.

### What It Is

Wookey Hole Caves is a limestone cave system located in the Mendip Hills near the village of Wookey Hole in Somerset, England, serving as the resurgence of the River Axe and renowned for its prehistoric human occupation, geological formations, and the enduring legend of the Witch of Wookey Hole. The caves extend approximately 3,890 metres in length with a vertical range of 141 metres, maintaining a constant temperature of 11°C.

The name Wookey is derived from the Celtic word for 'cave', ogo or ogof. Hole is Anglo-Saxon for cave. Therefore, the name Wookey Hole Cave basically means cave cave cave.

### How Old

People have used these caves for about 45,000 years. Tools from the Palaeolithic period have been found here, along with old animal bones. Later, people from the Iron Age and even Roman Britain lived or worked near the caves.

Human activity in the caves dates back to the Palaeolithic era, with archaeological evidence including flint tools from 35,000 to 25,000 BC and bones of extinct animals such as hyenas, mammoths, and rhinoceroses discovered in the Hyena Den. Roman-era artifacts, including coins and pottery, indicate continued use for shelter.

### The River Axe

The River Axe flows through the caves — entering as groundwater from the Mendips, flowing through the cave chambers, and emerging at the entrance as a proper river. The River Axe flows right through these caves. For hundreds of years, this river has powered a paper mill at the cave entrance — one of the oldest paper mills in Britain.

### The Witch

The cave is notable for the Witch of Wookey Hole, a roughly human-shaped stalagmite that legend says is a witch turned to stone by a monk from Glastonbury.

The legend: a monk from Glastonbury, seeking revenge on a witch who had cursed a romance, followed her into the caves and blessed the underground water, splashing it at her hiding place. She was immediately petrified. She stands in the first chamber still. In 1912, a 1,000-year-old skeleton of a woman was found in the caves, with a dagger, a knife, a billhook, and a stalagmite ball — this is probably the basis of the legend.

The witch as story is interesting: an old woman, living alone in a cave, blamed for the failures of other people's lives. Then turned to stone by a man with holy water. Then her bones displayed in a museum as a curiosity.

### Cave Diving

In the 1920s, the main business was the paper mill. The commercial show cave opened in 1927.

The caves were the site of the first cave dives in Britain, undertaken in the 1930s. Since then, divers have explored the extensive network of chambers. The full extent of the cave system is still unknown with approximately 4,000 metres, including 25 chambers, having been explored.

The deepest parts of the cave go down to 26 metres below sea level. The end of the explored system is still not reached. The cave continues.

### The Cheese

The constant temperature of 11°C in the caves is used by Ford Farm of Dorset to mature Cheddar cheese in the 'Cheese Tunnel' — an excavated side tunnel between the ninth chamber and the exit.

Cheese maturing in the dark, in a cave that has been used by humans for 45,000 years, one mile from England's smallest city. Very Somerset.

## Why Wells for a Future Story

The quality Wells shares with the stone at Venn Court: old in a way you can feel rather than just understand. The water rises from the same springs it always has. The swans ring the same bell. The steps of the chapter house undulate with seven centuries of use. The bones of an Iron Age woman sit in the museum two hundred yards from where she died.

The deep past is not distant in Wells. It is present, continuous, leaking into the contemporary through springs and worn stone and a bell rope tucked into a window until feeding time.

A story set in Wells — or using Wells as a touchstone — would have access to this quality. The cathedral. The holy wells. Wookey Hole a mile away, its river flowing through chambers where Palaeolithic hunters sheltered 35,000 years ago. The Mendips above it all, the limestone full of water and time.

The smallest city in England, entirely confident in what it is, having been itself for rather longer than most things.

## Practical Notes

- Market day: Wednesday and Saturday in the market square
- The Bishop's Palace is open to visitors; the moat is walkable even when the palace is closed
- Wookey Hole is a mile west of Wells on the B3139
- The Wells and Mendip Museum is on Cathedral Green, adjacent to Vicars' Close
- The cathedral has daily services; the clock strikes the hour with the jousting knights
- Parking: several car parks near the market square; the city is small enough to walk entirely
- Distance from Somerton (fictional Venn Court area): approximately 12 miles
