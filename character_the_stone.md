# Character Sheet — The Stone

---

## Basic Facts

- **Type:** Single menhir — a standing stone, erected by human hands in prehistory
- **Height:** Fifteen to eighteen feet above ground. No one knows how deep it goes.
- **Material:** Local limestone, the same stone as Venn Court itself, but far older in its working. Gone dark with centuries of weather, patched with lichen in orange, grey, and black.
- **Location:** At the far end of the meadow beyond the orchard, three to four hundred yards from the back of Venn Court. A destination rather than a waypoint.

---

## The Mound

The stone stands on a low rise — three to four feet above the surrounding meadow. Whether this is a natural limestone outcrop or the long-eroded remnant of a man-made barrow is unclear and probably undecidable. The ambiguity suits it. The mound gives the stone elevation enough to be visible from the upper windows of the house on a clear day, and from the far side of the orchard on approach.

---

## Appearance

The stone leans very slightly — not enough to suggest instability, just enough to suggest it has its own relationship with vertical. It is not smooth. The surface has texture — shallow concavities, faint marks that might be the work of weather over millennia and might be something else. It rewards close looking without quite answering it.

At its base the ground is bare or nearly so — not dramatically, not in a perfect circle, but the grass thins and shortens as you approach, as if the immediate vicinity of the stone operates by slightly different rules. No blackthorn or woody scrub grows near it. The vegetation simply declines to.

The stone is wider at the base than at the top, tapering irregularly. From a distance it has an upright, attentive quality. From close up it is simply enormous — larger than it appears from the house or the orchard, a fact that strikes every visitor freshly.

---

## The Sitting Boulders

Two boulders sit slightly apart from the stone's base — not directly against it, but close enough to feel its presence and far enough to take it in whole. They are of the same limestone, lower and rounder, their upper surfaces worn smooth in a way that requires either centuries of weather or centuries of use, or both. They are positioned naturally enough that they might be accidental. They are not accidental.

They are comfortable to sit on in the way that only very old things shaped by long use can be comfortable. Edmund uses one habitually. When Ned comes he will find the other without being directed to it.

---

## Alignment

The stone is aligned to something. Edmund has his own quiet theory — the midsummer sunset, a notch in the distant Mendip ridge, a star that would have been significant to whoever raised it. He has never spoken this theory aloud. He is not entirely certain it matters, and yet he is not certain it doesn't.

---

## Distance and Visibility

- **From the house:** Partly hidden by the orchard and the rise of the meadow. The top of the stone is visible from certain upper windows on clear days.
- **On approach:** The top appears first above the orchard treeline, then more of it reveals itself as you cross the meadow and the mound rises to meet you.
- **By moonlight:** Visible from Ned's bedroom window on a clear night — a dark vertical shape against the sky, the slight lean just perceptible.
- **Mobile signal:** Unreliable near the stone in ways that are not entirely explicable by geography.

---

## The Feeling of the Place

This is the hardest thing to document and the most important.

The stone is old beyond any useful comparison. It was already ancient when the Romans came to Somerset. It was standing when the first Christians built their first churches in England. It has no memory of anything that feels recent. To stand near it is to feel one's own life — and everything in it — become briefly, vertiginously small.

This is not unpleasant. It is closer to the feeling of looking up at a clear night sky than to anything frightening. A recalibration of scale.

Edmund comes here to think and to not-think in roughly equal measure. He sits on his boulder and the ordinary noise of his mind gradually settles. Time passes differently near the stone — not faster or slower, exactly, but less insistently.

Ned will feel this too, on his first visit. He will not have a word for it. He will sit on the second boulder and feel, for reasons he cannot explain, that the stone is aware of him. Not threateningly. The way a very old and very patient thing might be aware of something small and brief that has come to sit near it.

---

## Local Memory

The stone is known locally. It has no official name, though older Somerton residents sometimes call it the Venn Stone or simply the Stone — always with a slight capital letter in the voice. There is a thin persistent folklore around it, nothing dramatic: older residents remember being told not to touch it as children, without explanation. Nobody has ever seriously suggested moving it. Nobody has ever explained why not.

It appears on Ordnance Survey maps as a listed ancient monument. This confers a certain official importance that somehow makes it feel no more explicable than before.

---

## Notes on Its Role in the Story

The stone is the third character. It does not speak, act, or transform in any conventional sense. Its power is its age and its patience. It has been here longer than anything else in the story and will be here long after. It is the fixed point around which everything else moves.

What it means — if it means anything — is not for the stone to say.
