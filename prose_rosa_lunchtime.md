# Prose Draft — Rosa (A Lunchtime, Late September)

---

It was a Thursday, which meant shepherd's pie, which Ned had established was one of the better options. He had found a table at the end of the row nearest the window — not hidden exactly, just slightly separate — and was eating with one hand and reading with the other, which was technically against the rules but which nobody had yet asked him to stop doing.

Rosa sat down across from him without asking.

This was the third time she had done this. The first time Ned had looked up, assessed the situation, and returned to his book. The second time she had talked to him about a television programme he hadn't seen and he had listened without contributing much and she had not seemed to mind. The third time — today — she put her tray down and said, before she had even properly sat:

"Have you ever seen a murmuration?"

Ned put his finger in his book. "No."

"Right, so — " She sat. Picked up her fork. Put it down again. "My dad took me to the Levels last night, right, to the viewing spot at Shapwick Heath, and there were maybe — we think maybe three hundred thousand starlings. All at once. Moving together." She made a shape with both hands that was apparently meant to represent three hundred thousand starlings. "Like — you can't describe it. It's like the sky is alive. Like the whole sky is one thing."

"How do they do it?" Ned said. "Without crashing into each other."

"Each bird watches the seven nearest birds. That's it. That's the whole rule. You just watch the seven birds next to you and do what they do, and somehow — " She did the hand thing again. "Somehow that produces this. Which makes no sense. Which is the brilliant bit."

Ned put his book down. "Seven specifically?"

"Seven. There was a study. Italian scientists, I think — they did this massive mathematical analysis of photographs, thousands of photographs, working out which birds were responding to which birds." She picked up her fork again and started eating, which with Rosa meant eating and talking simultaneously, which she managed with competence if not elegance. "Seven's the number that works. Less than seven and the information doesn't spread fast enough. More than seven and — I forget what happens with more than seven but it's worse."

"The information."

"Yeah — so if a peregrine comes at them — that's what usually triggers the big formations, a predator — one bird reacts, and that reaction spreads, right, like — it travels through the flock at something like twenty metres a second." She paused to eat more shepherd's pie. "Twenty metres a second. A hundred thousand birds all changing direction in basically no time. No one giving orders. No one in charge."

Ned looked at her for a moment.

"What does it feel like?" he said. "To watch one."

Rosa stopped.

This was the question. She had told people about murmurations before — her mother, her brother (who had said *cool* and gone back to his phone), her friend Priya who had listened politely. She had explained the seven-bird rule and the Italian scientists and the twenty metres a second. Nobody had asked that.

She put her fork down.

"It feels like — " She looked at the table for a moment. This was new, the brief stillness, different from her usual quick-fire delivery. "It feels like there's something happening that you can't quite look at directly. Like if you tried to watch one specific bird you'd miss it, but if you watch the whole thing at once you can — kind of — see something that isn't any of the birds." She looked up. "That sounds stupid."

"It doesn't," Ned said.

"It's hard to describe."

"Most things that are actually interesting are hard to describe."

Rosa looked at him. This was also new — not the sentiment exactly, but the way he said it. Matter-of-fact. Not trying to make her feel better about the inadequate description, just agreeing that the phenomenon was genuinely difficult.

"Yeah," she said. "Exactly." She picked her fork back up. "Do you want to come? Next time my dad goes. He goes most weekends when they're roosting, October through January. He wouldn't mind."

Ned thought about this. "I'd have to ask Edmund."

"Who's Edmund?"

"He's — I live with him. He's a relative."

"Oh." Rosa accepted this with the completeness of someone who does not require more information than she has been given. "Is he strict?"

"No."

"Then he'll probably say yes." She said this with the confidence of someone whose father always said yes to reasonable things. "The best time is just before dusk. You have to wait and then it just — starts. And you never know how big it's going to be."

"Edmund would probably find it interesting," Ned said, half to himself.

"He can come too." Rosa had already moved on from whether Edmund might come to wondering what to have for pudding, which was either crumble or something less good. She craned her neck to look at the serving hatch. "Is that apple crumble do you think or is it rhubarb."

"Apple," Ned said. He had checked earlier.

"Brilliant." She stood up, tray in hand, apparently finished with the conversation and ready for the next one. Then she stopped. "Oh — that compass. The one from the first day. Do you actually use it for navigation?"

"Sometimes."

"Of which direction?"

"It depends," Ned said, which was true and also not quite an answer, which Rosa appeared to notice.

She looked at him for a moment with a specific attention — different from her usual quick scanning, something that had more focus in it, that reminded Ned slightly of the way she had looked at the table when she was thinking about how murmurations felt.

"Right," she said. "Okay." And went to get crumble.

Ned watched her go. Then he picked up his book. Then he put it down again, because he was thinking about three hundred thousand starlings watching seven birds each and somehow producing something that looked like a single intelligence moving across the sky, and the book was less interesting than that.

He had a question forming. Several questions, actually, accumulating in the part of him that accumulated things. He wrote two of them in the small observation notebook in his jacket pocket, because he wanted to ask Edmund and he didn't want to forget the exact form of them before he got home.

Then he picked up his fork and finished his shepherd's pie.

---

*When he told Edmund about the murmurations that evening — over dinner, not immediately, letting it find its own moment — Edmund put down his fork and listened with his full attention, which was what Edmund did when something interested him.*

*"Seven birds," Edmund said.*

*"Seven nearest birds. That's the whole rule."*

*Edmund was quiet for a moment. "I've read about this. The mathematics of it." He picked up his fork again. "The individual rule is simple. The collective behaviour is — not simple."*

*"Rosa says it feels like something you can't look at directly."*

*Edmund looked at him. "How old is Rosa?"*

*"Nine. Same as me."*

*Edmund looked back at his dinner. "She sounds," he said carefully, "like someone worth knowing."*

*"She's coming to watch a murmuration on the Levels next time her dad goes. She said we could come. Both of us, she said."*

*Edmund was quiet for a moment. "When?"*

*"October probably. When they're roosting properly." Ned paused. "She said her dad wouldn't mind."*

*"In that case," Edmund said, "I don't see why not."*

*He said it as if it were a small thing. As if a nine-year-old girl inviting him to stand in a Somerset field at dusk and watch three hundred thousand starlings was the kind of thing that happened. Perhaps, Ned thought, at Venn Court it was.*

---

*Draft notes: The scene does several things. Rosa's murmuration speech establishes her voice — quick, enthusiastic, specific, knowledgeable, with the competent multitasking of someone comfortable in the world. The seven-bird rule is genuinely interesting science, which means the conversation has real content rather than just being characterisation. Ned's question — what does it feel like to watch one — is the pivot: it is the question Rosa has not been asked, the one that stops her briefly. Her answer — something you can't quite look at directly, if you watch one bird you miss it — resonates with everything Ned knows about the stone without either of them knowing this. The compass question at the end is Rosa circling back, showing she has been thinking about it, giving Ned a slightly evasive answer and Rosa noticing the evasion. The scene ends with Ned writing questions in his notebook — the reflex of a boy who accumulates things carefully. The dinner coda introduces the murmuration plan and gives Edmund his response: *she sounds like someone worth knowing.* Edmund saying this carefully, which means he means it precisely. And Ned's final observation — that at Venn Court, perhaps, this kind of thing happened — is a small moment of his understanding of what Venn Court is becoming.*
