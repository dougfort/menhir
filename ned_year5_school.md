# Ned at School — Year 5 Curriculum and What He Makes of It

---

## Overview

Ned begins Year 5 at the Somerton primary school in early September 2026. The school follows the national curriculum for England, with the core subjects of English, Maths and Science supplemented by the foundation subjects: History, Geography, Art, Design and Technology, Computing, Modern Languages (French), Music, Physical Education, Religious Education and PSHE.

For a boy who has been passed through several schools in several towns, Ned arrives with a functional knowledge of how classrooms work and a private scepticism about how much they usually offer him. He is about to be surprised.

---

## English

His best subject by some distance, and the one most likely to make his teacher stop and look at him properly.

Ned reads widely and well above his level — currently working through *The Fellowship of the Ring* at bedtime, having previously read Machen and Blackwood with Edmund. His comprehension is excellent. His vocabulary is unusual for a nine year old: precise, slightly formal, occasionally surprising. He uses words with care.

His creative writing is the most striking thing. Year 5 English asks pupils to consider how characters and settings can be developed by drawing on knowledge of other texts. A boy whose imaginative world has its own geography, its own rules, its own history — drawn in careful notebooks for three years — brings something different to this task. His first piece of writing in Year 5 would be unusual. Precise and strange in equal measure. His teacher would not quite know what to put on it.

He would also be quietly distinguished in reading comprehension — the ability to attend carefully to a text, note what is and is not said, and draw accurate conclusions without over-interpreting. Ned has been doing this with people for years. Doing it with written texts is considerably easier.

**Story thread:** His teacher contacts Edmund at some point — perhaps parents' evening, perhaps before — about Ned's writing. The conversation Edmund has about his ward's creative work would be something to draft. Edmund receiving the teacher's mild concern with complete equanimity.

**Reading diary:** Most primary schools ask children to record home reading. Ned's entries — *currently reading The Fellowship of the Ring; previously The Tarn of Sacrifice by Algernon Blackwood* — would be a source of quiet interest.

---

## Maths

Competent and methodical. Year 5 maths covers place value, the four operations, fractions, and arithmetic throughout. Ned does it carefully, which produces sound results. It is not his passion but he respects its clarity — problems with definite answers, unlike most of the things that interest him.

He would be particularly good at any maths that involves spatial reasoning or pattern — geometry, data representation. Less interested in the purely procedural.

---

## Science

The autumn term typically covers **Earth and Beyond** — the solar system, planets, what causes day and night — and **Properties and Uses of Materials**.

Earth and Beyond catches Ned immediately. The scale of the solar system, the age of the universe, the way it makes everything feel small and provisional — he already has some instinct for this from the stone. The solar system is ancient, but the stone predates anything the solar system produced on Earth that could appreciate the fact. He does not say this in class. But he takes the lessons home in his head and thinks about them.

Properties of Materials is more workmanlike. He applies his usual careful attention.

---

## History

A common Year 5 autumn history topic covers **Anglo-Saxons and Vikings** — invaders and settlers, how they changed Britain. Some Somerset schools include local history, which in this county means an extraordinarily rich field of prehistoric, Roman and medieval material.

Ned is genuinely interested in history, particularly the deep past. A lesson touching on prehistoric Britain — on the people who were here before the Romans, before writing, before anything traceable — would make him go very still. His teacher might notice this and not quite understand it. He already knows, from Edmund, that the stone predates all of this — that whoever raised it was already ancient by the time the Anglo-Saxons arrived. History class provides a framework for something he has been feeling without having the vocabulary for it.

**The local history possibility:** A Somerset primary school might well include a unit on local prehistoric monuments. Somerset has Glastonbury, the Mendip caves, numerous standing stones and barrows. A teacher mentioning menhirs in the context of the local landscape — Ned sitting in the third row saying nothing, knowing rather more about this than anyone in the room, including the teacher.

---

## Geography

Map skills. Physical geography. Local area studies.

Ned is a mapper by nature. He has been drawing maps of his imaginary world for three years — precise, internally consistent, annotated. Map skills in geography class would be the one curriculum subject that directly connects to what he does in his notebooks. His maps would be more elaborate and more accurate than anyone else's. His teacher would hold one up as an example, which Ned would find mildly uncomfortable.

The local area study — fieldwork in and around Somerton — would give Ned a legitimate school reason to look at the landscape he is already attending to closely. Contour lines, drainage patterns, the way the Levels spread west of the town. He would bring a quality of attention to this fieldwork that the task probably does not require but that he cannot help.

---

## Modern Languages — French

The first unit typically covers animals and pets. Later, days and months.

Ned's mild interest is sharpened slightly by the knowledge that *Le Petit Prince* was originally French — Edmund mentioned this. He might begin looking at the original language in the evenings, comparing it to the English version they read together. He would ask Edmund about particular words. Edmund, who can read French competently if not fluently, would engage with this seriously.

---

## Computing

Programming. Count-controlled loops. Creating simple programs.

Ned approaches this with the same methodical competence he brings to maths. He gets the logic of it. He finds it less interesting than most things but more interesting than he expected.

---

## Art and Design Technology

Art: drawing, painting, possibly printmaking or sculpture depending on the school.

Ned draws constantly — the maps, the characters from his inner world, the increasingly detailed sketches in the margins of his notebooks. His art teacher would find him unusual: technically careful for his age, with an eye for spatial relationships and texture. Not especially interested in colour or abstraction. Very interested in the representation of form and place.

Design Technology: practical making. Ned's kitchen-garden competence — the careful attention, the willingness to follow instructions and then adapt them — serves him well here.

---

## Music

Ned has opinions about music that he keeps almost entirely to himself. He listens to it on his phone during the bicycle ride to Somerton in the mornings, earphones in, which Edmund has noted and not commented on. What he listens to is private.

Music lessons at school are more communal than private. He participates without distinguishing himself. He can hold a tune.

---

## Physical Education

Twice a week. Team sports and individual activities.

Ned is small and not immediately impressive on a football pitch, which is where most PE assessments happen informally among nine-year-olds. But he is precise and patient, which rewards certain sports disproportionately — swimming, if the school has access to it; orienteering, if it comes up; any activity that rewards careful attention over physical dominance.

He would not be the last picked. He would not be the first. He would be the one who turns out to be better than expected at things nobody initially noticed he was doing.

---

## Religious Education

In Somerset, with its strong Church of England tradition, RE would cover Christianity alongside other faiths. Discussion of sacred places — churches, temples, sites of worship — would occasionally touch something in Ned without his being able to explain why to anyone in the room.

The concept of a place being set apart — consecrated, different in quality from the surrounding world — is one he already has direct experience of. He would listen to these lessons more carefully than the teacher might expect from a quiet boy at the back.

---

## PSHE — Personal, Social, Health and Economic Education

Year 5 PSHE typically covers healthy and happy friendships, identity, peer pressure, and emotional wellbeing.

For a boy who has never had a friendship with actual history — who knows how to be easy with other children for a term but has never had the slow accumulated intimacy of a relationship that spans years — the friendship units would be quietly significant. He would think about them on the bicycle ride home and not quite put them into words.

The identity unit — *who are you, what makes you you* — would be one of the more genuinely difficult things Ned encounters in Year 5. Not because he doesn't know who he is, but because the question of whether identity is fixed or made, whether you are the same person in a different place, is one he has been living for three years without the vocabulary for it.

He would write something careful and slightly elliptical in his PSHE journal. His teacher would read it and pause.

---

## Homework

The load is light — a small Somerset state primary in 2026 does not believe in burdening nine year olds. Perhaps twenty to thirty minutes on three or four evenings a week.

**Reading** — the most consistent expectation. Ten minutes a night, reading diary checked on Fridays. Not homework for Ned in any meaningful sense. He is already reading *The Fellowship of the Ring* for stretches at a time.

**Spellings** — a weekly list, tested on Fridays. Ned learns them efficiently and finds them straightforward.

**Maths** — one or two worksheets a week. Twenty minutes. Done at the kitchen table with methodical attention while Edmund finishes dinner or reads at the other end.

**Projects** — half-termly research tasks linked to the current topic. History, geography, science. These take longer and are more interesting. A project on the Anglo-Saxons, or the solar system, or local Somerset history, would connect privately to things Ned is already thinking about.

The homework does not dominate the evenings. The kitchen remains what it has been — the range, the table, the comfortable silence. The worksheets are simply a new texture in it, brief and unremarkable.

**The reading diary** — a small comedy. Ned's entries on Fridays: *currently reading The Fellowship of the Ring, chapters 12-15. Previously: The Tarn of Sacrifice, Algernon Blackwood. Previously: The Ceremony, Arthur Machen.* Some teacher reads this list on a Friday afternoon and pauses. Edmund, finding the diary on the kitchen table one evening, reads it and says nothing. He finds it privately, exactly right.

## Edmund and School Events

A small Somerset primary asks parents — or whoever is responsible — for relatively little. Edmund manages each event with his usual serious attention and finds himself, repeatedly, in situations he has no prior experience of.

### Parents' Evening — Twice a Year

The most significant event. An after-school appointment, ten minutes with Ned's class teacher, a small chair across a small table in a primary school classroom.

Edmund books his slot, prepares questions, and arrives punctually. He has never attended a parents' evening before. He finds the institutional setting — strip lighting, children's artwork on the walls, a teacher perhaps twenty-five years younger than him — interesting rather than uncomfortable, the way he finds most things interesting. He is courteous and attentive and asks precise questions that make the teacher look at him slightly differently.

The teacher mentions Ned's reading diary. His creative writing — unusual, precise, slightly strange. The geography maps. Edmund receives all of this with equanimity. He asks one or two careful questions. The ten minutes are sufficient. Both of them leave with things to think about.

**The first parents' evening** is a scene worth drafting. Edmund off his home ground entirely, in an institution with its own codes he is not fluent in. Ned, at home, waiting. The debrief over tea when Edmund returns — what was said, how much Edmund shares, how Ned responds to the fact of Edmund having been there at all.

### The Christmas Concert — December

The school hall. Folding chairs. Thirty other parents. Year 5 performing something — carols, a short piece, something rehearsed for weeks.

Edmund sits among the other parents with the slight anomaly he carries into most public settings. He would find the noise and organisation mildly overwhelming and manage it with dignity. He might be unexpectedly moved by something — Ned's face in the performance, the singing, something small and ordinary that arrives without warning.

**Ned knowing Edmund is there.** Ned has never had anyone come to a school event for him. Whatever his face does when he spots Edmund in the hall — that is worth a sentence. Ned not expecting it to matter as much as it does.

### Class Assembly — Once a Year

Each class performs one assembly per year, parents of that class invited. Year 5's assembly would happen at some point in the spring or summer term. Edmund attending, sitting in a row of parents, watching Ned stand up and read something or perform a part.

### Sports Day — Summer Term

Beyond this story's current scope, but notable: Edmund standing at the side of a school field in June, watching Ned run. Edmund not being a person who has attended a sports day before. Edmund managing this with his usual equanimity, possibly bringing a thermos.

### Permission Slips and the School App

The school uses an online app for communications. Edmund engages with it dutifully and finds it mildly baffling. He would prefer a letter. He manages.

Permission slips arrive in Ned's school bag. Edmund reads them carefully, fills them in with deliberate attention to detail, signs them. He has never signed a school permission slip before. He treats each one as a document requiring his full consideration, which Ned finds slightly comic and does not say so.

### The Signing of the Reading Diary

Each Friday, Edmund signs Ned's reading diary to confirm he has read that week. This is a small weekly ritual — Edmund writing his name in a child's exercise book, confirming that yes, *The Fellowship of the Ring* was indeed read in this house this week, along with various other things the school's reading diary was probably not designed to accommodate.

He signs it without comment. It is the smallest possible act of parental responsibility, performed weekly, and it matters more than it appears to.

---

## What School Gives Ned That Venn Court Cannot

Edmund gives Ned the stone, the library, the kitchen garden, the long evenings and the reading ritual. School gives him something different: the ordinary social texture of being ten years old in a small Somerset town, among people his own age.

He needs both. The stone and the classroom. The menhir and the reading diary that says *currently: The Fellowship of the Ring*.
