## Stanton Drew — The Key Site

### What It Is

Stanton Drew is the second largest stone circle complex in Britain, after Avebury, located in the village of Stanton Drew in Somerset, approximately fifteen miles from the fictional Venn Court (near Somerton). It consists of three stone circles, two stone avenues, and a separate group of three stones called The Cove.

The Great Circle is 113 metres in diameter and originally consisted of approximately 30 stones, of which 27 survive. Most are now recumbent — lying in the grass at angles, not standing. A visitor has to read the landscape rather than simply see the monument. The circles are thought to date from between 3000 and 2000 BCE, placing them in the Late Neolithic to Early Bronze Age.

Geophysical surveys carried out by English Heritage in 1997 revealed nine concentric rings of postholes inside the Great Circle — more than 400 pits, each about a metre across, where massive wooden posts once stood. The monument is far larger and more elaborate underground than it appears above ground.

### The Cove

Three large limestone slabs standing in a horseshoe arrangement in the garden of the Druid's Arms pub. They predate the stone circles by approximately a thousand years (c. 4000–3000 BCE) and are thought to have formed the entrance to a chambered tomb. The pub was built around them. The incongruity — a Neolithic tomb in an English pub garden with picnic tables — is part of the site's character.

### Local Legends

The best-known legend holds that a wedding party was turned to stone for dancing and making merry on the Sabbath. A man in black (the Devil) came to play for them and would not stop. This story pattern is common to many stone circles in England and Wales.

A further local claim, taken seriously by some visitors, is that one of the stones will violently repel anyone who pushes it hard enough. Probably nonsense. Exactly the kind of thing Will Haddon would have found on the internet.

### Practical Details (for visiting or describing)

- Address: Stanton Drew, Somerset, BS39 4EW (for the circles); BS39 4EJ (for the Cove)
- OS Grid Reference: ST601633
- Access: The circles are on private farmland. There is an honesty box at the gate — £1, as described in the chapter. The Cove is open access via the Druid's Arms pub garden.
- Managed by English Heritage

## The Somerset Levels

### What They Are

The Somerset Levels are a low-lying plain in central Somerset, extending roughly between the Mendip Hills to the north and the Quantocks and Blackdown Hills to the south. They were a shallow inland sea and coastal marsh in prehistoric times. The landscape has been drained over centuries — primarily from the medieval period onwards — and is now agricultural land crossed by drainage channels called rhynes.

The extraordinary flatness of the Levels is visible from the train between Castle Cary and Taunton — the view Ned sees on his first journey to Venn Court. Villages and farms sit on slight rises barely above the surrounding plain. Glastonbury Tor rises dramatically from the flat land, which was its quality in prehistoric times too — an island in the marsh, which is why it was sacred.

### The Sweet Track

Built in 3806 BCE, dated precisely from tree-ring analysis of the timber used. The oldest known road in Britain and one of the oldest in the world. A wooden walkway approximately two kilometres long, crossing the marsh between what are now the Polden Hills and the Isle of Avalon (Glastonbury area).

Construction: ash, oak and lime trees were felled and split into planks, which were laid in a herringbone pattern on poles driven into the peat. Very precise, very well made. The peat preserved it entirely — without the anaerobic conditions of the peat, it would have rotted to nothing four thousand years ago.

The Sweet Track is older than the Egyptian pyramids by approximately 1,300 years.

Preserved sections are held by the British Museum and the Peat Moors Centre near Glastonbury. Search *Sweet Track Somerset* for images of the preserved wooden planks.

### For a Visual Sense of the Levels

Google Maps satellite view of the area between Glastonbury and Bridgwater gives the clearest impression — the extraordinary flatness, the patchwork of fields separated by straight drainage channels, the occasional slight rise of a village or hill. The map of the Somerset Levels and Moors (available from the Somerset Rivers Authority) shows the drainage system and the historic extent of the wetland.

## Other Key Somerset Prehistoric Sites

### Priddy Circles

Four Neolithic henge monuments on the Mendip Hills near Cheddar, contemporary with Stonehenge (c. 2180 BCE). Less well known than they deserve to be. The Priddy Nine Barrows nearby are Bronze Age burial mounds on the same ridge.

Search: *Priddy Circles Somerset* for aerial photographs.

### South Cadbury Castle

A hill fort with origins in the Early Neolithic, occupied from before 3000 BCE. The impressive defensive ramparts visible today are Iron Age. Traditionally associated with Arthurian legend — identified by some as Camelot, which gives it literary resonance alongside its archaeological significance.

Approximately 8 miles southeast of Somerton — close enough for Ned to have a view of the hill from various points in the area.

### Brent Knoll

A hill rising 137 metres above the Somerset Levels, visible for miles — a landmark from Ned's train window. In prehistoric times it was an island in the marsh. Bronze Age hill fort. The Romans knew it as *The Mount of Frogs* and built a temple there.

## The Landscape as a Whole

Search *Somerset prehistoric sites map* for distribution maps showing all the main monuments. The concentration is striking — around the Mendips, the Levels, and the area south of Bristol. Stanton Drew, Priddy, Glastonbury Tor, South Cadbury — they form a landscape rather than isolated points.

This is the point Edmund makes in the school project chapter: the stone at Venn Court was not a solitary gesture. It was part of a conversation across the landscape. The people who raised it probably knew the people who raised Stanton Drew. They moved through this country, knew its landmarks, chose their positions deliberately.

The map Ned makes for his project — marking all the sites, with distances and dates — would show this distribution clearly. It is what the project is actually about: not the individual sites but the pattern they make together, and the question of what that pattern meant to the people who created it.

## A Note on Access

Most of the sites mentioned are accessible to the public, though with varying degrees of ease:

- **Stanton Drew**: £1 honesty box, the Cove free via pub garden
- **Glastonbury Tor**: National Trust, free, good footpath
- **South Cadbury Castle**: Public footpath to the summit, free
- **Brent Knoll**: Public footpath, free
- **Priddy Circles**: On private land, visible from the road and surrounding footpaths

Somerset is a county where prehistory is woven into the accessible landscape in a way that few other English counties match. Edmund and Ned, living two or three miles from Somerton, are within a short drive of sites that would have required a major expedition from most of England.

This is not incidental to the story.
