# The Conversations — A Sequence

## Overview

The real engine of the story's second movement is a series of conversations between Edmund and Ned, spread across the summer and into the autumn school term. These conversations move in small careful steps — neither character pushing, both approaching the central subject obliquely, from different directions, until they arrive at it together.

Edmund approaches from thirty years of reading and thinking and observing the stone. Ned approaches from nine years of living with something he cannot explain and has never been able to tell anyone. Neither of them knows, at the start, that the other holds the key to what they have been trying to understand.

The conversations are not therapy. They are not confession. They are two people who both think carefully about strange things, gradually discovering that they have been thinking about the same strange thing from opposite sides.

## The Literary Background — What Edmund Has Read

Edmund's library contains the full tradition of British supernatural fiction touching on standing stones and ancient landscape. He knows this literature as a scholar knows a field. The following texts are particularly relevant to the conversations.

### Arthur Machen — *The Ceremony*

A brief prose poem from *Ornaments in Jade* (one of ten literary vignettes in the volume). A woman recalls, from childhood, a grey stone in a wood — something between pillar and pyramid. As a little girl she strayed from her nurse and cried out in panic terror when the stone rose before her. Years later she watches another woman perform a ceremony at the stone, crowning it with flowers, and eventually she herself brings flowers to it.

**What it gets right:** The stone has presence. People are drawn to it across generations. There is something that persists.

**What it gets wrong, from Ned's perspective:** The stone inspires panic terror on first sight. Ned's stone did not. The ceremony is dark and erotic. Ned's experience is nothing like this. The tradition Machen represents says: ancient stones are sinister. Ned's experience says: that is not what this is.

**Why Edmund suggests it to Ned:** Because it is the classic statement of the tradition — and because its wrongness, from Ned's perspective, will open a conversation.

### Algernon Blackwood — *The Tarn of Sacrifice*

A veteran of the Somme on a walking tour encounters a standing stone, a tarn, a crag, and a young woman and her father who seem connected to ancient rites. The story involves past lives — a timeslip. The protagonist is ultimately made whole by the experience, reconquering his love of life. The girl says to him: *I am glad that you have come at last. We have been expecting you.*

**Why this matters:** Unlike most of the tradition, Blackwood's stone is ultimately healing rather than sinister. The protagonist is welcomed, not menaced. He is drawn into something ancient as a participant rather than a victim. This is much closer to what Ned experiences.

**A note on Blackwood generally:** His most typical work seeks less to frighten than to induce awe. His characters feel the draw of older religions — a pagan animism in which every stone is imbued with some kind of spirit. He is the tradition's most nuanced writer on the subject.

### Algernon Blackwood — *The Willows*

Not about a stone but the supreme example of landscape as sentient presence — two men on a canoe journey who realise they have entered somewhere that is aware of them. H.P. Lovecraft called it the finest weird tale ever written. Relevant to Ned's experience of the stone as something that notices.

### Algernon Blackwood — *Ancient Sorceries*

A man drawn into a French village's ancient pagan life — the sense of being recognised by something old, of having been expected. The theme of *we have been waiting for you* which resonates with Edmund's private thinking about the stone and Ned's arrival.

### Sarban — *Ringstones*

A novella set around an ancient stone circle in northern England. Subtle and deeply strange — one of the tradition's most literary treatments. A sense of the stones as connected to deep time in ways that are not malevolent but are not entirely safe either.

### Nigel Kneale — stone stories

Kneale wrote *The Stone Tape* for BBC television — the idea that stone can record and replay past events, human emotion and experience absorbed into the fabric of ancient rock and released under the right conditions. Directly relevant to Edmund's private theory about what his stone is doing.

### Walter de la Mare

The English master of the quietly uncanny. His ghost stories frequently feature children with unusual perception — children who see or sense what adults cannot. *Out of the Deep* and various shorter stories. Edmund would have these and they become relevant when he begins to think about Ned.

### Alan Garner — *The Owl Service* and *Red Shift*

Children in old Welsh and Cheshire landscapes who are somehow continuous with the past — caught in patterns that repeat across centuries, sensitive to what the landscape carries. Edmund has these. They are the closest thing in his library to what Ned actually is.

## The Theoretical Background — Edmund's Private Reading

Beyond fiction, Edmund's library contains several works on the practical and theoretical study of ancient sites that inform his private thinking.

**T.C. Lethbridge** — a Cambridge archaeologist who became convinced that standing stones could store and release energy, detectable by dowsing. His books (*Ghost and Divining Rod*, *ESP: Beyond Time and Distance*, others) are eccentric and contested but his observations are careful. Edmund is sceptical of some conclusions but attentive to the underlying data.

**John Michell — *The View Over Atlantis*** — ley lines, stone alignments, sacred geography. Edmund finds the grand theory unconvincing but the observations about stone placement and landscape alignment interesting. His own theory about the stone's alignment draws on this.

**Guy Underwood — *The Pattern of the Past*** — dowsing and earth energies at ancient sites. Similar territory to Lethbridge.

These books form the background to Edmund's private observation that his stone is always warm, that birds don't land on it, that time runs differently nearby. He has built his own framework, privately and carefully, from thirty years of attention and reading.

## The Conversation Sequence

### Conversation One — The Tradition (A day or two after the first visit to the stone)

*Setting: Kitchen, tea.*

Ned has been thinking about what Edmund said at the stone — *I think some trace remains. Not the meaning. The meaning is gone.* He comes back to it obliquely. Asks what Edmund has read about stones like this. Edmund describes the tradition — Machen, Blackwood, the literary context. Sinister pull, dark ceremonies, malevolent residue.

*What does your experience say?* Ned asks.

*I told you at the stone,* Edmund says. *Something persists. I think it is not frightening.*

*It wasn't frightening to me either.*

A short silence. Edmund notes this.

**Purpose:** Establishes that Ned's experience matches Edmund's, not the tradition. Plants the seed of a shared understanding.

---

### Conversation Two — Edmund Leaves The Ceremony (A few days later)

*Setting: Kitchen table, morning.*

Without ceremony Edmund sets a slim volume on the table. *This is a Machen story about a standing stone. Short. You might find it interesting.* He goes to work. Ned reads it that day.

**Purpose:** Gives Ned a text to respond to. Machen's wrongness — from Ned's perspective — opens the next conversation.

---

### Conversation Three — Ned Returns The Ceremony (That evening)

*Setting: Kitchen, evening.*

Ned puts the book on the table.

*She was afraid of it,* he says.

*Yes.*

*I wasn't.*

Edmund waits.

*It felt old,* Ned says. *Not frightening. Just — very old. Like it had been waiting. Not for anything in particular.*

Edmund considers carefully. *That's closer to what I think than anything I've read.*

A pause.

*Is there more?* Ned asks. Meaning: more to read. More to understand.

*Yes,* says Edmund. *There's rather a lot more.*

**Purpose:** The first explicit statement that Ned's experience and Edmund's private thinking align. The door to further reading and conversation opens.

---

### Conversation Four — The Compass Becomes Explicit (Second or third visit to the stone)

*Setting: At the stone.*

Ned takes out the compass and holds it out without speaking. Edmund looks at it. The needle is not pointing north.

*How long has it done that?*

*Only here.*

*You've checked it elsewhere?*

*Yes.*

Edmund nods slowly. He is thinking of his watch. The kitchen lamp. He says none of this yet.

**Purpose:** The first time Ned shows anyone the compass. Edmund's quiet reception — no alarm, only attention — is significant. Ned is watching for alarm and does not find it.

---

### Conversation Five — Edmund's Equanimity Opens the Door (A few days later)

*Setting: Kitchen.*

Ned has been thinking about Edmund's reaction to the compass. The absence of alarm.

*Doesn't it bother you?* Ned asks. *The compass.*

*No. Should it?*

*Most people would think it was strange.*

*It is strange,* Edmund says. *Strange things happen near the stone. I've observed this for thirty years.* A pause. *What else have you observed?*

Ned goes very still. No adult has ever asked him this — framed as curiosity rather than concern. He is not ready to answer fully. But he says one thing. A test.

*Sometimes things move,* he says. *Objects. When I'm in a particular kind of place.*

Edmund receives this without flinching. *What kind of place?*

*A place that doesn't feel right.*

*And this place?*

A long pause.

*This place feels right,* Ned says.

**Purpose:** The pivot of the whole sequence. Edmund's equanimity is the thing that makes everything possible. Ned tests him with one small disclosure and finds — for the first time — an adult who does not flinch. The phrase *this place feels right* carries enormous weight.

---

### Conversation Six — Ned Opens Further (Later — into the school term)

*Setting: Kitchen, evening. The right moment, which arrives without being arranged.*

Edmund mentions, carefully and without drama, that he has noticed one or two things since Ned arrived. The kitchen lamp. His watch losing time. He describes them as observations, not accusations.

Ned is very still. *You noticed.*

*Yes.*

*It doesn't — * Ned stops. Starts again. *It's happened before. In other houses.*

*I thought it might have,* Edmund says quietly.

Then Ned tells him. Not everything. But something. The first house he can remember. The thing he has never told anyone. And Edmund listens, with attention and without hurry, the way he does everything.

**Purpose:** The opening of Ned's history. Not full disclosure — that will come in stages — but the first crack in the wall he has maintained for as long as he can remember. Edmund's prior knowledge (*I thought it might have*) means Ned does not have to explain from the beginning. He is, for the first time, being understood rather than explaining.

## What These Conversations Are Building Toward

Edmund's private theory — forming slowly through the summer — is that the phenomena Ned carries, which in other houses had nowhere to go and displaced themselves into small frightening disturbances, at Venn Court find the stone. The stone receives what Ned carries the way a deep harbour receives a tide.

Ned's growing understanding — built through these conversations and the reading Edmund makes available — is that what has been frightening people away from him is connected to what Edmund has spent his life studying. Not a disorder. Not something wrong with him. A sensitivity that has been unhoused and is now, for the first time, in the right place.

The stone is not incidental to the plot. The stone is the answer to the plot.

And Edmund's deepest, most private thought — held carefully and not yet spoken — is that the right book about the stone has not been written yet. And that it may not be his to write. That perhaps it belongs to a boy who experiences what Edmund has only ever observed.

---

## Notes on Pacing

These conversations should be spread across several weeks. They are not a programme. They arise naturally from the texture of daily life — at the kitchen table, over tea, at the stone itself, in the library when Ned comes to borrow something.

Between each conversation there is living: school beginning in September, the kitchen garden, the bicycle rides to Somerton, the bedtime reading, the ordinary accumulation of days. The conversations are the moments when the deeper thing surfaces, briefly, before the ordinary current of life closes over it again.

This is how trust actually works. And Ned, above anyone, would need it to happen this way.
