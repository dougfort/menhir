# Prose Draft — The Birthday (Tenth, October)

---

## Morning

He woke earlier than usual.

Not because of anything — no alarm, no noise from the house. Just the particular quality of a birthday morning, which Ned had noticed before even when there was no particular reason to notice it: something in the air that was slightly different from other mornings, a very faint charge. He lay in the dark of his room for a moment and looked at the ceiling and thought: *ten.*

He was ten years old.

He considered this for a moment in the way he considered most things — steadily, without rushing. He had been nine for a year. He had been nine when he arrived at Castle Cary station with his backpack on his front and ate chips and talked about the Somerset Levels. He had been nine through the whole summer and the stone and the Ceremony and Stanton Drew and the murmurations. A lot had happened while he was nine.

He got up.

---

Edmund was in the kitchen. He was not doing anything remarkable — at the range with his back to the door — but there was something slightly more deliberate about the kitchen than usual. Ned registered this in the way he registered everything, quietly and without comment.

The table had been laid properly, which Edmund sometimes did and sometimes didn't. The bread was from Somerton — Mrs Perrett's, the good loaf with the particular crust — which meant Edmund had been out early. There was orange juice in the small blue jug they used for orange juice, and eggs on the go, and something that was probably the good marmalade rather than the everyday jar.

"Happy birthday," Edmund said, without turning from the range.

"Thank you," said Ned.

He sat down at his place — the left side, near the window, which had been his place since the second morning without anyone saying so. Edmund brought scrambled eggs. They ate in the comfortable silence that had become the texture of breakfast at Venn Court.

After a while Edmund said: "How old do you feel?"

Ned considered this. It was a specific question, a real one, and deserved a real answer.

"The same," he said. "But different same." He looked at his plate. "Like when you're reading a book and you've been reading it for a while and you're not at the beginning anymore but you're not near the end either. You're in the part where things have happened."

Edmund looked at him. "That," he said, "is a precise description of being ten."

Ned ate his toast.

"Does it feel different," Ned said, "when you look at it from the other side? Being forty-something and looking at ten?"

"I think it feels like the part where everything important is still possible," Edmund said. "Which it was. Which I didn't know at the time."

Ned nodded. He looked out at the orchard, at the October morning coming in through the kitchen window, at the particular quality of light that belonged to this time of year — lower and more golden than summer, the shadows longer. His birthday had always landed in this light. He had just never spent it in a place where the light was something he knew.

---

## Afternoon

Rosa arrived at two o'clock with the punctuality of someone whose father had told her to be ready by quarter to and she had been ready by half past one.

Martin at the door, brief exchange with Edmund — the easy quiet of people who have stood in a dark field together. Rosa already halfway past both of them before they had finished.

"Brilliant house," she said, without breaking stride. "How old is it?"

"Parts of it are medieval," Edmund said.

Rosa stopped. Turned. Looked at the ceiling of the kitchen corridor with the full attentive focus she usually reserved for murmurations. "Medieval meaning actually medieval or meaning it's old but people say medieval."

"Medieval meaning the core of the house dates from the fourteenth century. The front is eighteenth. The wing we don't use is Victorian."

Rosa looked at him. "You don't use a whole wing?"

"It would require considerable work to bring it back into use."

"What's in it?"

"Furniture, mainly. Some books. Damp."

Rosa appeared to find this entirely satisfactory. She turned back to the kitchen, where Ned was standing with his hands in his pockets, watching this exchange with the expression he wore when Rosa did something he found privately funny.

"Happy birthday," she said to him.

"Thanks."

"You're ten now."

"Yes."

"Does it feel different?"

"I was just asked that."

Rosa grinned. She put her bag down on the kitchen table — it had present-shaped objects in it, imperfectly concealed — and looked around at the kitchen with the same attention she had given the ceiling. The range, the flagstone floor, the herbs on the windowsill, the Vilmorin-Andrieux visible on the dresser shelf. "It smells like a kitchen," she said. "Like an actual kitchen. Ours smells like a kitchen but not like this."

"Like what?" said Edmund.

"Like things have been cooked in it for a very long time," Rosa said. "Like the walls have absorbed it." She said this without self-consciousness, the way she said everything. "Is that weird to say?"

"No," said Edmund. "It's accurate."

Martin declined tea and said he'd be back by six and left with the ease of a man who trusted his daughter in the way she deserved to be trusted.

---

Ned showed Rosa the house. Not a formal tour — he didn't think in those terms — but the places that were his: the kitchen and the library doorway, the back stairs, the small landing where you could see the orchard from above. Rosa went into everything with curiosity and no particular caution. She looked at Edmund's bookshelves with the evaluating eye of a serious reader.

"He's got loads of weird books," she said quietly to Ned, as if Edmund might not know this about his own library.

"He writes them," Ned said.

Rosa turned. "Edmund writes books?"

"Under a different name."

"What name?"

"I don't know yet." This was true — Edmund's pseudonym was still a private matter, a thread that hadn't quite been pulled yet.

Rosa looked at the shelves again. "I'm going to find out," she said, in the tone of someone making a reasonable plan.

"He'll probably tell you if you ask."

"Then I'll ask." She moved on. Outside, visible through the library's bay window, the orchard spread in October colour — the leaves at the edge of turning, the long grass under the trees unmown, the light falling through at its late angle. Rosa stopped.

"Is that a meadow?" she said. "Through the orchard."

"Yes."

"What's that in it?"

Ned looked where she was looking. From the library window, if you knew what to look for and the angle was right, you could see — just the upper portion of it, dark against the sky — the stone.

He was quiet for a moment.

"That's the stone," he said.

---

Edmund was in the kitchen. Rosa appeared in the doorway.

"Edmund," she said. "What's that stone in the field?"

Edmund turned. He looked at Rosa, and then at Ned who had appeared behind her, and then back at Rosa.

"A prehistoric standing stone," he said. "A menhir. It has been there for approximately three to four thousand years, depending on which survey you accept. No one knows with certainty why it was put there. It is a scheduled ancient monument — it appears on the Ordnance Survey map."

Rosa absorbed this. "Can we go and look at it?"

A pause. Edmund turned back to what he was doing at the range.

"Another time, perhaps," he said. "Today is for other things."

There was something in the way he said it — not a refusal, not a deflection. A real answer that had a real reason behind it which Rosa did not press on, because she understood without entirely knowing that she understood that this was a different kind of thing. She filed it under *later* without deciding to.

She looked at Ned. He was looking at the kitchen window, at the angle where the stone was not visible from here but was present in the way it was always present. He wasn't performing anything. He was just aware of it.

"Right," Rosa said. She went to find her bag and produced the present-shaped objects, which turned out to be the Collins Bird Guide — the proper one, the adult one, the one with nine hundred and twenty pages and all the European species — and a bag of Percy Pigs, which she had included because you should always include Percy Pigs. "Happy birthday," she said. "The bird guide is because you should have a real one. And the Percy Pigs are because it's your birthday."

Ned turned from the window. He looked at both items, and then at Rosa.

"These are both exactly right," he said.

Rosa looked pleased. She sat down at the kitchen table and opened the bird guide to a random page and began reading about the Eurasian bittern.

---

## Tea

Edmund had made a Victoria sponge.

It was not quite level — one layer had risen slightly more than the other, giving it a gentle lean — but it was a proper cake, with jam and cream and icing sugar on the top and Ned's name written in it in icing, in Edmund's careful architectural handwriting, which was perhaps the least natural thing anyone had ever written in icing.

Rosa examined it with professional interest. "Did you make this?"

"Yes."

"It leans."

"Yes."

"My mum's always lean. She says it means the oven's not even."

"The oven is approximately level," Edmund said. "I think the error was in the baking powder."

"It looks brilliant," Rosa said, in the tone of someone who means it. "Ours always lean and they always taste good."

Edmund put candles on the cake. Ten of them, placed with the slight precision of a man who approaches most things with slight precision. He produced matches — actual matches, not a lighter, which was typical of Edmund — and lit them one by one.

"Right," said Rosa, and began to count down from ten with complete conviction, leaving Edmund no option but to wait.

Ned looked at the cake. At the ten candles and his name in icing and the slight lean and the kitchen and Rosa counting loudly and Edmund standing to the side with the particular expression that meant he was noting something and finding it acceptable. And the stone in the meadow, not visible from here, present anyway.

He blew out the candles on three.

Rosa cheered with a volume that was probably audible from the orchard. Edmund did not cheer but his expression shifted in a way that meant the same thing.

They had two slices each, which meant the cake was more than half gone by the time it was done. Rosa talked throughout — about the bird guide and the bittern and about something Will Haddon had said at school and about whether the Victorian wing was actually haunted because it had the exact feel of a haunted wing — and Ned listened and contributed occasionally and Edmund refilled the teapot and found that the noise of this particular afternoon was not unpleasant in the least.

---

## The Gifts

Edmund put them on the table after tea, without announcement. Two things, simply placed.

The field notebook first — Ned picked it up before he had quite looked at it properly, because the weight and cover of it told him something immediately. Hardcover, waterproof, the size that fits in a jacket pocket. He opened it to the first page. Blank. Ready. Paper that would take pencil in any weather, in any field, on any mound.

He ran his thumb along the spine and looked at Edmund.

"For outside," Edmund said simply.

"Yes," said Ned. He closed it and set it carefully to one side and looked at the second thing.

The OS map. The 1:25000 Explorer sheet. He recognised it before he unfolded it — the particular format, the orange cover, the sheet number. He unfolded it with the care of someone who understood what maps were. Found Venn Court. Found the lane. Found the orchard and the meadow beyond.

Found the stone.

The Gothic-script symbol. The scheduled ancient monument marker. His stone, on his own map, in his own name — Edmund had written it inside the front cover, *For Ned*, in the same careful hand as the icing, but more natural here, more at home.

He looked at it for a long moment.

Rosa, who had been watching, leaned over. "Oh — is that the stone? The one in the field?"

"Yes," said Ned.

"It's on the map. It's an actual scheduled thing." She looked at it with interest. "That's brilliant." Then she went back to her bird guide.

Ned folded the map along its original folds. Set it beside the notebook. Looked at both of them together.

*Thank you,* he said, to the table rather than to Edmund's face. The only way he could say it for something this size.

"You're welcome," said Edmund.

---

## After

Martin at six o'clock. Rosa departing with the energy with which she had arrived, the bird guide under her arm. At the door she turned back.

"Next murmuration weekend," she said to Ned, "my dad says there might be half a million."

"I'll be there," said Ned.

"Obviously." She went.

Martin and Edmund exchanged the brief look of people who have both watched the same small hurricane depart. Then Martin left, and the lane was quiet, and it was just the two of them again.

Edmund washed up. Ned dried, as he always did.

The kitchen settling back into its own quality of quiet — not empty, just returned to itself. The remains of the cake on the counter. The teapot cooling.

"Edmund," Ned said.

"Yes."

"How old were you when you first went to the stone?"

Edmund was quiet for a moment. "Eleven," he said. "Perhaps twelve. My father showed it to me. He had been shown it by his."

Ned dried a plate. Put it on the rack.

"Will you show me things," he said. "Other things. When I'm older."

He said it without drama, without particular weight — the way he said things that mattered, which was the same as the way he said things that didn't. But the question was enormous and both of them knew it. It was not quite the adoption question. It was the question underneath the adoption question, the one about time and continuity and what it means for a place to be yours across years rather than just for now.

Edmund rinsed a cup. Set it on the draining board.

"Yes," he said. "I will."

He said it the same way — without drama, without weight — but with the complete certainty of someone who has already decided. And then they finished the washing up, and Ned went to put the notebook and the map on his shelf, and Edmund made the last pot of tea of the day, and the kitchen was warm and dark outside and somewhere in the meadow the stone stood in its field as it always did, patient and unhurried, having been doing this for rather longer than any of them had been alive.

*Draft notes: Four movements. The morning is Edmund and Ned alone, the particular quality of a birthday that is being kept properly for the first time, Ned's answer to how old he feels — the book metaphor, being in the part where things have happened — and Edmund's response about everything still being possible. The afternoon is Rosa: her arrival, her frank assessment of the house, the beginning of her relationship with Edmund which goes exactly as the character sheet predicted. The stone glimpsed from the library window — Ned saying simply *that's the stone* — and then Rosa asking Edmund directly, and Edmund's answer, and his *another time, perhaps* which Rosa registers and files without pressing. The tea is domestic and warm — the lopsided cake, Rosa's countdown, the noise of the afternoon which Edmund finds not unpleasant. The gifts: the field notebook and the OS map, the Gothic-script symbol, Ned finding the stone on his own map. The close is the washing up — Ned asking whether Edmund will show him things, other things, when he is older. Not the adoption question but the question underneath it. Edmund's answer: yes, without drama, with complete certainty. The stone in the field at the end, patient and unhurried.*
