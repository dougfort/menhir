# Prose Draft — The Adoption (February)

---

## Morning — The Hearing

They drove to Wells on a Thursday in February.

Edmund had taken the day as he took most things — with advance preparation and without excess comment. He had told the school Ned would be absent. He had confirmed the appointment time with the court. He had looked up the parking. On the morning he made breakfast as usual — the good bread, the eggs — and neither of them mentioned where they were going until Ned appeared at the back door in something slightly smarter than his usual clothes, which Edmund noted without commenting on.

"Ready?" Edmund said.

"Yes," said Ned.

They drove through the February morning — the lanes, the A roads, Somerset in its winter state, the fields grey-green and the hedges bare and a pale wash of light coming from the south that might, later, become sun. The Mendips visible to the north as they crossed the Levels. Ned looked out the window. Edmund drove.

They didn't talk much, which was fine. They had always been comfortable with not talking.

---

Wells was the smallest city in England and knew it with the easy confidence of somewhere that has been itself for a very long time. The cathedral rose above the market square as they parked — enormous, medieval, the towers catching the pale February light. Edmund had been here before, many times. Ned had been here once, briefly, during the summer — Somerton children sometimes came to Wells on school trips. He looked at the cathedral as they crossed the square and then looked away, which was his way of noting something and filing it.

The courthouse was on Priory Road — a modest building for so significant a function, which was perhaps appropriate. Edmund held the door. They went in.

---

The celebration hearing was brief and warm in the way the accounts had described — informal, the courtroom smaller than Ned had imagined, the judge a woman of perhaps sixty with the particular quality of someone who has done a great deal of this and has not grown tired of it.

She had read the file. She said something about the road that had brought Ned here — careful, not sentimental, acknowledging the houses without dwelling on them. She said something about Venn Court, about Edmund's particular qualities as described by the social worker, which made Edmund sit very slightly more upright without otherwise moving. She said that in her experience the arrangements that worked best were the ones that had already, in every practical sense, been made — and that this was clearly one of those.

She gave Ned the certificate. A proper piece of card, his name and Edmund's name and the date, in good font. And then she produced a book — which made Ned look at Edmund briefly and Edmund look at the middle distance with the expression of a man experiencing mild private amusement — and gave it to Ned with the explanation that she gave all the children something to mark the day, and she had been told he was a reader.

It was a field guide to the birds of Somerset. Serious, adult, the kind with nine hundred pages.

"It's a proper one," Ned said, without thinking.

"I'm glad," the judge said. She looked at Edmund. "He seems like a boy who would want a proper one."

"He is," Edmund said.

---

They came out into the February morning. The cathedral across the square, enormous and patient. Ned had the field guide under his arm and the certificate folded in his jacket pocket — next to the compass, which was where it would live from now on. The two things together in the same pocket.

Edmund stood beside him on the courthouse steps for a moment.

"All right?" Edmund said.

"Yes," Ned said. He looked at the cathedral. "It's very old."

"Twelfth century. The west front is somewhat later."

Ned nodded. He turned from the cathedral and looked at Edmund with a look that was not quite any of the looks Edmund had seen before — steadier than his usual steadiness, more open than his usual openness. Something that had been held for a long time and was now, with the certificate in his pocket, no longer necessary to hold quite so tightly.

"Thank you," he said.

Edmund received this — the largest thank you Ned had ever offered, in the same quiet register as all the others, to Edmund's face this time rather than to his tea. He received it the way he received everything important: without flinching, without making too much of it.

"Of course," he said. Which was not a deflection. It was Edmund saying: this was always going to happen. From the beginning, this was where we were going.

They walked across the square toward the restaurant where Rosa and Martin were waiting.

---

## Midday — The Lunch

Rosa saw them through the window before they reached the door and was outside before they had reached the step.

She hugged Ned.

Not a polite hug — a proper one, Rosa-sized, her arms around him completely, her chin on his shoulder. She was slightly taller than him, which meant this worked geometrically, and she held on for a moment longer than most people would have, because Rosa didn't calculate these things, she just did them.

Ned stood inside the hug with his hands at his sides for a half-second — the old reflex, the body's memory of not knowing what to do when touched — and then his arms came up and he hugged her back. Briefly. Enough.

"Finally," Rosa said, releasing him, stepping back. "I've been waiting since October."

"Since October?" Ned said.

"Since he registered you at school. Obviously." She said this as if it were self-evident. "Come on, it's cold."

Martin, who had come out more slowly behind her, shook Edmund's hand. The same compact nod he had given at the murmuration, at the concert, at the birthday. A nod that had accumulated, over the months, into something like friendship.

"Good morning?" Martin said.

"Good morning," Edmund said.

That was sufficient. They went inside.

---

The restaurant was the kind Edmund chose — not expensive, not showy, the kind that had been in the same building for thirty years and knew what it was doing with local ingredients. A Wednesday lunch crowd, not busy. A table by the window.

Rosa ordered with the decisiveness of someone who had examined the menu on the way in and made her decisions before sitting down. Martin ordered something unpretentious. Edmund ordered the soup and then changed it to the pie, which he did not usually do, which Ned noted.

"What was it like?" Rosa said to Ned, when the menus had been taken away.

"Fine," Ned said.

Rosa looked at him. "Fine."

"Yes."

"That's it. Fine."

"It was fine, Rosa."

She looked at him for a moment with the brief focused attention she brought to murmurations — reading what was there rather than what was said. Then she accepted it, in the way she had learned to accept Ned's *fines*, which were not dismissals but completions. The thing had happened, it was done, it was exactly what it needed to be, and *fine* was the right word for something that had gone as it should.

"All right," she said. And began talking about something else entirely, which was the correct thing to do.

The lunch was warm and easy. Rosa talked about school and Mr Ashby and a project she was doing on estuary birds and a theory she had about where the starlings went after February. Martin contributed occasionally in his measured way. Edmund ate his pie and listened and asked one or two precise questions about the estuary birds which Rosa answered with enthusiasm and then countered with a question of her own which Edmund answered properly, which was what Rosa had come to expect from him.

At some point — the food mostly eaten, the table settled into the comfortable stage of a good lunch — Rosa looked at Edmund directly.

"He's lucky," she said. "You both are."

She said it in the tone of someone stating a fact, which was her tone for everything. No sentiment in it. Just the observation, offered cleanly.

Edmund looked at her. "Yes," he said. "We are."

He meant it exactly and she knew he meant it exactly and that was the end of it, which was right.

Ned, who had heard this exchange, looked at his plate and said nothing. His expression did something brief and private and then returned to its usual quality.

---

## Afternoon — The Drive Home

Martin took Rosa in the Land Rover — she had school in the morning and Wells was slightly out of their way. They said goodbye in the car park, Rosa to Ned with the economy of people who see each other most days and don't need to make things of partings.

"Same time next week," she said, which was what they always said. Meaning school, meaning lunch, meaning the ordinary continuity of it.

"Same time next week," Ned said.

Edmund and Martin said their brief farewells. Martin got in the Land Rover. As Rosa climbed in she paused and looked back at Ned through the window — a look that lasted half a second, too quick for most people to catch. Ned caught it. It was Rosa's version of everything she hadn't said at lunch and wasn't going to say, which was: *I know what today was. I know what it means. I'm glad.*

Then she turned away and said something to her father and they drove off.

---

Edmund and Ned drove home through the February afternoon, the light going early, the Somerset lanes narrowing as they left Wells and headed south. The Mendips behind them. The Levels opening out to the left — the flat country, the reed beds, the rhynes catching the last of the pale sky.

Neither of them said much. The day had been what it needed to be and had said what needed saying, and the silence of the drive home was the silence of two people who have come through something together and don't need to discuss it.

At some point — the lanes familiar now, Somerton close, the turn toward Venn Court ahead — Ned reached into his jacket pocket. He took out the certificate and looked at it in the fading light. His name. Edmund's name. The date.

He folded it back along its crease and put it in his pocket.

"Edmund," he said.

"Yes."

"The judge chose well." About the bird guide. "Rosa said it was a proper one."

"I heard her say so," Edmund said.

"She would know."

"She would."

The turn. The lane. The hedges pressing in. And at the end of it, the house — the kitchen light on, which Edmund had left on before they went because they would be coming back in the dark.

Ned looked at it.

"Home," he said. Not to Edmund. To himself, or to the house, or to the fact of it. Quietly, the way he said the things that mattered most.

Edmund parked in the stable block. They went in through the back door. The kitchen was warm, the range going, the house making its comfortable small sounds.

Edmund put the kettle on.

Ned put the bird guide on the shelf — the shelf with the OS map and the compass and Edmund's three books that had been there since the first evening. He stood for a moment looking at the shelf.

Then he sat down at the kitchen table and opened the field notebook.

Edmund made the tea.
