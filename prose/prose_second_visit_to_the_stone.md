# Prose Draft — The Second Visit to the Stone

---

It was Ned's idea, the second time.

Edmund was in the library after breakfast, and Ned had been in the kitchen garden for an hour checking on things, and when he came back in he stood in the library doorway with dirt on his knees and said, without preamble: "Can we go to the stone?"

Edmund looked up from his manuscript. "Now?"

"You're not doing anything."

Edmund looked at the manuscript. "I am, in point of fact, doing something."

"You've been looking at the same page since I came in from the garden." Ned said this with the air of someone presenting evidence rather than making an accusation. "I saw you through the window."

Edmund considered this. The sentence he had been trying to write had not, in the course of the last hour, improved. "Give me ten minutes," he said.

Ned sat down in the green chair and looked at the bookshelves. After a moment he said: "You've got loads of books."

"Yes."

"Have you read all of them?"

"Most. Some are for reference."

"What's the difference?"

Edmund put the cap on his pen. "Reference books you consult. Other books you read."

"I consult Vilmorin," Ned said, with some satisfaction, as if this placed him in an interesting category.

"So you do." Edmund stood. "Come on then."

---

The meadow was at its best in late morning — the light coming from the south and the grass showing two colours where the breeze moved through it, dark then pale, like a tide going over. Ned walked beside Edmund at Edmund's pace, which he had learned to match, and watched a red kite working the field boundary on the far side of the hedge.

"Is that a kite?" he said.

"Yes. Red kite."

"They're bigger than I thought." He watched it bank and slide. "Will said there aren't any round here."

"Will is occasionally wrong," said Edmund.

Ned looked pleased. He had a complicated relationship with Will Haddon, who was older and knew many things about Somerton but was not always right about them.

The stone came into view above the orchard treeline as they came through the ha-ha. Ned looked at it the way he had been looking at it since the first visit — with attention, not quite casual, as if it required his full engagement each time.

"It's different in the morning," he said.

"Different how?"

Ned thought about it. "More — awake. I don't know. That's not the right word either." He made a small frustrated sound, which was new. He was becoming, Edmund noted, willing to be frustrated by inadequate language rather than simply abandoning the attempt. That was a change from three weeks ago. "It just looks different depending on the light."

"Most things do."

"Yeah, but this is more different than most things."

Edmund allowed this. It was accurate.

---

The grass thinned as they crossed the mound and Ned went straight to his boulder — the right one, as you faced the stone — and sat down. He had done this on the first visit without being directed and had done it again now without hesitation. His boulder. Edmund sat on his own.

Ned looked at the stone for a while. His hands were on his knees and his face had the particular quality it got when he was working something out — not worried, just engaged, the way he looked over Vilmorin.

"D'you reckon it knows we're here?" he said.

Edmund considered this carefully, which was how he considered most things. "I think that depends on what you mean by *knows*."

"Yeah." Ned picked a stem of grass from the thinned margin near his feet and turned it in his fingers. "It's not like — obviously it doesn't have a brain. But when I'm here it feels like — " He stopped. Tried again. "Okay so there's a difference between a place that doesn't care and a place that's just — not thinking about you. Right? Like the difference between someone ignoring you and someone being asleep."

Edmund looked at him.

"The stone feels like — the second one," Ned said. "Not ignoring. Just — something else going on. Something massive. That isn't us."

"That," said Edmund, after a moment, "is rather good."

Ned looked at him suspiciously. "Are you going to write that down?"

"Probably not. You might."

Ned considered this, turning the grass stem. Then he dropped it and reached into his jacket pocket and took out the compass.

He held it flat on his palm and tilted it toward Edmund without saying anything.

Edmund leaned forward and looked at the needle. It was pointing — southeast, approximately. Toward the stone's face. Which was not north.

Edmund looked at it for a long moment without speaking, the way he looked at interesting things. Not alarmed. Just — attentive.

"How long has it done that?" he said.

"Only here." Ned tilted the compass slightly and the needle tracked, steady and convinced. "I checked it in the kitchen this morning. And in the orchard. It's only here."

"You've been checking."

"Since last time."

Edmund nodded slowly. He was thinking about his watch, about the kitchen lamp, about three weeks of small observations filed away without occasion to share them. He did not share them now. Not yet.

"It's pointing at the stone," Ned said.

"Yes."

"D'you reckon it always does that? Even when no one's here?"

Edmund looked at the stone. "I don't know. I've never thought to leave a compass unattended."

Ned's mouth twitched very slightly at one corner. "We could tie one to a stick or something."

"We could," Edmund agreed seriously. "Though I suspect the results would be the same."

"Still." Ned closed his fingers around the compass and put it back in his pocket. He looked at the stone again. "It's pointing at it like it's north. Like it thinks the stone is where north should be."

Edmund looked at him.

"That's probably rubbish," Ned said.

"It is not obviously rubbish," Edmund said. "There are theories about magnetic anomalies at prehistoric sites. I have some of the literature, if you're interested."

Ned looked up at this. "Books?"

"Some books. Some papers. More technical than Machen."

"Yeah but I can try." He said this with the complete confidence of someone who has always found a way into difficult books eventually. "What kind of theories?"

"The idea — and it is contested, I should say — is that certain sites were chosen because of their properties. Measurable properties. That the people who erected these stones were more sensitive to the landscape than we are."

Ned was very still. Not the watchful stillness of his first weeks but a different quality of attention — focused, leaning in.

"More sensitive," he said.

"That's one way of putting it. Another is that they were paying a different kind of attention. Noticing things we've learned not to notice."

Ned looked at the stone. The stone stood in its slight lean and received this information, as it received everything, without comment.

"My compass notices," Ned said. "Even if I don't know what it's noticing."

"Yes," said Edmund. "That's rather the point."

They sat for a while. A pair of jackdaws crossed overhead and banked away from the stone without landing on it, as birds always did. Ned watched them go.

"Will says his gran went to the stone once," he said. "When she was a girl. She said it gave her a headache."

"Different people respond differently."

"Does it give you a headache?"

"No."

"Me neither." Ned picked up another grass stem, examined it, put it down. "Will thinks it's haunted. He explained it very seriously. He had a whole theory."

"What was the theory?"

"Something about Roman soldiers and a curse. He'd got it off the internet." Ned paused. "I didn't say anything."

"That was probably wise."

"He'd done research," Ned said, with a particular dryness that sat in him like something he had recently discovered he had. "Hours of it, he said. So."

Edmund had a brief, private struggle with his expression. "I see."

"The internet has a lot of information about standing stones."

"It does."

"Most of it is — " Ned searched for the word. "It's like those horror films where the scary thing has a really complicated backstory that explains everything. But when you're here it's just — not like that. Is it."

"No," Edmund agreed. "It isn't."

Ned looked at the stone for a while longer. Then he stood up, brushing the back of his jeans, and said: "I want to look something up in the library when we get back. About magnetic anomalies."

"That's a reasonable use of an afternoon."

"And can I read the papers? The technical ones."

"You may find them heavy going."

"Yeah, but I can try," Ned said again, exactly as he had before. And set off back across the meadow toward the orchard, his shadow going ahead of him in the late morning sun.

Edmund sat for a moment longer. The stone stood in its field, warm and dry, entirely itself.

He had been coming here for thirty years and in all that time had never had a conversation at the stone. Had never brought anyone here with any particular intention. Had kept this place, and what he thought about it, entirely private.

And now there was this boy. Who had worked out, in two visits, things that had taken Edmund the better part of a decade to articulate. Who had said *not ignoring, just something else going on, something massive* and meant it precisely.

Edmund looked at the compass-direction the needle had pointed. Southeast. Toward the stone's face.

*It thinks the stone is where north should be.*

He stood, put his hands in his pockets, and followed Ned back through the orchard.

*Draft notes: This scene does several things simultaneously. Ned initiates — the first time he has suggested going to the stone, which is itself a development. His observation from the library doorway — "you've been looking at the same page since I came in" — is new: confident, slightly cheeky, evidence of his growing ease. The "loads of books" exchange establishes his voice — direct, specific, following his own logic. At the stone, his language is doing something interesting: he tries "awake," finds it wrong, tries to explain the difference between being ignored and being asleep, arrives at something exact. He is learning to push past inadequate words rather than stop at them. The compass is shown without drama — held out, tilted toward Edmund. Edmund's reception: no alarm, just attention. The observation that the needle points at the stone *like it's north* — like it thinks that's where north should be — is Ned's own theory, offered and then immediately called "probably rubbish," which is him testing the water. Edmund's "it is not obviously rubbish" is the response that matters. Ned's dryness about Will's internet research is the scene's comic note — and the first time Ned has been wry with Edmund about something. The final image is Edmund alone at the stone for a moment, thinking about what Ned has said. The boy is ahead of him in some ways. This is the beginning of Edmund understanding that.*
