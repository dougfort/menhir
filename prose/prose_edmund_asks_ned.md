# Prose Draft — Edmund Asks Ned

---

It was a Thursday evening, about three weeks into Ned's stay. Dinner had been cleared. Edmund had made tea and put a mug in front of Ned without asking, which had become the habit, and Ned had wrapped both hands around it the way he did when he wasn't thinking about what his hands were doing.

Edmund sat down across the table.

He had been thinking about how to say this for several days and had not arrived at a satisfactory formulation, which was unusual for him. He decided, as he sometimes did with difficult sentences, to simply begin and trust that the rest would follow.

"I'd like to register you at the primary school in Somerton," he said. "For September. Year Five."

He stopped. That was the thing itself, stated plainly. He waited.

Ned looked at him across the table. His hands were still around the mug. His expression was the one Edmund had come to think of as his thinking expression — not blank, not guarded, but turned slightly inward, working something out.

The kitchen made its sounds. The range ticked. Outside, a wood pigeon was conducting its repetitive and apparently inexhaustible enquiry into the evening.

"Is that — " Ned started, and then stopped.

"It's what I'd like to do," Edmund said. "If you're agreeable. I'm asking because it's your school. It seemed right to ask."

Ned looked down at his mug. Up again.

"What happens," he said carefully, "at the end of summer."

It was not quite a question. It was the question underneath the question — the one Ned had been not-asking since he arrived, filed away in that part of himself he kept for things that mattered.

Edmund had thought about this too. He had thought about it quite a lot, in the early mornings, walking to the stone and back. He had not arrived at anything he could call a conclusion. What he had arrived at was something more like a direction.

"I don't know the answer to that in any formal sense," he said. "There are things that would need to be arranged. People who would need to be spoken to. It would take time." He paused. "What I can tell you is that I am not registering you at a school in order to remove you from it in July."

Ned absorbed this. Edmund watched him do it — the slight stillness, the working-through.

"All right," Ned said.

Two words. Quiet and considered, with nothing performed about them. Edmund had heard Ned say things with more apparent feeling about which biscuit to have with his tea. Which was, he thought, exactly right.

"Good," Edmund said. "I'll go in next week."

He picked up his own mug. Ned picked up his. The wood pigeon had moved on to a different tree and a slightly different version of its question, which it seemed no more likely to resolve.

After a moment Ned said, without looking up from his tea: "Edmund."

"Yes."

"Thank you."

Edmund said nothing. He drank his tea. The kitchen held them both in its usual warm, unhurried way, as it had held rather a lot of people over rather a lot of years, and had no particular comment to make about any of them.

---

*Draft notes: The scene is deliberately short — the weight carried in what surrounds the words rather than the words themselves. Ned's "What happens at the end of summer" is not quite a question because Ned does not quite dare make it one. Edmund's answer is honest about its uncertainty while making his intention absolutely clear — he is not registering Ned at a school in order to remove him from it. That sentence is Edmund's declaration, stated without drama. Ned's "All right" carries everything. His "Thank you" at the end, after the silence has settled, is the scene's final note — offered quietly, to his tea rather than to Edmund's face, which is the only way Ned could say it yet.*
