# Prose Draft — Edmund at the Stone (Before Ned)

---

He went early, as he usually did, while the dew was still on the meadow grass and the light came flat and clear from the east.

The house was behind him — he had left his coffee half finished on the kitchen table, an old habit, as if something in him knew before the rest of him did that the morning called for the stone. He crossed the yard, went through the kitchen garden gate, latched it behind him. The espaliered pears along the south wall were swelling now, still hard and green. Another month, perhaps.

The ha-ha steps were worn in the middle where feet had always found them. He descended into the meadow.

The orchard was at its best in the early morning — the old trees casting long shadows across the grass, the light filtering through leaves not yet stiffened by the day's heat. He walked the path between them that was not really a path, just the way he always went, the grass slightly more worn here than elsewhere. A wood pigeon went up from somewhere overhead with its familiar clatter and complaint. Otherwise it was quiet.

Beyond the orchard the meadow opened.

He always noticed the stone differently at this point. Behind the orchard, you could not quite see it. Then the last trees fell away and it was simply there — already larger than it seemed it should be, the top of it dark against the pale morning sky, the lean of it just perceptible if you were looking for it, which he always was.

Edmund walked across the meadow at his ordinary pace.

The grass changed as he approached. Not dramatically — you would have to know to notice — but the tall meadow grasses and their attendant wildflowers thinned gradually, gave way to something shorter and sparser, as if the ground here had different ideas about what should grow on it. The change was never sudden. It was a slow declension, measurable in feet, unremarkable unless you were paying attention.

He paid attention.

---

At the stone he stopped, as he always stopped, and looked at it for a moment before sitting down.

Up close it was different from any distance. Large in a way that distances flattered — the mass of it was not apparent until you were beside it, until you put your hand against it and felt the scale of the thing. He did this now. Pressed his palm flat against the surface on the south-facing side.

Warm. Already, at this hour, distinctly warmer than his hand. He had long since stopped being surprised by this. The dew was thick on the meadow grass, lying on everything in small cold drops, but the stone was dry.

He examined the surface for a while, as he often did. The limestone was not uniform — it had a texture, pitted and marked by millennia of weather, patched with lichen in orange and grey and a dark greenish black that appeared almost oiled in certain lights. Some of the marks went deeper. Slight concavities that gathered shadow. He had looked at them for thirty years and still could not decide whether they were natural or not. They were not obviously deliberate. But they were not obviously accidental either.

He had a rubbing of one section in the library, done on a quiet afternoon fifteen years ago. He looked at it occasionally. It had not resolved anything.

He sat down on his boulder.

---

The light moved while he sat there. That was the first thing he always noticed — not that it moved quickly, but that he noticed its movement, which he didn't ordinarily. At his desk, time passed without his attention. Here, time passed and he was present in each moment of it, in a way that was not effortful but simply occurred.

He thought about the chapter he had been working on. The manuscript was three-quarters done — a story set in the Quantock Hills, a family who bought a farmhouse and found, beneath the garden, an arrangement of stones. The usual architecture of dread. He was good at it. He knew the mechanisms. You established the normal world thoroughly enough that the reader trusted you, and then you tilted it, very slowly, until the angle was wrong and nothing would straighten again.

He thought about the chapter for a while and then found he had stopped thinking about it.

This was the reliable effect. The ordinary noise of his mind — the work, the small domestic calculations, the background hum of things unfinished — gradually lost its urgency. Not forgotten, exactly. More as if it receded to its proper proportion. Most of what he thought about, sitting at his desk, turned out, sitting here, to be quite small.

What remained was harder to describe.

Not silence exactly — the meadow was full of sound, insects and birds and the distant machinery of Jim Turley's farm somewhere beyond the boundary hedge. But underneath the sound there was something he had no better word for than stillness. A quality of the place itself that had nothing to do with noise. As if the ground beneath him had a different relationship to time than the ground at the house, and he was simply sitting in it, the way you sit in shade on a hot day and feel the temperature change.

He looked at the stone.

It looked back, after its fashion.

---

He had been writing about stones like this for twenty years. Ancient menace. Residual evil. The dark persistence of pre-Christian practice. His readers expected it, his reviews cited Machen and Blackwood, and he delivered what was expected with genuine craft if not genuine conviction.

The conviction, if he was honest with himself — which he generally tried to be, sitting here — lay elsewhere.

What he actually thought, after thirty years of early morning visits, was this: that the people who made this stone had brought something here, had concentrated something here, through whatever combination of ritual and intention and sheer accumulated attention, and that the concentrated thing had persisted after them the way a smell persists in a room after the person who wore it has gone. Not frightening. Not malevolent. Simply present. Residual. The afterimage of a significance he could not read.

He thought this was probably the most interesting thing he knew, and he had never written it.

He had written instead about the horror of the ancient — which was easier, and which sold — while keeping the actual experience of the ancient for himself, in these early morning visits, unreported.

He put his hand on the stone again. Warm, dry, slightly rough against his palm.

Outside, somewhere in the direction of the Mendips, a buzzard called once — that high, plaintive sound, more lonely than threatening.

Edmund sat for a while longer. Then he stood, put his hands in his pockets, and walked back through the orchard toward the house and his half-finished coffee.

The chapter would go better today. It always did, after this.

---

*Draft notes: This scene is set well before Ned arrives and establishes Edmund's relationship with the stone entirely on its own terms — no child, no plot, no event. The rubbing of the surface in the library is a detail that might appear later. The internal admission that he has been writing the wrong thing about stones his entire career — that the actual experience is something he has kept private — is the key to Edmund's character as it relates to the story. The buzzard at the end is the correct bird: a common Somerset raptor, its call genuinely plaintive, not melodramatic. Edmund returning to his half-finished coffee is the right closing note — the ancient and the domestic, in their usual proportion.*
