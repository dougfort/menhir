# Prose Draft — Parents' Evening (October)

---

The appointment was at half past five on a Thursday.

Edmund had booked the slot online, which had required navigating the school's booking system with the determined patience he brought to things he found mildly baffling. The system appeared to want him to log in, and then to create an account, and then to verify the account, and then to confirm his relationship to the child, at which point it had offered him a choice of appointments in five-minute increments between four and seven o'clock. He had selected half past five on the grounds that it was neither the first slot — which would suggest anxiety — nor the last, which would suggest reluctance. He had received a confirmation email which he had printed and filed.

He mentioned none of this to Ned.

---

Ned, for his part, had mentioned parents' evening once — on the Monday of the week it was happening — and then not again, which was his way of indicating that he was thinking about it without wanting to discuss it. Edmund had confirmed he would be attending and returned to the manuscript he had been reading, and that had been that.

On the Thursday Ned came home from school, had a biscuit, did twenty minutes of spellings at the kitchen table, and then went to his room with his mapping notebook. He came back down at five o'clock when Edmund was getting ready to leave.

"You'll be back by seven," Ned said. Not a question.

"By six thirty, I would think." Edmund put on his jacket. "It's ten minutes."

"They run late sometimes." Ned appeared to be offering this as useful information. "Rosa says hers always runs late."

"I'll allow for that." Edmund picked up his car keys. "There's leftover soup on the range if you're hungry."

He left. Ned stood at the kitchen window and watched the car go down the lane, and then went back to his notebook.

---

The school at five thirty on a Thursday was different from the school at any other time Edmund had seen it. The main entrance propped open, a handwritten sign directing parents to the Year 5 classroom, the corridors that were usually full of children now full of a different kind of traffic — adults in work clothes or coats, navigating hallways scaled for smaller people, most of them carrying the slightly uncertain air of being on someone else's ground.

Edmund followed the sign.

Outside the classroom a line of chairs had been placed in the corridor — four of them, three occupied. He took the fourth and sat with his coat on his lap and looked at the corridor wall, which was covered in the work of Year 5. Geography maps. Creative writing extracts. A project display on the Anglo-Saxons.

He found Ned's map without difficulty. It was in the centre of the geography display, which suggested Mr Ashby had placed it there deliberately. A map of the local area — Somerton, the surrounding villages, the Levels to the west. The contour lines were precise, the annotation careful, the whole thing executed with a quality of attention that was unusual in the context of the other maps beside it, which were entirely competent but somehow less inhabited.

Edmund looked at it for some time.

In the creative writing display, nearby, a typed extract — he found the name at the bottom, *Ned K*, the surname abbreviated in the way of school display work — which began:

*The hill had been there before the road, and before the village, and before the name of the village, which was a name given by people who had come later and had not known what to call a place that was already something.*

Edmund read this twice. Then the door to the classroom opened and a couple came out, and a man in his thirties leaned out of the door and said: "Mr Kelway?"

---

Mr Ashby was younger than Edmund in the way that most teachers he encountered seemed to be — the particular youth of someone who was very good at their job and had been doing it long enough to be confident but not long enough to be tired. He had a quality of focused attention that Edmund recognised and approved of. His desk was orderly. He had the reading diary on the table between them, and a folder of work, and a small handwritten list which Edmund could not read upside down but suspected was notes.

They shook hands across the small table. Edmund sat in the chair, which was a child's chair and somewhat low, and arranged himself in it with as much dignity as the chair permitted.

"Thank you for coming," Mr Ashby said.

"Of course."

Mr Ashby opened the folder. "Ned is — " He paused, in the manner of someone who has thought about how to say something and is now deciding whether the thing he prepared is right. "Ned is one of the most interesting students I've taught in ten years. I want to say that first, because I think it's important context for everything else."

Edmund waited.

"His English work is exceptional. The reading, the comprehension, the creative writing — all of it operating well above his year level, and in the case of the writing, operating in a way I find genuinely difficult to assess because I'm not sure the standard assessment criteria are the right tools." He picked up the folder and produced a printed sheet — the extract from the corridor display, Edmund recognised, and several other pieces. "May I show you some examples?"

He showed Edmund several pieces of Ned's writing. Edmund read each one with full attention. They were, he found, very good — precise and strange in the particular way of someone who has been reading Machen and Blackwood and Tolkien and processing the experience through their own imagination. The hill that had been there before the road. A short piece about a compass that knew where north was but was not always certain north was the right direction. A description of a stone in a field from the point of view of everything else in the field.

"He never writes about people," Mr Ashby said, watching Edmund read. "Or almost never. He writes about places and objects. The people are always just off the edge of the piece."

"I've noticed this," Edmund said.

"You've read his work?"

"Some of it. What he's shown me." Which was not precisely what Ned had shown him, but was the right answer.

Mr Ashby looked at him for a moment with the expression of a man recalibrating. "What does he read at home?"

Edmund glanced at the reading diary, which was open on the table. He had signed it, most recently, three days ago. "Currently *The Fellowship of the Ring*," he said. "Before that, Algernon Blackwood's *The Tarn of Sacrifice*. Before that, a story by Arthur Machen."

Mr Ashby was quiet for a moment. "He's ten," he said. Not reproachfully. Almost with something like wonder.

"He finds it manageable," Edmund said. "We read together in the evenings."

"You read the Machen together."

"And the Blackwood. And Tolkien, yes."

Mr Ashby looked at the reading diary. Then at Edmund. Then he appeared to make a decision and moved on, in the manner of a man who has encountered something he wants to think about later at more leisure. "The geography," he said, and produced the map from the folder.

---

They covered geography — the maps, Edmund confirming that yes, Ned made maps at home, had been doing so for some time, that the accuracy of the local area map came from firsthand familiarity with the landscape rather than from reference materials.

They covered science — Ned's engagement with the Earth and Beyond unit, his specific questions about the age of geological formations, whether the solar system predated the formation of the continents.

They covered history — Mr Ashby noting, carefully, that Ned had a quality of attention in the local history unit that was different from his attention in other units. More personal, somehow. Harder to describe. Edmund received this without elaborating on it.

"PSHE," Mr Ashby said, and paused. "He wrote something in his reflection journal in the identity unit that I've been thinking about since October." He didn't produce the journal — it was private, Edmund understood, between pupil and teacher. "He wrote about whether a place could know you. Whether a landscape could recognise something in a person that the person didn't recognise in themselves." He looked at Edmund. "It was very precise. I didn't know what to do with it, assessment-wise."

"I imagine not," Edmund said.

"Is he all right?" Mr Ashby asked. Not the professional version of the question — the real version.

"Yes," Edmund said. "He is, I think, increasingly all right."

Mr Ashby looked at him for a moment with the particular look of a teacher who knows he is only seeing part of the picture and has decided to trust the part he can see. "Good," he said. He closed the folder. "Maths is fine. Science is good. PE he is adequate — better than he appears initially, which I think applies to him in general."

"Yes," Edmund said. "I think it does."

They shook hands again. Edmund thanked him. Mr Ashby said he was glad they had met, in a way that suggested he meant it specifically rather than as a formality. Edmund went back down the corridor — past the maps, past the creative writing extract, past the other parents waiting on their chairs — and out into the October evening.

He sat in the car for a moment.

*Whether a place could know you. Whether a landscape could recognise something in a person that the person didn't recognise in themselves.*

He had said the words in his head before Mr Ashby had finished saying them. He had been thinking about them for months, in his own terms, without quite knowing that Ned had been thinking about them too — in a school PSHE journal, in October, in the matter-of-fact way Ned thought about everything.

He started the car and drove home.

---

Ned was at the kitchen table when he came in — homework finished, the mapping notebook open, a mug of tea he had made for himself. He looked up when Edmund came through the door.

"It ran late," he said.

"Slightly." Edmund hung up his coat. "I saw your map on the wall. And your writing."

Ned looked at the table. "Which one?"

"The hill that was there before the road."

"Oh." A pause. "That one."

Edmund sat down. Put the kettle on. "Mr Ashby thinks well of you."

"What did he say?"

Edmund told him. Carefully, accurately, without softening or excess. The exceptional English. The geography maps. The quality of attention in the history unit. The adequate PE, which he delivered with the same level tone as the rest, and which produced the brief internal version of a laugh in Ned's face.

"What else?" Ned said.

"He asked what you read at home. I told him."

Ned looked up. "What did he say?"

"He said you were ten." Edmund made the tea. "I said you found it manageable."

Ned absorbed this. "Did he mind?"

"I don't think so. I think he found it — interesting." Edmund brought the tea to the table and sat down. "He mentioned your PSHE journal."

Something moved in Ned's expression — not alarm, but a slight attention, the way he attended to things that were closer to him than he usually let on. "What about it?"

"He said you wrote about whether a place could know you." Edmund looked at his tea. "Whether a landscape could recognise something in a person that the person didn't recognise in themselves."

A silence.

"He said that?" Ned said.

"His words."

Ned looked at the mapping notebook. At the tea. At nothing in particular.

"I didn't think he'd read it properly," he said. "The PSHE journal. I thought they just ticked it and moved on."

"It appears he read it properly."

"Oh." Another pause. "What did you say?"

"I said I imagined assessment-wise it was difficult."

The internal laugh again, slightly more present than before. "Yeah," Ned said. "It probably is."

Edmund drank his tea. Ned went back to the mapping notebook, adding something small in the margin with a pencil. The kitchen range went on being the range. Outside the October dark was complete.

"Edmund," Ned said, without looking up.

"Yes."

"Thank you for going."

Edmund looked at him — the familiar bent head, the pencil moving, the careful writing. The boy at the kitchen table who had been at this kitchen table for three months now and who had written in a school PSHE journal about whether a landscape could know you.

"Of course," Edmund said. Which was the right answer, and which meant: this is not a thing that requires thanks. This is simply what happens.

Ned went on with his notebook.

---

*Draft notes: Three movements. The preparation — Edmund navigating the booking system with patient determination, filing the confirmation email, saying nothing to Ned. The appointment — the corridor, Ned's map and the creative writing extract, ten minutes with Mr Ashby. The debrief — Edmund coming home, Ned having made tea for himself, the careful accurate account of what was said. The PSHE journal: whether a place could know you, whether a landscape could recognise something in a person that the person didn't recognise in themselves. Ned surprised that Mr Ashby read it properly. Edmund's closing answer to Ned's thank you: of course. Which is simply what happens.*

*Mr Ashby is the scene's revelation. He is a careful and attentive teacher who has been watching Ned since September and has registered something he can't fully account for. His question — is he all right, the real version, not the professional one — and Edmund's answer: yes, increasingly all right. The transfer of information between two people who both know more than they are saying, and trust what they can see.*

*The PSHE journal entry is the scene's emotional centre — Ned having articulated, in a school journal in October, the thing Edmund has been thinking about for thirty years. Neither of them knowing the other had been thinking it. Both of them, now, knowing.*
