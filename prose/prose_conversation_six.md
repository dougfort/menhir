# Prose Draft — Conversation Six (November)

---

It was a Tuesday in November, which meant the days were short and the lane home from school was already dark by half past four.

Ned came in and put his bag on the hook and his school shoes on the rack with less attention than usual. Edmund, in the kitchen, noticed this — the slight imprecision of it, the shoes not quite aligned — without saying anything. He put the kettle on.

Ned sat down at the table and looked at nothing in particular.

Edmund made tea. Set it in front of Ned. Sat down at his own end of the table with a book he was unlikely to read.

He waited.

---

The thing that had happened was this:

In computing class, the screen in front of Ned had flickered. Not dramatically — a brief irregularity, the image stuttering once and then resolving. The teacher hadn't noticed. The boy next to Ned — Matthew something, they were not particular friends — had glanced at the screen and then at his own screen and then back at the board. He had not mentioned it.

But Ned had felt it. The particular quality of it — the familiar signature, the small emanation of wrongness — and known immediately what it was. And then had spent the rest of computing class doing exactly what he always did: still, careful, assessing. Waiting to see if anyone would connect the flicker to him. Waiting for the familiar sequence to begin.

It had not continued. It had been one moment, small, contained. Nobody was looking at him differently. The class had ended and the day had continued and nothing else had happened.

But the sequence had begun in Ned's interior, as it always began, even when nothing followed: the assessment, the monitoring, the slow armouring. The checking of exits. The return of a vigilance he had, without quite noticing, let soften over the months.

He had cycled home in the November dark and by the time he arrived at Venn Court he was carrying something he didn't know how to put down.

---

Edmund did not ask immediately. He read his book — or appeared to — while Ned sat with his tea and the kitchen range went about its business and the house made its small night-sounds. The tawny owl in the orchard, early. The third stair, somewhere above them, adjusting to the cold.

After a while Edmund said, without looking up from his book: "Something happened today."

Not a question. An observation, offered lightly, which Ned could take or leave.

Ned looked at his tea. "Something small," he said. "At school. The screen flickered in computing."

"Your screen."

"Yes."

Edmund turned a page. He had not read the page. "Has it happened here? Since the first weeks."

Ned looked up. "How did you — " He stopped.

"The lamp," Edmund said. He closed his book and set it aside. "At your end of the table. It flickered when you first came. I changed the bulb. It continued for a while and then it settled." He looked at Ned with the expression that meant he was going to say something carefully and meant every word of it. "My watch has been keeping better time since September."

A silence.

"You noticed," Ned said.

"Yes."

"I didn't — I thought here it wasn't — " Ned stopped. Started again. His voice was even, as it always was, but there was something underneath it that was not quite even. "It's been quiet here. Mostly."

"I know," Edmund said. "I have a theory about why." He paused. "I have been wanting to tell you my theory for some time. But I thought it should wait until you were ready to hear it."

Ned was very still. Not the watchful stillness of his first weeks — a different quality, the stillness of someone who has turned fully toward something.

Edmund got up to refill the kettle. As he passed behind Ned's chair he rested his hand briefly on Ned's shoulder — not the careful unpractised gesture of the stone in July, but something easier, more certain. A hand that knew the shoulder it was resting on. He continued to the kettle without comment.

"You can tell me," Ned said. "The theory."

"I will," Edmund said. "But first — if you want to — you could tell me what happened. Not today. Before." He filled the kettle and set it on the range. "What it was like, in the other houses."

He said this with his back still to Ned. Not pressing, not watching for the reaction. Giving Ned the space of not being looked at.

A long silence.

"I don't know where to start," Ned said.

"The earliest one you remember," Edmund said. "Or any of them. Wherever feels right."

He turned from the range. Sat back down. Folded his hands on the table and gave Ned his full, unhurried attention.

---

Ned told him about the first house he could actually remember — not the aunt and uncle, whose house was before memory, but House Two, the cousins. He was seven. He had been there for two months.

He did not tell it as a list of incidents. He told it as a feeling — the way it felt to arrive somewhere new and know, in the first few days, that it was wrong. Not wrong the way a bad place was wrong — not cruel or frightening. Just wrong in the way a shoe that doesn't fit is wrong. The wrongness coming off the walls, off the carpet, off the particular quality of the light in the room they gave him.

And then the things starting. Small things, electrical things, the router and the lamp and the cousin's phone. He hadn't known at the time that it was connected to him. He hadn't understood the pattern yet. He had just known that things were happening and that the adults were becoming different with him — a tightness in the house, a carefulness — and that this meant the arrangement was going to end.

He told Edmund what it was like to know this was coming. The way you start conserving yourself — pulling back, making yourself smaller, not asking for things because asking for things creates attachment and attachment makes the leaving worse. The practice of not hoping.

He told it steadily, without drama, in the flat precise voice he used for things that had required a lot of practice to become flat and precise.

Edmund listened. He did not move. He did not make sounds of sympathy or lean forward or touch Ned's arm. He simply received it, with the same quality of attention he brought to the stone in the early morning, to the technical papers, to the Lord of the Rings read aloud. The fullness of his attention, given without condition.

When Ned stopped — not at the end of the story, because there wasn't quite an end yet, but at a pause where something had been said — Edmund was quiet for a moment.

"I thought it might have happened before," he said. "More than once."

"Every house," Ned said. "Every place I've been."

"Yes." Edmund looked at his folded hands. "Do you know — " He paused, finding the words carefully. "Do you know that it stopped, here, after the first weeks?"

"I thought — " Ned began.

"Not stopped entirely. The lamp, occasionally. My watch. But quieter than it would have been, I think, in another place." He looked up. "I have a theory about why. Would you like to hear it?"

Ned nodded.

---

Edmund told him.

Not everything — not the full thirty years, not Lethbridge and the magnetometer studies, not the technical architecture of the private theory he had been building all summer. Just the essential thing, simply put.

That what Ned carried — and Edmund said *carried* as if it were the most natural word, as if he had been looking for it and found it — was not a disorder. Was not something wrong. Was a sensitivity, a particular relationship with certain kinds of place, that had the quality of expressing itself in small disturbances because in the places Ned had been there was nowhere for it to go.

And that at Venn Court there was somewhere for it to go.

"The stone," Ned said.

"I think so," Edmund said. "I'm not certain. I've been watching for thirty years and I still don't know exactly what the stone does or how it does it. But I think — " He paused. "I think this place was not the wrong place. I think it may be rather specifically the right place."

Ned was quiet for a long time.

Outside the November dark had settled fully. The range went on being the range. The owl called once more from the orchard and then was silent.

"Why didn't it happen sooner?" Ned said. "If this was the right place. Why did it take — houses. Years."

Edmund was quiet for a moment. "I don't know," he said. "I think about this." He looked at the table. "I have been here for most of my life, and the stone has been here far longer than that. Neither of us arranged for you to come." He paused. "But here you are."

He said this simply, as a fact. *But here you are.* Not as sentiment, not as reassurance. Just the fact of it, which was perhaps the most reassuring thing he could have said.

Ned looked at him. His brown eyes steady, as they always were, but with something in them that had not been there before — not open exactly, because Ned was never quite open in the way most people were open. Something more like a door ajar. A degree of permission.

"I've never told anyone," he said. "What it's like. Not the things that happen — the adults always notice the things eventually. But what it's like, from the inside."

"I know," Edmund said. "Thank you for telling me."

He said it without excess. Without making more of it than it was — which was, in fact, considerable.

---

They sat for a while longer. The tea had gone cold; Edmund made more without asking. The kitchen held them in its usual way, warm and old and unhurried.

After a while Ned said: "What happens next? With your theory."

Edmund considered this. "I keep watching," he said. "And you tell me more, when you have more to tell. And we see what we understand together that neither of us understands separately." He paused. "I don't think there is a resolution, exactly. I think there is — an ongoing arrangement."

Ned almost smiled. "An ongoing arrangement," he said.

"Between you and the stone, perhaps. Between you and this place." Edmund picked up his book again, which meant the conversation was not ending but had said what it needed to say for tonight. "It has been making arrangements for rather longer than either of us."

Ned picked up his tea.

They sat in the warm kitchen for a while, not talking, the Lord of the Rings waiting upstairs in its usual place on Ned's bedside table.

---

Later, reading — they were in the middle of the chapter called *The Shadow of the Past*, which Edmund had read before and knew was coming, and which had always seemed to him one of the most accurate descriptions of receiving difficult knowledge with equanimity — Ned stopped at a passage and looked at it for a moment before reading.

*I wish it need not have happened in my time,* Frodo says.

*So do I,* Gandalf says, *and so do all who live to see such times. But that is not for them to decide. All we have to decide is what to do with the time that is given us.*

Ned read it without comment and passed the book to Edmund.

Edmund read the next passage and passed it back.

They went on.

---

*Draft notes: The scene has three movements. The arrival — Ned coming in with something he is carrying, Edmund seeing it, the space created in silence before anything is said. The disclosure — Ned telling Edmund about House Two, the feeling of it rather than the catalogue of incidents, what it is like from the inside: the wrongness, the pulling back, the practice of not hoping. Edmund listening with full unhurried attention. Edmund's theory — the essential thing simply put, not the full thirty years, just: what you carry is not a disorder, this place has somewhere for it to go. And the close — the ongoing arrangement, the Lord of the Rings, the passage from The Shadow of the Past, which Edmund reads knowing it is coming. Neither of them comments on the passage. They go on.*

*The physical moment: Edmund's hand on Ned's shoulder as he passes to the kettle — no longer the unpractised gesture of the first stone visit. A hand that knows the shoulder it is resting on. Brief, uncommented on, more certain than before.*

*The conversation does not resolve everything. It is the opening of the history, not the history itself. More will come, in stages, over time. But Ned has said the essential thing — what it is like from the inside — and Edmund has said the essential thing in return: not a disorder, the right place. The door is ajar. That is enough for tonight.*
