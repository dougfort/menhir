# Prose Draft — Three Scenes: The Papers, The Stone, The Door

---

## Scene One — The Technical Papers (Kitchen Table)

The papers were dense.

Ned had known they would be dense — Edmund had said so — but he had not quite anticipated the specific quality of their density, which was not like a difficult novel or even like Vilmorin at its most elaborate. This was a different kind of difficult. Numbers appeared. Graphs. Sentences that began with something like *the electromagnetic field strength at the site of the menhir was measured at intervals of* and then kept going for quite a long time.

He was at the kitchen table with a glass of apple juice, a pencil, and three photocopied papers from Edmund's library. The pencil was for underlining things he wanted to come back to, which was a system he had developed with Vilmorin and which worked tolerably well.

Edmund was at the far end of the table with his own papers, which were the manuscript, and was apparently having a better morning than Ned was because he had been writing in a steady quiet way for an hour without stopping.

"What's a magnetometer?" Ned said.

Edmund looked up. "An instrument for measuring the strength of a magnetic field."

"Like a compass but more precise?"

"Much more precise. A compass tells you direction. A magnetometer tells you intensity."

Ned looked back at the paper. "Someone brought one to Avebury," he said. "In 1978. And found anomalies."

"Several people have done similar work at several sites. The results are contested."

"What does contested mean in science?"

Edmund set his pen down. "It means that some people's measurements support a conclusion and other people's measurements don't, and nobody is certain enough about either set of measurements to declare the matter settled."

Ned turned this over. "So it might be real or it might not."

"More precisely: the evidence exists but its interpretation is disputed."

"That's basically everything about standing stones though," Ned said. "Isn't it. You can look at the same thing and get completely different answers."

Edmund looked at him with the expression that meant mild surprise found acceptable. "That is a fair characterisation of the field, yes."

Ned went back to the paper. He read another paragraph and underlined something. Then: "Lethbridge. T.C. Lethbridge. You've got his books."

"On the lower shelves. Green spines."

"Will said he was a nutcase."

"Will," said Edmund carefully, "is eleven. Lethbridge was a Cambridge archaeologist who followed his observations to conclusions that many of his colleagues found uncomfortable. Whether that makes him a nutcase depends on whether his observations were sound." He paused. "Some of them appear to have been."

Ned absorbed this. "So he might be right about some things and wrong about others."

"That is the case with most people."

Ned looked up from the paper. "Including you?"

Edmund picked his pen back up. "Including me," he agreed, with the equanimity of a man who had made his peace with this some years ago.

Ned returned to the paper. He read for a while in silence. Edmund wrote. The kitchen range made its small domestic sounds. Outside the window the orchard was still in the late morning, a wood pigeon somewhere in it repeating its question to no one in particular.

"Can I ask you something?" Ned said, without looking up.

"Yes."

"When you go to the stone. Does your watch go wrong?"

Edmund was quiet for a moment. "Yes," he said. "It loses time. Two or three minutes over an hour."

"Every time?"

"Every time I've checked."

Ned looked up now. "How long has that been happening?"

"As long as I've been wearing a watch. Twenty-odd years." A pause. "I noticed it fairly early and then — stopped mentioning it, since there was no one to mention it to."

Ned looked at him steadily. He was doing his reading-people thing — that precise, attending gaze.

"Does the lamp in the kitchen flicker?" he said.

Edmund was very still. "Sometimes," he said carefully. "Near your end of the table."

"I've noticed that," Ned said. He looked down at the papers again. Turned a page. "Sorry," he added, to the paper rather than to Edmund, in the tone of someone apologising not for causing a problem but for not being able to help it.

"Don't be," Edmund said. He said it quietly and meant it entirely.

They went back to their respective work. The wood pigeon continued its enquiry. After a while Edmund found that the sentence he had been trying to write for three days had arrived, more or less complete, and he wrote it down without fuss before it could change its mind.

## Scene Two — Edmund at the Stone (Early Morning)

He went earlier than usual the next morning, before Ned was up.

The orchard was wet with dew and the light was the thin particular light of early August, when the year has not yet turned but something in the quality of morning knows it is going to. Edmund walked through the trees at his usual pace and out the other side into the meadow, and across the meadow to the mound, and up the mound to the stone.

He put his hand on it. Warm, as always. Dry.

He sat on his boulder and looked at it for a while without thinking about anything specific.

After a time the specific thoughts arrived, as they usually did — the residue of the previous day sorting itself into order while he sat here in the quality of time the stone offered.

The boy had asked about his watch.

He had asked about the kitchen lamp.

Not — *is there something wrong with your watch, Edmund, have you had it serviced* — but the specific question, the targeted question, the question of someone who was already in possession of related information and was checking it against their own.

Edmund looked at the stone's surface — the concavities, the marks that might be weathering and might not. He had looked at these for thirty years and they had not resolved into meaning. He had come to accept that they would not. Some things did not resolve. The question was what you did with them.

He thought about the papers Ned was reading. Lethbridge. The magnetometer studies. The contested evidence. He had given them to Ned expecting the boy to find them interesting — he had not quite anticipated the quality of attention Ned would bring. The underlining with the pencil. The specific questions. The word *contested* examined and turned over and placed correctly.

*That's basically everything about standing stones though. You can look at the same thing and get completely different answers.*

Edmund's watch was on his wrist. He glanced at it. He had been here twelve minutes, and it showed ten.

He thought about the incidents in other houses — the fish, the electricity, the objects moving. He had been thinking about these since the letter arrived, had been building a quiet private case from the small evidence available. The compass had confirmed it, or confirmed enough. A boy whose particular quality of — he did not want to call it sensitivity, the word was imprecise — a boy whose particular relationship with the world expressed itself in small perturbations around him. Deniable. Frightening to people who had no framework for them. Perfectly comprehensible, in outline, to a man who had spent thirty years reading about such things and living beside a stone that did something to magnetic fields and the accurate measurement of time.

The boy was not haunted. The boy was not damaged or disturbed or in need of anything other than — this. Somewhere that understood what he carried. Somewhere the perturbations had somewhere to go.

Edmund looked at the stone.

*It thinks the stone is where north should be.*

He had been turning this over since Ned said it. There was something in it that was not quite right as physics and was exactly right as something else. The compass was not malfunctioning. It was responding to a real field, a genuine anomaly. And the needle pointed at the stone not because the stone was magnetic north but because — this was the thought Edmund had been approaching carefully for two days — because whatever the stone was doing to fields, to time, to the quality of the air in its immediate vicinity, the compass registered as *orientation*. As the direction you should be facing.

He had no language for this that satisfied him. He had been a writer for twenty years and the language failed him here as it had always failed him at the stone.

But the boy had found language. *Not ignoring. Just something else going on. Something massive. That isn't us.* Sitting on a boulder at nine years old, two visits in, and arriving at the thing Edmund had been circling since his twenties.

He was going to have to think carefully about what that meant. About what to do with it. About the conversations that were coming — he could see the shape of them from here — and what they would ask of him. What it would mean to be the first adult who did not flinch when Ned showed him something strange. What it would mean to be trusted with the history of a child who had been, until now, entirely unable to trust anyone with it.

Edmund was not a man who made decisions quickly. He sat with things. He turned them over.

He sat with this for a while, in the particular quality of time the stone offered, and let it settle into its proper weight.

Then he stood, put his hands in his pockets, and walked back through the meadow toward the house. He could smell toast from the kitchen. Ned was up.

## Scene Three — Conversation Five (Kitchen Table, An Evening)

It was perhaps four days later.

They had been reading after dinner — Ned on his side of the kitchen table with the Blackwood, Edmund on his with a proof copy someone had sent him, which was not very good but required a response. The kitchen was warm and dim, the range working steadily, the window showing the last pale streak of the August evening.

Ned put the Blackwood down.

"Doesn't it bother you?" he said.

Edmund looked up. "What specifically."

"The compass. At the stone."

Edmund set the proof copy aside. "No," he said. "Should it?"

Ned looked at him with the reading-people gaze, checking something. "Most people would think it was strange."

"It is strange," Edmund said. "Strange things happen near the stone. I've been observing this for thirty years." He paused. "The watch. The lamp. There are one or two other things. Strange things don't particularly bother me. I find them interesting."

Ned was quiet for a moment.

"What else have you observed?" Edmund said. "Other than the compass."

The question arrived without weight, without special emphasis — the same tone as asking about the beans or the pears. A genuine enquiry, framed as such. Nothing loaded in it.

Ned went very still.

Edmund had asked this knowing it was a significant question. He had been at the stone four days ago, he had been thinking about it carefully, he had formed his outline of an answer before asking it. But he asked it simply, because the simple version was the right one, because Ned could hear the difference between an adult performing casualness and an adult who was actually curious, and he was the latter.

The kitchen held them both.

"Sometimes things move," Ned said. He said it to the table surface rather than to Edmund. His voice was even, careful, not quite flat. "Objects. When I'm — in a particular kind of place."

"What kind of place?"

"A place that doesn't feel right." A pause. "That doesn't — suit. I don't know how else to say it."

"And this place?"

Ned looked up. Looked at Edmund directly, which he had not done a moment ago. His brown eyes were steady, and behind the steadiness was something that might have been held there for a very long time, waiting to see if this was the moment it was safe to let go.

"This place feels right," he said.

Edmund nodded. Just once, without making anything of it.

"The objects that move," he said. "Does it happen often?"

"It used to happen more." Ned looked back at the table. "When I was — younger. When I moved to a new place it would be worse for a bit." A pause. "It doesn't really happen here."

"No," Edmund said. "I don't think it does."

Ned looked up sharply. "You've noticed something though. Haven't you."

It was not quite a question.

"The lamp," Edmund said. "In the first week. I changed the bulb. The flickering continued. And then — gradually — it stopped." He held Ned's gaze. "And my watch has been keeping better time than it did in July."

Ned said nothing. He was very still, the way he was still when something was happening in him that required all his attention.

"I don't think you did anything wrong," Edmund said carefully. "I think you brought something here that had, in other places, nowhere suitable to go. And here — " he paused, finding the right words — "there may be somewhere for it to go."

The kitchen held this.

"The stone," Ned said.

"Perhaps."

Ned looked at the table. Turned his glass of apple juice very slightly. Set it back.

"In other places," he said. "Before here. It was — " He stopped. Started again. "It happened a lot. People would — " He stopped again.

"People would notice," Edmund said quietly.

"Yes."

A silence.

"And then I'd move," Ned said. "To a new place."

Edmund said nothing. He waited, the way he waited at his desk for sentences — without forcing, without filling the space.

"It happened in every house," Ned said. "Every place I've been." He was still looking at the table. "I didn't know I was doing it." He paused. "I still don't know if I am. Doing it. Or if it just — happens."

"I don't know either," Edmund said. "Not with certainty. But I have some thoughts about it, when you're ready to hear them."

Ned looked up. "Now?"

Edmund considered. "Not tonight. Tonight I think you've said enough. And I think," he said, with care, "that you've been holding that for quite a long time, and saying even this much is — not nothing."

Ned looked at him for a moment. Something moved in his expression — brief, controlled, the way a nine year old controls something when they have had a lot of practice.

"All right," he said. He picked up the Blackwood again. Opened it to his place.

Edmund picked up the proof copy. It was still not very good. He read it anyway, in the comfortable warm kitchen, while Ned read Blackwood across the table.

After a while, without looking up from his book, Ned said: "Edmund."

"Yes."

"Thank you." The same quiet word as the night Edmund had said he would register him at school. The same tone — offered to the book rather than to the face, the only way it could be said yet.

"You're welcome," said Edmund, which was also the right answer.

They read until the kitchen was dark and Edmund made tea, and the wood pigeon outside had long since gone to sleep, and the stone stood at the end of the meadow in the August night, warm and dry and entirely itself.

*Draft notes: The three scenes form a single movement. Scene One establishes Ned's engagement with the material and the first exchange about Edmund's watch — Ned's specific targeted question showing he is already correlating information. His sorry, said to the paper, is the scene's most important note: he has lived so long with the assumption that what he does is wrong that the apology is automatic. Edmund's "don't be" — quiet, complete — is the counter to years of that assumption. Scene Two is Edmund's interior at the turning point — the private theory solidifying, the weight of what the coming conversations will ask of him. The smell of toast at the end: Ned is up, life continues, he goes back. Scene Three is the pivot. Ned's disclosure is minimal — sometimes things move, in places that don't feel right — but it is the first disclosure to anyone. Edmund's reception: no flinching, no alarm, only *I have some thoughts about it when you're ready to hear them.* The delay — not tonight — is exactly right: Edmund recognising that Ned has done something significant and not pushing past the limit of what can be said in one evening. Ned's second thank you echoes the school registration scene: the same word, the same tone, offered to the book. Two moments of genuine gratitude from a boy who has had very little to be grateful for.*
