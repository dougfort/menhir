# Prose Draft — Ned's First Impression of Somerset

---

The train had been running through flat country for a while, the kind that didn't ask anything of you — warehouses, retail parks, the backs of houses with their ordinary gardens. Ned had his forehead against the glass and was watching without really watching, the way you do when your mind is elsewhere.

Then something changed.

He wasn't sure at first what it was. The land simply opened — spread itself out wide and flat in a way that felt deliberate, as if it had been waiting for the right moment. The sky doubled in size. There were flat green fields stretching away to a distant line of low hills, and between the fields long straight channels of water catching the afternoon light. Everything was very still. There were no buildings worth mentioning.

Ned sat up straighter.

He had lived in cities his whole life, or the edges of cities, or the kinds of towns that are really just bits of cities that got separated. He knew what land looked like from a train window. It looked like this was getting past as quickly as possible on the way to somewhere else.

This didn't look like that.

This looked like somewhere that had been here for a very long time and intended to stay. The flatness wasn't emptiness — it was patience. The water in the channels wasn't going anywhere quickly. The hills on the horizon had the rounded, settled look of things that had been worn down to exactly the right shape centuries ago and hadn't moved since.

He pressed his nose against the glass.

Somewhere out there, he knew, was Glastonbury. He had looked it up on his phone before the signal went patchy somewhere after Bristol. Ancient, it had said. Sacred. He hadn't quite known what sacred meant for a place rather than a church, but looking out the window now he thought he might be beginning to understand. There was something about the light on that flat water. Something about the way the hills sat.

Old, he thought. It's just very, very old.

He had never thought that about a place before. He had thought it about objects occasionally — a fossil in a museum case, a Roman coin in a school display — but those were small things you could put in a box. This was enormous and it was outside and it was older than anything he had ever seen.

The train ran on. The levels gave way gradually to slightly higher ground, hedged fields, a church tower here and there. The ordinary world reassembling itself.

But something had happened in those few minutes that Ned could not quite account for. He felt, in a way he had no words for yet, that he had been seen. Not by anyone in particular. Just — noticed. By the land itself, perhaps. By whatever it was that was underneath the land and had been there long before anyone put fields and water channels on top of it.

He sat back in his seat.

His backpack was on his lap, the notebooks inside it. He thought about opening one and writing something down, but he didn't know what he would write. Not yet.

He watched Somerset go past the window instead, and Somerset watched him back.

---

*Draft notes: This passage establishes Ned's observant, interior quality before he arrives at Venn Court. The land noticing him back is the first quiet suggestion of the numinous — presented entirely through his perception, nothing stated directly. The Glastonbury reference grounds it geographically without requiring any fantasy. The notebooks almost opened but not — something saved for later.*
