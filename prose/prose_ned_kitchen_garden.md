# Prose Draft — Ned and the Kitchen Garden

---

The book was enormous.

Edmund had brought it from the library without apparent ceremony — simply appeared in the kitchen doorway one morning holding it, a dense Victorian volume with cloth boards and a gilt vignette of asparagus on the cover, and held it out.

"I thought this might be useful," he said. "If you're going to take on the kitchen garden."

Ned accepted it with both hands, which was necessary. It was heavier than it looked. He turned it over. *The Vegetable Garden*, it said on the spine, *Vilmorin-Andrieux*. 1885.

"It's old," Ned said.

"Vegetables haven't changed much," said Edmund, and went back to the library.

---

The kitchen garden was walled, south-facing, with the particular warmth of enclosed spaces on a July morning. The brick inner wall still held the night's coolness on the shaded side, but the beds ran warm and the smell of the place — earth and green and something slightly medicinal from the herbs along the south wall — was unlike anything Ned had encountered before.

He stood inside the gate with the book under his arm and looked at the beds.

He had been sent to check the beans. Edmund had said the beans on the second row looked ready. Ned located the second row, found the beans, and examined one. He was not certain what *ready* looked like. He opened the book.

The volume fell open somewhere in the middle — *Kidney Beans*, said the heading, in the elaborate font of another century. He read three sentences, turned back to the beans, read the sentences again. The prose was Victorian and intricate and used words like *tendril* and *bracteole* without apology. He persevered.

After a while he concluded that the beans were ready. He picked them into the trug that Edmund had left by the gate, moving down the row methodically, which was how he did most things.

---

He came back the next morning without being asked.

He had brought Vilmorin-Andrieux with him again, under his arm, and he spent some time in the corner by the pears reading about espaliering, which the book covered in a section of approximately fourteen pages with detailed engravings. The pears in the corner were trained against the old brick in a pattern that now made sense to him, and he traced it with one finger without touching the fruit.

A spider had made a web in the angle between two trained branches, perfectly engineered, catching the light. He looked at it for a while. Then he went to check on the tomatoes, which the book described as requiring, in its polite Victorian way, attentive and regular attention.

Edmund, working at his desk in the library bay window overlooking the orchard, noticed the boy appearing and disappearing among the kitchen garden beds from the corner of his eye. He had a large and slightly ridiculous book under his arm and moved with the careful attention of someone doing something properly. Edmund found he could not concentrate on his own work for watching this, and after a while gave up trying and simply watched.

The boy moved down a row of beans with a trug and a considered expression and did not look up.

Edmund returned to his manuscript.

---

A week into it, at dinner, Ned said: "The carrots want thinning."

Edmund looked at him.

"Vilmorin says they need six inches between them. These are closer than that."

Edmund considered the carrots, which he had been neglecting. "You're right," he said. "Will you?"

"Yes," said Ned. "I'll need the fork with the short handle."

"In the stable block. On the left wall."

Ned nodded. He ate a bean, which he had picked that morning. He did not say this. It would have seemed like drawing attention to himself, and he was not yet — quite — comfortable drawing attention to himself. But he noticed the bean, and that it was good, and he filed this away in the part of himself that kept account of things.

---

By August the kitchen garden was partly his in a way that had not been formally established and did not need to be. He knew which beds needed water, which tomatoes were getting too heavy for their canes, where the mint had gone its own way entirely and required management. He kept Vilmorin-Andrieux on the low shelf by the garden gate, under a piece of old oilcloth to keep the damp off, and consulted it with lessening frequency as summer went on. The book was still there when he needed it. He liked knowing it was there.

One morning he found Edmund in the kitchen garden ahead of him, examining the pears.

Ned stopped inside the gate.

"They'll be ready in September," Edmund said, without looking round. "The Doyenné du Comice especially."

"Is that what they are?"

"It's in Vilmorin. Pages four hundred and seventeen to four hundred and twenty."

Ned retrieved the book from under its oilcloth and found the page. He read for a moment. Then he looked at the pear trees and back at the page, and something in the engravings — the shape of the leaf, the particular hang of the fruit — shifted from being information into being recognition. He had looked at these trees every morning for three weeks. He knew them. Now he knew their name.

He closed the book.

"I'll be here in September," he said. Quietly, to the trees rather than to Edmund, in the tone he used for things he was not quite ready to say out loud.

Edmund did not comment on this. He moved to the next pear and examined its ripeness with his thumb.

"I know," he said.

---

*Draft notes: The Victorian book is the scene's comic spine — too large, too technical, completely serious. Ned taking it equally seriously is his character exactly. The scene works through accumulation: his return the next morning without being asked, his report about the carrots at dinner, the fork with the short handle. Each of these is Ned inserting himself into the estate's practical life without fanfare. The pear scene at the end carries the emotional weight — the engravings becoming recognition, knowledge becoming belonging. Ned's "I'll be here in September" is said to the trees rather than to Edmund, which is the only way he can say it yet. Edmund's "I know" is the scene's final note: the quiet, unhurried certainty of a man who has already decided.*
