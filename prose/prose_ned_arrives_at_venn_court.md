# Prose Draft — Ned Arrives at Venn Court (Revised)

---

The road had been getting narrower for a while. Ned had been watching it happen — hedges pressing in on both sides, high and old, the tarmac giving way to a track with grass growing in a stripe down the middle. The kind of road that didn't know it was a road anymore.

Edmund drove without hurrying, which seemed to be how he did most things.

The track turned once, and then the hedges opened and there was a house.

Ned looked at it.

It wasn't what he'd been imagining, although he hadn't been imagining anything in particular, having learned not to. It was stone — the same yellowish grey as the lane walls, as if everything here was made of the same material and always had been. It had a proper front, tall windows and a kind of dignity about it, but you could see that the front wasn't really the point. The point was the back — a yard, a woodshed with its door standing open, a door to the house itself that looked like it hadn't been locked in years.

Old, Ned thought. Not the same as the levels had been old — not wide and flat and patient. This was a different kind of old. Layered. Like the house had been added to and subtracted from over a very long time and had simply absorbed all of it.

He got out of the car and stood in the yard. The air smelled different from anywhere he'd been before. Green, somehow. And underneath the green, something older still — stone and earth and a faint sweetness that might have been the orchard he could see beyond a low wall.

Edmund was already at the back door. He didn't say *well, here we are* or *what do you think* or any of the things adults usually said to fill up space. He just held the door open.

Ned went in.

---

The kitchen was large and warm and slightly dim, and it was immediately obvious that this was where Edmund actually lived. A long table ran down the middle, its surface scattered with papers and a coffee mug and a book left open face-down. A range against the far wall gave off a steady, serious heat. The floor was stone, worn into smooth dips and rises that recorded generations of feet without admitting it.

It smelled of woodsmoke and old paper and something cooking slowly.

Ned stood just inside the door and looked at all of it.

He had a system, in new places. You didn't react. You watched. You found out where the exits were, which adults were in charge and what they wanted from you, whether the atmosphere was one you could manage. It had served him well across more houses than he'd counted.

The trouble was that this house didn't behave like somewhere you needed a system for. It felt — he searched for the word and couldn't quite find it — settled. As if it had been the same temperature and the same quality of light for a very long time and had no intention of changing.

Edmund put the kettle on without asking.

Ned put his backpack on the table, then took it off again and held it instead.

"I'll show you your room," Edmund said, "and then there's tea, if you want it."

He said *if you want it* rather than *you must be tired* or *you've had a long day*, and Ned noticed this. Edmund, he was beginning to understand, didn't assume things about you. It was an unusual quality in an adult.

---

The room was up a staircase that turned unexpectedly halfway, and along a corridor that had a slight slope to it, as if the house had settled over the centuries into a more comfortable position. Edmund opened a door and stood back.

The room was plain and clean and had clearly been prepared — a bed with fresh sheets, a rug on the wooden floor, a window that looked out over the orchard, the old trees still carrying the last of the season's apples.

And a bookshelf. Not empty — there were three books on it, standing upright with a small gap between each, as if they had been placed there with some thought.

Ned went to them before he went to the window, which was not his usual habit.

The first was small and squarish with a pale cover — *The Little Prince*, and old enough that the cover had softened at the corners. He opened it carefully. The illustrations were the author's own, he could tell — simple and strange and completely sure of themselves. A small figure on a tiny planet. A rose under a glass. A fox in the grass.

The second was *The Wind in the Willows*, a proper edition, the kind with weight to it, and inside were illustrations that made him stand still for a moment — a riverbank so particular and inhabited that it felt like somewhere you could actually find, if you knew which way to go.

The third was *Treasure Island*. He had heard of it but never read it. The illustrations were large and painted and slightly alarming — cliffs and pirates and a boy not much older than Ned himself standing at the edge of something dangerous and not stepping back.

He held it for a moment and then put it back carefully.

These were not books that had been bought for him. He understood that without quite knowing how. They were old in the particular way of things that have been kept — not stored, but kept, which is different. Someone had held these books when they were young, and had held onto them since.

He looked at Edmund, who was leaning in the doorway with the expression Ned was beginning to recognize — watchful, quiet, not requiring anything.

"These are yours," Ned said. It wasn't a question.

Edmund looked at the shelf. "They were," he said. "I thought they might do better being read."

Ned turned back to the books. He wanted to say something but wasn't sure what, so he said nothing, which felt like the right decision. He straightened *The Little Prince* very slightly, so that its spine lined up with the others.

"Tea," said Edmund, and went back along the tilted corridor.

Ned looked around the room once more. The worn rug. The window and the orchard beyond it. The three books on the shelf, waiting.

He put his backpack down on the bed. Just set it down.

He wasn't sure why that felt like anything. But it did.

---

*Draft notes: The stone is removed entirely from this scene — its moment will come separately and properly. The books now carry the weight of the scene's emotional turn. Ned going to the books before the window is a small but telling reversal of his usual habit — the first sign that Venn Court is already doing something to his system. Edmund's explanation — "I thought they might do better being read" — is characteristically oblique and characteristically generous. The straightening of The Little Prince is Ned's wordless response: a small act of care toward something he has already decided matters.*
