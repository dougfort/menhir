# Prose Draft — The Last Night of The Little Prince, and What Came After

---

They had been reading *The Little Prince* for just under three weeks.

It had become its own kind of time — the chair beside the bed, the small lamp, the book passed back and forth. They had settled into each other's reading pace. Edmund knew when Ned was going to slow down before he did. Ned had learned that Edmund's pauses before passing the book meant he had found something worth sitting with, not that he had lost his place. The ritual had absorbed itself into the structure of the house the way the range had and the kitchen table had and the particular creak of the third stair — present and unremarkable and, when Ned thought about it, which he tried not to because thinking about it felt like pressing on something that might not hold, essential.

This was the last night of it.

They had reached the ending — the little prince returning to his planet by way of the snake's bite, which Saint-Exupéry rendered with the quiet directness of someone who believed entirely in what he was saying. Edmund read the last pages at his usual pace, without slowing or quickening, without making anything of what was coming.

*It is here that the little prince appeared on Earth, and disappeared. Look at it carefully so that you will be sure to recognise it in case you travel some day to the African desert.*

A pause. Edmund turned the final page. Read on.

*And if a child comes to you, if he laughs, if he has golden hair, if he never answers your questions, you will know who he is. Then be kind, please. Tell me that he has come back.*

Edmund closed the book.

He sat for a moment. Ned was still propped against his pillows, the blanket pulled up, looking at the ceiling with the expression he wore when something had landed and he was letting it settle.

"He dies," Ned said. Not as a complaint. Just placing it.

"He goes home," Edmund said. "Whether that's the same thing is left open."

Ned considered the ceiling. "He misses his rose."

"Yes."

"Even though she's vain and difficult and complains all the time."

"Even so."

Another silence. The tawny owl called somewhere in the orchard, once. Ned's eyes moved toward the window and back.

"Is there going to be another book?" he said. Not anxious — measured. The question of someone confirming something they already intended to pursue.

"Yes," said Edmund.

"What?"

Edmund set *The Little Prince* on the bedside table and looked at it for a moment. Then he said: "Not tonight. Tonight let this one settle." He stood and put the chair back. "Sleep."

Ned looked at him. "You already know what it is."

"Yes."

"Is it long?"

"Very."

"How long?"

Edmund turned off the lamp. "Considerably longer than this one. Sleep, Ned."

In the dark he heard Ned settle into the pillow with the particular exhale of a boy who is going to think about the little prince for a while yet before he does anything as practical as sleeping.

Edmund went along the tilted corridor to his own room.

---

Two nights later he brought the book.

He produced it from behind his back as Ned was getting into bed, which was uncharacteristic — Edmund did not generally produce things from behind his back — and held it out. It was a proper edition, old, the spine faded green, the boards worn at the corners from years of handling.

*The Fellowship of the Ring.* And beneath that: *Being the first part of The Lord of the Rings.*

Ned took it and turned it over. Looked at the back. Turned it over again and opened it at random to the middle.

"There's a map," he said, finding it immediately, as Edmund had known he would.

"There are several."

Ned looked at the map with the focused attention he brought to all maps — tracing roads with one finger, finding the mountains, the forests, reading the names of places that did not exist in a lettering designed to look ancient. His finger stopped on something. "What's this?"

"Mordor."

"It's where the bad things are?"

"Broadly speaking."

Ned nodded. He closed the book and held it in his lap and looked at the cover. "How long will it take us? To read it."

Edmund sat down in the chair. "Months, I should think. There are three volumes. We'll be at it well into the school term."

Ned looked at him. Something passed very briefly across his face — relief, possibly, or something quieter and more fundamental than relief — and then he opened the book to the first page and held it toward Edmund.

"You start," he said.

Edmund took the book. Settled it. Found the place.

*When Mr. Bilbo Baggins of Bag End announced that he would shortly be celebrating his eleventy-first birthday with a party of special magnificence, there was much talk and excitement in Hobbiton.*

Ned pulled the blanket up. He was already listening with his whole body, the way he listened to things that mattered.

Edmund read on.

---

The chapters were longer than *The Little Prince* and the world was larger, requiring more of both of them. Ned had questions — more questions than he had asked during *The Little Prince*, which had offered less to ask about. What was a hobbit exactly. What was the Shire. Why did the elves call Gandalf Mithrandir. Edmund answered what he could and said *you'll find out* for the rest, which Ned accepted because Edmund said it in a way that made clear it was a promise rather than a deflection.

They moved through the book in the way they moved through everything together — unhurriedly, at their own pace. A chapter a night when there was time, half a chapter when there wasn't. Some nights Ned's reading grew quieter before they reached a stopping point, and Edmund would finish the passage and find him asleep with one hand still curved as if holding the book. Some nights Ned was more awake than Edmund and would read on past the point where Edmund would have stopped, pulling the story forward.

"Can I ask you something?" Ned said one evening, three weeks in, somewhere in the middle of the chapter called *The Shadow of the Past*. He had been holding the book for his turn and had not yet opened to the page.

"Yes."

"Tolkien. Did he know? About old things in the landscape. Like — actually know, not just invent it."

Edmund considered this. It was a careful question, precisely aimed. "He was a philologist. A scholar of languages — very old languages. He believed, I think, that old stories carried genuine memory. That what looked like myth might contain historical truth so old it had become unrecognisable."

Ned turned the book over in his hands. "So the old things in his story. The things that were there before everything else. He didn't just make those up."

"He made up the specifics. But the — shape of it. The sense that the landscape is older than the people in it, and carries things the people don't understand. I think he felt that was true."

Ned nodded slowly. He opened the book to the page and read, in his careful reading-aloud voice:

*I should like to know about Gollum and the Ring.*

*The history is long. It is long since the days of Isildur and the elder kings. I am not sure I can tell it all. But I will try — for, in this matter, I think it would be well if you understood what you are involved in.*

He stopped. Looked up at Edmund.

"That's what you were doing," he said. "At the stone. When you told me what you actually thought."

Edmund looked at him for a moment.

"Yes," he said. "I suppose it was."

Ned looked back at the book with the expression Edmund had come to think of as his arriving-at-something face. Then he read on, and Edmund listened, and the house held them both in the warmth of the August night.

---

*Draft notes: The scene has two movements. The first is the ending of The Little Prince — quiet, the little prince going home and whether that's the same as dying left open. Ned's observation that he misses his rose even though she's difficult is characteristic: precise, unsentimental, exact. Edmund's refusal to show the new book that night — let this one settle — is right for both of them. The second movement is the introduction of Tolkien two nights later. Ned finding the map immediately is inevitable for a boy who makes maps. Edmund's promise that three volumes will take them well into the school term — and Ned's brief involuntary response to that, something quieter than relief — is the scene's emotional centre without needing to be stated. The conversation about whether Tolkien actually knew is the scene's most important exchange: Ned, a few weeks after his first visits to the stone, asking whether the sense that the landscape carries things the people don't understand is real. Edmund's answer: the shape of it, yes. And then Ned recognising it — that's what you were doing. At the stone. The Lord of the Rings becoming one more thread in the web of understanding they are slowly weaving together.*
