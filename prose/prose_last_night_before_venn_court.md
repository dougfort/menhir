# Prose Draft — The Last Night (Before Venn Court)

---

Her name was Aunt Marian, though she was not really an aunt.

She had explained the relationship once, carefully, using words like *second cousin* and *once removed* that meant nothing to Ned except that they were both Kelways somewhere back along a line that had gone thin with distance. She was in her seventies, small and precise, with the particular quality of elderly people who have kept a careful house for so long that the house has become a version of themselves.

She had been kind. Ned wanted to be clear about this, in the part of himself where he kept things straight. She had been kind. She had made his meals and asked about his day and bought him a new pencil case when she noticed his old one was broken. She had been kind, and then something had changed in her — a new quality of watchfulness, a careful distance, as if she was not quite sure where he was in a room even when she could see him — and then this morning she had sat him down and explained, in the language adults use when they are telling you something is over, that a new arrangement had been made.

He was going to stay with someone called Edmund Kelway.

"He lives in Somerset," Aunt Marian said. "In the country. I think you'll find it very pleasant."

She did not say: *I am sorry.* She did not say: *I don't know why this doesn't work.* She said he would find it very pleasant, and offered him a biscuit, and Ned took it because refusing would have made things awkward for both of them.

---

Now it was evening and he was packing.

He was good at this. He knew which things went in the backpack — the notebooks, the compass, the book he was currently reading, the pencils, the good socks — and which went in the bag. He had a system. Everything had its place, which meant that in the moving between places there was at least one constant, the arrangement of his own things, the specific weight of the backpack on his lap.

He worked methodically, the way he did most things, not rushing.

The room had been his for eleven weeks. He had not put anything on the walls — he never did — but he knew the room, the way the light came in the mornings, the particular creak of the third floorboard from the door, the smell of the house that was old wood and lavender and something underneath both that was just the smell of the house itself, accumulated over decades. He would not miss it exactly. That was not quite the word. But he noted it — filed it in the part of himself that kept a record of all the rooms.

The photograph of his mother he folded carefully and put inside the front cover of the smallest notebook, where it always went. It was a small photograph, slightly blurred, taken by someone who had not quite got the focus right. She was laughing at something outside the frame.

He closed the notebook.

---

He became aware, then, that something had changed in the room.

It was not a sound. Not a movement. More like a shift in the quality of the air — a pressure, barely perceptible, the feeling of something that had been held very carefully beginning to be less carefully held. He had felt this before. He did not have a name for it. It was connected, he knew without knowing how he knew, to the particular feeling of this moment — the packed bag, the folded photograph, the room that was already becoming somewhere he used to be.

On the windowsill, Aunt Marian's small china dish — the one she had put there for him to keep his things in, keys and coins and the small things of a day — slid six inches to the left.

Ned looked at it.

It sat there, six inches to the left, as if it had always been there.

He crossed the room and moved it back to where it had been. He did this carefully, with both hands, setting it down precisely. Then he stood there for a moment with his hands at his sides.

This happened sometimes. He had never been able to explain it and had learned not to try. What he knew — what he had worked out across several houses, carefully and without telling anyone — was that it happened more when he felt like this. When the leaving feeling was strong. He didn't know what *this* was, or where it came from, or whether it was connected to him or simply coincided with him. He only knew the feeling that preceded it, and the feeling afterward: a slight settling, as if something that had been building had quietly released.

He felt guilty about it anyway. He always felt guilty about it.

He picked up the compass from the bed where he had set it while packing and held it in his palm. The needle swung, settled. North. He looked at it for a while — the scratch across the face, the slight tarnish of the brass, the needle that always knew where it was going regardless of where he happened to be.

He put it in his jacket pocket.

He picked up his backpack and put it on, and then his bag. He looked around the room once more. The windowsill with the china dish in its right place. The cleared bedside table. The window and the garden below it — the gate with its broken latch that Aunt Marian had been meaning to fix.

He had drawn that gate in his notebook three weeks ago, before he knew it was there. He didn't know why. He drew things sometimes that turned out to be real, and he had learned not to examine this too closely, the way you learn not to press on a bruise.

He turned off the light and went out and pulled the door closed behind him.

---

On the landing he passed the framed photograph that Aunt Marian kept on the wall — her late husband, a formal portrait, facing left. It had been facing left for as long as Ned had been here. He had noticed this the way he noticed most things, filed it away without purpose.

It was facing right.

He looked at it for a moment. Then he went downstairs.

Aunt Marian was in the kitchen, making tea she had not asked if he wanted. She looked up when he came in and her face did the thing it had been doing for two weeks now — a quick, involuntary search of the room, finding him, settling into something that was not quite relief but was adjacent to it.

"All packed?" she said.

"Yes," said Ned.

She put a mug in front of him and sat down across the table and they drank their tea in the particular silence of two people who are being careful with each other, for reasons neither of them could have named precisely.

"I hope you'll be happy there," Aunt Marian said, eventually. "In Somerset."

"Thank you," Ned said.

He meant it. He was good at meaning things he said, even when there was nothing else to say.

Later, after he had gone to bed, Aunt Marian went upstairs to check that the landing light was off. She passed the photograph of her husband and stopped. She looked at it for a long moment. Then she straightened it — left-facing, as it had always been — and went to bed herself.

She did not sleep well. She thought about the boy in the spare room, his careful quietness, the way he took up so little space. She thought about the notebook she had looked in, the map with her house in it, the gate latch. She thought about the cup that had moved from the table to the windowsill on a Tuesday afternoon in May, when she had been certain — as certain as she had ever been of anything — that she had not touched it.

She did not put these things together into a sentence. She kept them separate, the way you keep separate the parts of a problem you don't want to solve.

In the morning there would be a car. The boy would go to Somerset. The house would go back to being itself.

She hoped he would be happy there. She genuinely hoped this. Whatever he was — whatever it was that he carried, that she couldn't name — she hoped he would find somewhere it was all right to carry it.

---

*Draft notes: The scene is Ned's, but Aunt Marian has her own quiet presence throughout — she is not a villain, she is a frightened and decent woman at the limit of what she can manage. The china dish is the incident — small, undeniable to Ned, completely deniable to anyone else. Ned moving it back is the detail that matters: he does this automatically, knowing it needs to be right. The photograph of the husband is the second incident — witnessed by nobody, noticed by both of them, never discussed. The compass at the centre of the scene is crucial: north, reliable, the fixed point. Ned doesn't yet know there is a place where it won't be. Aunt Marian's final thoughts — she hoped he would find somewhere it was all right to carry it — contain, without knowing it, exactly what Venn Court will turn out to be.*
