# Prose Draft — A Day in Late July (The Ceremony)

---

The morning began the way most mornings at Venn Court began.

Edmund was up before Ned, as always, and had drunk his first cup of tea standing at the kitchen window by the time Ned appeared at half past seven with his hair doing something independent and the smaller of his notebooks under his arm. He sat down at the table without appearing to notice that he had sat down, opened the notebook to a page of closely worked pencil drawings, and looked at them with the critical attention of someone returning to a problem.

Edmund made more tea. Put bread under the grill.

"There's a letter for you," he said. "On the table."

Ned looked at the envelope. It was addressed in an elderly hand — careful, slightly formal. He opened it, read it, and put it in the notebook without comment.

Edmund did not ask. He turned the toast.

"It's from Aunt Marian," Ned said, after a moment. "She says she hopes I'm well."

"That's good of her."

"Yes." A pause, in which Ned appeared to be deciding something. "She sends a card sometimes. To places I've been."

Edmund put a plate of toast in front of him and sat down with his own. "Then I shall look forward to getting one," he said, "in due course."

Ned looked at him. Then he picked up his toast.

---

Edmund worked well that morning. The chapter he had been worrying at for a week had a kind of looseness to it now — the sentences finding their way forward without his having to force them. He was writing about a family who found stones beneath their garden, and the particular quality of dread that followed. He wrote about it with professional competence and mild personal scepticism, as he always did.

At eleven he stopped and made tea, which he took back to his desk and drank while reading back what he had written. Outside the bay window the orchard was still and bright. Somewhere beyond the orchard — not visible from here, but present in the way things are present when you know they are there — was the meadow, and the mound, and the stone.

He thought about Ned's face at the stone three days ago. The way he had said *I think you're right* — not as agreement but as recognition.

Edmund set down his mug and went to the shelves.

*Ornaments in Jade* was where it always was — a slim volume between two denser neighbours, its spine faded to the colour of old grass. He took it down and turned to *The Ceremony*. Read it through once, quickly, to remind himself of the shape of it. Stood for a moment looking at the last page.

Then he carried it downstairs.

---

Ned was not in the kitchen. His backpack was on the hook by the door — he had not gone to Somerton. Edmund went to the kitchen garden and found him there, at the far end of the second bed, kneeling in the dirt with a look of focused concentration directed at a row of something that appeared to require his close attention.

"Carrots," Ned said, without looking up. "I'm doing the ones at this end. The spacing was wrong."

"Mm," said Edmund, who had been neglecting the carrots. He watched Ned work for a moment — the careful, methodical movement, thinning and firming by turns. He was good at this. He had become good at this without Edmund particularly noticing when it happened.

Edmund held out the book.

"This is a Machen story," he said. "About a standing stone. Short — you could read it in an hour. You might find it interesting."

Ned took the book, looked at the cover, and set it on the low wall beside his trug. "Thank you," he said, and went back to the carrots.

Edmund went back to his manuscript.

---

At lunch — bread and cheese and tomatoes from the garden, eaten outside in the yard because the day was fine — Ned had the book beside him but had not yet opened it. He ate with his usual focused efficiency and then sat for a moment with his apple juice, looking at the woodshed wall.

"Do you know," he said, apparently to the wall, "what Vilmorin says about storing carrots?"

"I do not," said Edmund.

"Sand," said Ned. He had the air of someone delivering a verdict. "Layers of sand. You pack them in with dry sand and they last through winter. We don't have a root cellar, though."

"There is something that might serve in the stable block. I'll have a look."

Ned nodded. He picked up *The Ceremony* and turned it over and read the back. Then he opened it to the first page.

Edmund collected the plates and left him to it.

---

He heard Ned come in from the garden late in the afternoon, heard the particular sound of the back door and then footsteps that paused in the corridor before continuing. A few minutes later the sound of water running in the bathroom above — Ned washing the dirt off his hands, which he did as a matter of course before returning to the house proper, one of several small habits he had arrived with that Edmund had noticed without commenting on.

Then Ned appeared in the library doorway.

Edmund looked up.

"I've finished it," Ned said.

"And?"

Ned came in and sat down in the chair that had become his chair in the library — not deliberately assigned, simply the one he always chose, the old green one beside the cold fireplace. He held *The Ceremony* in his lap and looked at it for a moment.

"She was afraid of it," he said.

"Yes."

"From when she was a little girl. She couldn't even hear its name." He turned the book over. "I wasn't afraid of it."

Edmund put his pen down. "No," he said.

A pause. Ned appeared to be assembling something.

"It felt old," he said. "Not frightening. Just — very old. Like it had been waiting. Not for anything in particular. Just — patient." He stopped. "That's not quite right either but it's the closest I can get."

Edmund was quiet for a moment. Outside the window the orchard light was shifting toward evening. He thought about thirty years of visiting the stone and never having quite the right words for it, and this boy who had been there once and was arriving at something closer than most of what he had read.

"That is closer to what I think," he said carefully, "than anything I've read on the subject. Including things I've written myself."

Ned looked at him. "Is there more? Other things to read?"

"There's rather a lot more," Edmund said. "The tradition is extensive. Most of it gets the tone wrong, in my view. But it's worth knowing what it says."

"Why — if it's wrong?"

Edmund considered this. "Because knowing what the tradition says helps you understand what it doesn't say. Where the gaps are. Sometimes the gaps are more interesting than the content."

Ned thought about this with the particular stillness he brought to things worth thinking about. "Like the meaning being gone," he said. "But something underneath the meaning still being there."

"Exactly like that."

Another pause. Edmund watched Ned's face, which was doing something careful and interior. He had learned not to press at these moments. The boy arrived at things in his own time, by his own route.

"Could I read something else?" Ned asked. "Something you think gets closer?"

Edmund got up from his desk. He was at the shelves for a moment, considering. Then he took down the Blackwood — *The Wolves of God*, where *The Tarn of Sacrifice* lived — and held it out.

"This one," he said. "Start at page sixty-seven. It's longer than the Machen. And it's stranger. But I think you'll find it — more recognisable."

Ned took the book. He looked at the spine and then at Edmund.

"Did you write this one?"

"No. A man called Algernon Blackwood. He died before I was born." Edmund paused. "He understood that some things in the landscape are very old and not necessarily frightening. He's one of the few who did."

Ned nodded. He put both books under his arm — the Machen to return, the Blackwood to read — and stood up. "Thank you," he said, which he said often and meant every time.

"There's lamb for dinner," Edmund said. "It's been in since two."

Ned stopped at the door. He had not said anything about the stone, or about what he had felt there, beyond the small things he'd offered. He would not say more tonight — Edmund could tell this, the same way he could tell when a sentence was not ready to be written. But he had said something. He had said *patient* and *waiting* and *not frightening*. He had asked for more to read.

For now that was sufficient. Rather more than sufficient, actually.

"I could smell it from the garden," Ned said, about the lamb. "From about four o'clock."

Edmund turned back to his desk. "Good," he said. "That's when it starts being right."

*Draft notes: This chapter contains Conversations Two and Three from the sequence document, embedded in the texture of an ordinary day. The chapter begins with Aunt Marian's letter — a small thread connecting Ned to his past, handled without drama, Edmund not asking and Ned offering anyway. This is new: Ned volunteering something personal. A small marker of development. The carrot and sand conversation is the mundane that gives the chapter its grounding — Vilmorin-Andrieux making an appearance, Ned's competence in the garden evident. Edmund taking down The Ceremony is a considered act framed as casual. Ned's response to it — patient, waiting, not frightening — is his own language for what Edmund has spent thirty years trying to say. Edmund's recognition of this is the scene's emotional centre: "closer than anything I've read, including things I've written myself." The Blackwood is introduced without too much weight on it. And the chapter closes where it began — in the ordinary, the lamb, the kitchen — the deeper thing present but not dwelt upon. Ned saying he could smell the lamb from four o'clock is both mundane and a sign of his increasing ease: a small spontaneous observation, offered without being asked.*
