# Prose Draft — The Christmas Concert (December)

---

The letter had come home in Ned's book bag in the first week of December.

*Dear Parent/Carer — Year 5 Christmas Concert — Tuesday 16th December, 6:30pm — please arrive by 6:15 — children to be dropped at the main entrance by 6:00 — the concert will last approximately one hour — refreshments available afterward in the hall.*

Edmund had read it on the kitchen table that evening with the focused attention he brought to all school communications, as if it were a document of some importance, which in its way it was. He had written *concert — Tues 16 Dec — 6:15* in his desk diary in his careful hand and said nothing more about it.

Ned, watching this from across the table, had said: "You don't have to come."

"I know," Edmund said. He turned to the next item in the book bag, which was a spelling list.

Ned looked at him for a moment. Then went back to his homework.

---

They arrived at quarter past six. Edmund had driven, which meant they were four minutes early by Edmund's watch and therefore, given the watch's current relationship with accurate time, probably closer to seven minutes early, which Edmund had accounted for. The car park was already busy — other parents, other cars, a general organised chaos of people who did this every year and knew how it worked. Edmund did not know how it worked. He followed the signs.

The school at night was different from the school in daylight. The windows lit from inside, the entrance propped open, the sounds of children and music leaking out into the December air. Ned went ahead to the entrance where the children were being collected — a teacher at the door with a clipboard, names ticked off. Ned turned back once, briefly, and Edmund raised a hand, and Ned went in.

Edmund found the hall.

---

Folding chairs in rows. Strip lighting. Children's artwork on the walls — handmade Christmas decorations, painted cards, a large collaborative piece involving a great deal of tinsel that Edmund studied for a moment with genuine attention while he waited for a seat.

He chose a chair somewhere in the middle of the third row, which seemed neither conspicuously forward nor pointedly distant. Parents settled around him — couples mainly, one or two grandparents, a man in a work suit who had come straight from somewhere, a woman with a toddler on her lap who was making intermittent observations about the chairs in a loud whisper. Edmund folded his coat over his knees and sat with it and looked at the small stage at the far end of the hall, which was empty except for a keyboard, a music stand, and a large cardboard star that someone had assembled with commendable ambition.

He was aware, in a mild way, that this was the first time he had sat in a primary school hall since he was a child himself. He had not thought about this before tonight. He thought about it now, briefly, and then set it aside.

The teacher with the clipboard — who turned out to be Mr Ashby — was making his way along the far wall, checking things. Edmund had not yet met Mr Ashby in person, only on the phone and by email. He was younger than Edmund had pictured, and had the air of a man managing a large number of variables with reasonable competence.

A hand appeared on Edmund's shoulder.

"You came," Rosa said, from the row behind him.

Edmund turned. Rosa was there with her mother — a small, neat woman who bore almost no resemblance to her daughter — and Martin Ferris, who occupied his folding chair with the equanimity of someone who had attended many of these and would attend many more.

"Good evening," Edmund said.

"We're in Year 5 too," Rosa said, with the air of someone who considered this a satisfactory arrangement. "So we'll see each other's class. Did you know they're doing a thing with recorders? There are a lot of recorders. It's fine," she added, charitably. "Most of them are quite good."

Rosa's mother introduced herself — Elena Ferris, she said, with a warmth that suggested Rosa had mentioned Edmund. Martin nodded across with the compact acknowledgement of a man who had stood in a dark field with Edmund watching starlings and felt no need to repeat the formal introductions.

"He's nervous," Rosa said, nodding toward the stage, meaning Ned.

Edmund looked at her. "Did he say so?"

"No." She considered. "He went very still on the way in. He does that when he's nervous. Didn't you know?" She said this without criticism, as information.

"I did know," Edmund said. "I wasn't sure you did."

Rosa looked briefly surprised, and then pleased, as if a good thing had been confirmed. She settled back in her chair as the lights dimmed.

---

The younger classes came first.

Year 3 sang three carols with the sincerity of children who had been practising for six weeks and knew every word. Year 4 performed a short nativity with blocking that had clearly been ambitious and was now somewhat compressed due to the stage being smaller than anticipated. The child playing a shepherd had lost his tea-towel headdress and was conducting himself with great dignity in its absence.

Edmund watched all of this with the quality of attention he brought to things he had not encountered before. He found he was not bored. He was not entirely sure what he had expected to feel, but it was not this — something that was not quite sentiment and not quite amusement but sat somewhere between them, warm and particular.

Then Year 5.

---

They filed onto the stage in two rows. Edmund found Ned immediately — he was in the second row, toward the left, standing with the particular stillness that was his usual quality in large spaces. He was wearing his school uniform, which Edmund had seen every day for months, but in the stage lighting it looked slightly different. Slightly more formal. Ned in his place.

For a moment — just a moment — Edmund saw the boy who had arrived at Castle Cary station in July with his backpack held against his chest and his expression careful and giving nothing away. The same boy. A different version of him.

Ned was scanning the audience.

Edmund was in the third row, fourth chair from the left. He was perhaps not easy to find in strip lighting with forty or fifty adults in the way. But Ned found him. Edmund saw the exact moment — the slight shift of Ned's gaze, the stillness deepening for a half-second in a different way, and then releasing. Not a smile. Not a wave. Just the private acknowledgement of finding what you were looking for where you expected it to be.

Edmund nodded once. Ned looked back at the music stand.

In the row behind, Rosa, who had been watching this exchange with the focused attention she brought to interesting things, said nothing. For once.

---

Year 5 sang three carols and performed a short original piece about the winter solstice that someone's parent had written and which was, Edmund thought, genuinely rather good. They had a small recorder ensemble, as Rosa had predicted — seven children, the playing variable in quality but united in commitment. The child next to Rosa played with her eyes closed, which Edmund found, for reasons he couldn't entirely account for, affecting.

Rosa herself sang with complete conviction and a volume that was perhaps marginally in excess of what the acoustics required. She was very good.

Ned sang. He had a clear, even voice — not a soloist's voice, but true, and he knew all the words without looking at the sheet. Edmund had not known Ned could sing. He filed this away with all the other things he was still discovering.

The concert ended. Applause, shifting chairs, the lights coming up. Children streaming off the stage to find their people in the crowd.

Edmund stood and waited.

He was aware of parents reuniting around him — the particular warmth of it, the children finding their parents and being briefly very young again, the hug that happened before the composed face reasserted itself. He watched this with the interest of someone observing a practice he was new to.

And then Ned, appearing through the crowd with his coat half on, finding Edmund and stopping a few feet away with the expression of someone who has arrived somewhere and is reassessing what to say.

"You came," he said. Which was what Rosa had said, and was probably what Ned had been thinking since he found Edmund in the third row.

"I told you I would," Edmund said. He had, in fact, not told Ned any such thing — he had simply put it in his diary and said nothing more about it, which for Edmund was the same thing. "You sang well."

Ned looked at him. "You can hear individual voices?"

"Not specifically. But the piece was good." He paused. "Also Rosa was audible from a considerable distance, so I could tell where Year 5 was positioned."

Something happened in Ned's face — the internal version of a laugh, the thing he did when he found something funny and didn't quite produce the outward version. "She said she wasn't going to be loud this year," he said.

"A commendable ambition."

Rosa arrived at speed with her parents immediately behind her. "You were brilliant," she said to Ned, without preamble. "The bit in the second carol where you all went quiet — that was my idea in rehearsal. Mr Ashby didn't want to do it and then he did it and it was brilliant." She turned to Edmund. "What did you think?"

"I thought the winter solstice piece was unexpectedly good," Edmund said. "And the dynamics in the second carol were well handled."

Rosa looked at him for a moment with the expression of someone who has been paid a compliment by someone whose opinion she has been wanting. "Yeah," she said. "They were."

Martin and Edmund exchanged the brief look of people who have watched the same small hurricane and find it, privately, admirable.

---

Refreshments in the hall afterward — instant coffee, orange squash, mince pies from a tin. Edmund stood with a mince pie he had accepted on the grounds that declining would have required an explanation, and talked briefly with Mr Ashby, who had found his way over with the air of a man who had something he wanted to say.

"Mr Kelway. I'm glad you could come."

"Thank you for the letter."

Mr Ashby looked at him for a moment. "Ned's been doing well this term," he said. "I wanted to say that in person as well as at parents' evening. He's — " He paused, finding his words carefully. "He's a particular kind of student. The sort you get once every few years. The reading diary is," he paused again, "notable."

"I thought it might attract some comment," Edmund said.

"Algernon Blackwood," Mr Ashby said. He said it with the air of someone producing evidence. "In Year 5."

"He found it interesting."

"I imagine he did." Mr Ashby looked across the room to where Ned was standing with Rosa, who appeared to be explaining something at some length. "He's settled well. Properly settled. I wasn't certain in September — new school, unusual circumstances — but he's found his feet." He looked back at Edmund. "He's a happy child."

Edmund received this without visibly doing anything to it. "Good," he said. "I'm glad to hear it."

He ate the mince pie. It was from a tin and not especially good and he ate it anyway.

---

The drive home.

The December dark, the lanes, the particular quality of the car after an event — warm and enclosed, the evening settling. Ned in the passenger seat with his coat unzipped, slightly untethered from the concert, not quite back to his ordinary self yet.

After a while he said: "Mr Ashby talked to you."

"Briefly, yes."

"What did he say?"

Edmund indicated for the Venn Court lane. "That you're a particular kind of student. That you've settled well. That you're a happy child." He said the last three words carefully, without inflection.

Ned was quiet for a moment.

"Is that what he said exactly?" he asked. "A happy child?"

"His exact words."

The lane narrowed. The hedges pressed in on either side, the headlights picking out the frost beginning on the leaves. The house appeared at the end of the track with the kitchen light on, which Edmund had left on because they would be coming home in the dark.

"Hm," said Ned. He looked out the window at the frost.

He did not say anything else about it. But he sat with it the way he sat with things — steadily, without hurry. Taking it in.

Edmund parked in the stable block and they went inside and he made tea and Ned went upstairs to change out of his school uniform, and when he came back down they sat at the kitchen table in their usual way and after a while Edmund went to get the Lord of the Rings.

They had reached the chapter called *Many Meetings* — Frodo waking in Rivendell, finding himself surrounded by people who know what he has been through and do not require him to explain it. Edmund read it and Ned listened and neither of them said anything about the concert or Mr Ashby or what a happy child meant from the inside. They simply went on with the story, which was what they did.

---

*Draft notes: Four movements. The arrival — Edmund navigating the unfamiliar territory of a primary school Christmas concert with his usual equanimity, finding his seat, Rosa appearing behind him and establishing that she has been watching Ned closely enough to know when he is nervous. The concert itself — Edmund's mild surprise at finding himself not bored, Year 5 filing on, Ned scanning the audience and finding Edmund, the private acknowledgement between them. Rosa loud and excellent. Edmund discovering Ned can sing. The afterward — Ned finding Edmund in the crowd, *you came*, the exchange with Mr Ashby: *a happy child*. The drive home — Ned sitting with those words, *is that what he said exactly*, taking them in without excess. The Tolkien chapter: Frodo in Rivendell, surrounded by people who know what he has been through and do not require him to explain it. Neither of them noting the parallel. They simply go on.*

*The scene's structural centre is the moment Ned finds Edmund in the third row. Not a smile, not a wave — the private acknowledgement of finding what you were looking for where you expected it to be. This is the thing that has never happened before at a school event for Ned. Edmund was there. Edmund was where he said he would be, except that he didn't say he would be there — he simply was.*

*Rosa, watching this exchange and saying nothing for once, is perfect.*
