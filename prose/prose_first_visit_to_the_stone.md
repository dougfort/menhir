# Prose Draft — The First Visit to the Stone (Revised)

---

It had been about two weeks.

Ned had seen Edmund go to the meadow several times — past the orchard, over the ha-ha, across the long grass — and come back an hour later looking the way he looked after he had finished a good morning's writing: quieter than usual, which was quiet, and somehow more present. Ned had noticed this without asking about it, which was his way.

He had also heard what the local children said about the stone.

Some of it was obviously nonsense — the midnight rituals, the treasure, the man who talked to the dead. Will, who was eleven and considered himself an authority, said his gran had told him not to go near it, which was the most interesting thing anyone had said because Will's gran did not seem like a woman who frightened easily.

Ned had filed all of this in the part of himself he kept for things that mattered. He had not gone to the meadow alone. He had not quite been ready.

One morning at the kitchen table, Edmund was putting his jacket on — the particular jacket, the boots — and Ned looked up from his book.

"Where do you go?" he asked.

Edmund looked at him for a moment. "The stone," he said. "Would you like to see it?"

"Yes," said Ned.

---

The orchard was full of bees. Late July and the grass beneath the trees was thick and unhurried, the light filtering down through leaves just beginning to think about August. Ned walked beside Edmund through the familiar gnarled trees and out the other side, and there was the meadow ahead, and beyond the meadow —

He had seen it before, from the kitchen garden, from his bedroom window at night — a dark shape at the end of the world. But this was different. This was walking toward it.

The stone appeared above the long grass as they crossed the meadow — just the top at first, dark grey against the sky, then more of it as the ground rose. Ned understood then why he had underestimated it from a distance. It was bigger than it looked from anywhere that wasn't here.

The grass thinned as they approached. Ned noticed this — the tall meadow plants gradually surrendering to something shorter, sparser, the ground itself changing quality underfoot, firmer and less yielding. He looked down at it and then up at the stone, and the stone was simply there, enormous and unhurried, leaning its small lean, covered in lichen the colour of old rust and ash.

He stopped a few feet away and looked at it.

He had been to museums. He had seen Roman coins, fossil ammonites, the cast of a dinosaur skeleton. He had understood that these things were old. But this was different from understanding it. The stone was old in a way you could feel — not as information but as a physical fact, like heat or weight.

He reached out and put his hand on it.

Warm. He had not expected that. Warm and dry, while the meadow around him was still damp from the night's dew. He pressed his palm flat against the surface and felt the slight roughness of it, the lichen dry and granular under his fingers, and the warmth coming through steady and even, from somewhere inside the stone, from somewhere it had been keeping for a very long time.

He stood like that for a moment.

It occurred to him, standing there, that nothing was wrong. This was the strangest place he had ever been — a stone that had been here before human memory, warm as a living thing, in a field where the grass didn't grow properly and the compass didn't work — and nothing was wrong. He was not waiting for something to go wrong. He was not holding himself carefully in case he caused something. He was simply standing here with his hand on an ancient stone in the morning sun, and the feeling was not familiar at all. Not at all.

He would have to think about that.

Then he became aware that his compass was in his pocket and his hand was already going to it before he had quite decided to.

He looked at the needle.

It was not pointing north.

Not dramatically wrong — not spinning or shaking. Just pointing somewhere that was not north, quietly and with apparent conviction, as if it had decided that north was a matter of opinion in this particular place. He turned slowly and the needle turned with him, tracking something other than magnetic north, something the compass had apparently decided was more important.

He stared at it for a long moment.

He thought of Edmund, sitting on his boulder a few feet away, and had the unfamiliar and not quite manageable feeling that this was something he might not need to put immediately in his pocket. That this was perhaps, for the first time, someone he could show it to. That Edmund would look at it the way he looked at most things — with attention, and without alarm.

He was not ready for that yet. But he noted the thought and put it aside for later, the way you put aside a word you don't know how to use yet.

He closed his fist around the compass and put it back in his pocket.

Edmund was sitting on one of the low boulders — the left one, as you faced the stone — in the manner of someone who had been sitting there for years, which he had. Ned went to the other boulder and sat down.

Neither of them said anything for a while.

---

"The children in town have stories about this place," Ned said eventually.

"I imagine they do," said Edmund.

"Midnight rituals. Treasure buried under it." He paused. "Will Haddon's gran told him not to come near it."

"Margaret Haddon has lived in Somerton for seventy years," Edmund said mildly. "Her grandmother probably told her the same thing. And her grandmother's grandmother told her. The instruction has been passed down rather carefully for a number of generations, without anyone quite remembering where it started."

Ned looked at the stone. "What do you think it is?"

Edmund was quiet for long enough that Ned thought he might not answer. Then he said: "May I tell you what I actually think? Rather than what I would say to most people who asked."

Ned looked at him. "Yes."

Edmund looked at the stone. He turned his hat in his hands — he had brought a hat, against the sun — and Ned had the sense of a man locating something that had been sitting in him for a long time without occasion to come out.

---

"The books I write," Edmund said, "are about places like this. Ancient things in the landscape that have — persistence. That leave marks. My readers expect a particular kind of story from me, and I provide it. Dread. The sinister residue of old practice. Stones like this one are usually, in the tradition I write in, sources of malevolence. Dark purpose that has outlasted its makers."

He paused.

"I don't believe that. Or rather — I believe that something persists here, but I don't believe it's malevolent. I've been coming to this stone since I was not much older than you are, and what I feel here has never been anything like dread. What I feel is something much harder to describe."

Ned waited.

"The stone was put here by people," Edmund said. "Human beings, with hands and intentions. Thousands of years ago — so long ago that everything they were is gone. Their language. Their beliefs. What they called themselves. What they called this place, and why. All of it completely and irrecoverably gone." He turned his hat in his hands. "And yet they were here. They chose this particular rise in this particular meadow. They moved this stone — which weighs, I should think, something in the region of fifteen tons — and they stood it upright here, and they came back, probably for generations, with whatever they brought and whatever they meant by it."

He stopped.

"What they meant," he said, more quietly, "has gone. But the stone hasn't. And I think — this is what I think, though I have never said it to anyone — I think that whatever they brought here, whatever quality of concentrated attention or belief or intention, over however many years of use — I think some trace of it remains. Not the meaning. The meaning is gone. But something underneath the meaning. Some quality of the place that was here before they came and that they recognised and made use of, and that is still here."

He looked at Ned.

"That is probably not a satisfying explanation."

"No," Ned agreed. "But I think you're right."

He said this quietly and with the particular quality of certainty that he reserved for things he knew from direct experience rather than from reasoning. Edmund heard the difference, though he would not have been able to say precisely how.

Edmund looked at him for a moment with an expression Ned was becoming familiar with — the one that meant he had been mildly surprised and found the surprise acceptable.

"What made you say that?" Edmund asked.

Ned thought about the compass in his pocket. He thought about the warmth under his palm. He thought about the needle pointing somewhere that was not north, quietly and with conviction. He thought about the thought he had put aside — that this was someone he might eventually show it to.

"I don't know yet," he said. Which was true, and which he thought was probably the right answer for now.

Edmund received this without pressing. He understood, Ned thought, the difference between *I don't know* and *I don't know yet.* Most adults didn't.

---

They sat for a while longer. The meadow was full of insect sound. A buzzard went over very high — Edmund tilted his head and Ned looked up and they watched it together without comment.

The stone stood in its slight lean and said nothing, which was what it always did, and which was, Ned was beginning to understand, exactly what it meant.

When they stood to go Edmund put his hand briefly on Ned's shoulder. A light touch, a little awkward — the gesture of a man who has arrived at something he means and is still learning the physical vocabulary for it. Ned didn't flinch or lean into it. He simply stood there and let it be what it was, which seemed to be enough for both of them.

They walked back through the orchard without talking. The bees were still working the long grass under the trees.

---

Later, alone in his room, Ned took out the compass and looked at it for a long time. The needle had returned to north — proper north, ordinary north, the north it had always known.

He opened his mapping notebook — the one with the world in it, his world, the one he had been building for three years through all the houses — and turned to a blank page.

He drew the stone first. Then the mound it stood on. Then the two boulders. He marked where Edmund sat and where he had sat himself. He marked the direction the compass had pointed, as precisely as he could remember.

Then he sat back and looked at what he had drawn.

He had been making maps of his own world for three years, a world he had invented because the actual world had not, until now, offered him anywhere that felt real enough to map.

He looked at the page for a long time.

Then he turned to a fresh page and wrote, at the top, in careful letters:

*Venn Court.*

He sat with the pen in his hand for a while, not writing anything else. Then he closed the notebook and put it on the shelf — the shelf with Edmund's three books on it, which he had not disturbed — and went down to the kitchen for tea.

*Draft notes: The main revisions are three. First, the passage where Ned notices that nothing is wrong — the strangest place he has ever been, and no familiar dread — names without over-explaining what is different about Venn Court. Second, Ned's thought about showing Edmund the compass: not ready yet, but the thought arrives, is noted, is put aside for later. This plants the seed of Conversation Four from the sequence document. Third, the qualifier on his agreement with Edmund — "I don't know yet" rather than simply "I don't know," and Edmund hearing the difference — establishes that Ned is already, cautiously, thinking of this as knowledge he is approaching rather than something permanently closed. The touch on the shoulder is revised to acknowledge Edmund's awareness of making an unfamiliar gesture — "still learning the physical vocabulary for it" — without overexplaining. Everything else is kept as it was.*
