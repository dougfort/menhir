# Prose Draft — The Stone Conversation (February)

---

It was two days after the adoption.

Not the next morning — Edmund had a sense that some things needed a day to settle before you could speak about them properly, and Ned had a similar sense without having articulated it, and so the day after had been an ordinary day: school, the bicycle, homework at the kitchen table, the Lord of the Rings in the evening. Both of them carrying the Thursday quietly, without mentioning it.

The Saturday morning Edmund came downstairs to find Ned already in the kitchen, in his coat.

"Going to the stone," Ned said.

"I'll come," Edmund said. He hadn't been asked. He didn't wait to be.

---

February was different from January in the way that each month in Somerset has its own particular quality. The frost was less persistent, the light slightly more willing. There were snowdrops at the base of the orchard wall — the first ones, visible if you looked — and the blackthorn at the meadow edge was beginning to think about what it would do in March. Small signs. The year turning.

They went through the orchard in the February morning. Through the ha-ha. Across the meadow. The stone.

Ned put his hand on it without breaking stride — just reaching out as he passed, touching it briefly, the way you might touch the shoulder of someone you are walking with. Not stopping. Going to his boulder. Sitting.

Edmund sat on his.

The stone stood in its slight lean. The meadow was quiet. A buzzard somewhere above them, circling — Edmund could hear it before he saw it, the thin mewing call. Ned looked up and found it immediately, as he always found things.

They sat for a while without speaking. This was easy between them now. It had always been relatively easy — even in July, on the first visit, the silence had been of the right quality. Now it was something else again. The silence of people who have been here enough times to know what the silence is for.

---

Edmund spoke.

"I've been thinking," he said, "about what you said in October. In the car. After the murmurations."

Ned looked at him.

"Each stone responding to the nearest stones," Edmund said. "No one in charge. Something coming from all of them together."

Ned waited.

"I think," Edmund said carefully, "that what I have been doing here for thirty years is — listening to one note. One stone. The particular thing this stone does, this place does. The warmth. The compass. The quality of time. I've been — attending to it." He paused. "I didn't know what I was doing, exactly. I knew it mattered. I didn't know quite what it was."

He looked at the stone.

"And then you arrived. And I began to understand something." He said this in the same tone he used for most things — level, precise, not performing anything. "What I understand is that the stone knows a great deal more than I do. And that you — " He stopped. Chose the words. "I think you understand this place better than I do."

Ned was very still.

"Not because you've been here longer," Edmund said. "Not because you've read the right books or thought the right thoughts. Because of what you are. Because of what you carry. You don't observe the stone — you're in conversation with it. That's different from what I do." Another pause. "I think it's always been different. I think the stone — or this place, this landscape, whatever it is — I think it's been waiting for someone who could do that. And here you are."

The words again. *Here you are.* Which Edmund said for the second time and meant in the same precise way — not as sentiment, but as the bare fact of a presence that had been, perhaps, anticipated.

The stone stood in the February morning and received all of this without comment, as it always did.

---

Ned looked at the stone for a while. Not at Edmund. He was doing his arriving-at-something, the quality of attention that came before he said a precise thing.

"I think," he said, "that it wasn't waiting for me specifically." He paused. "I think it's been in conversation the whole time. With the landscape. With the other stones. With everyone who came here — your father, his father, all the way back." He looked at the mound they were sitting on, the slight rise that might have been, Edmund had always thought, the remnant of something older. "I'm just the first person who — noticed it was talking."

Edmund looked at him.

"And you noticed too," Ned said. "You've been noticing for thirty years. You just didn't know what you were noticing." He glanced at Edmund briefly. "That's not nothing."

Edmund was quiet for a moment.

"No," he said. "I suppose it isn't."

A silence. The buzzard had gone. The meadow settled back into its particular February quiet. Snowdrops at the orchard wall, the blackthorn thinking about spring, the stone warm and dry in its field as it had always been.

Ned stood. He looked at the stone for a moment — the way he always looked at it before leaving, a brief full attention, as if completing a circuit.

Then he stepped sideways, once, so that his shoulder was against Edmund's shoulder. A small contact. A nudge, barely — the kind of thing that is barely anything and is also everything. He stood there for a moment, their shoulders touching, both of them looking at the stone.

Edmund did not move. He did not make anything of it. He simply received it, the way he received all the important things — steadily, without flinching, letting it be what it was.

Then Ned stepped away and they walked back through the orchard.

---

The orchard in February. The snowdrops at the wall. The bare branches of the apple trees making their precise shapes. Their footsteps in the wet grass, side by side, the same pace now — when had that happened, Edmund thought, the boy who arrived in July with his backpack on his front, and now this — and the house ahead with the kitchen window lit from inside and the range going and the day ready to continue.

They went in.

Edmund put the kettle on.

Ned sat at the kitchen table and after a moment opened the field notebook — the birthday one, hardcover, waterproof, for what actually happens. He turned to a blank page. Picked up his pencil.

He didn't write anything for a moment. Just sat with the blank page and the pencil, the way you sit before you begin something.

Edmund made the tea and didn't ask.
