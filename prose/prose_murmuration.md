# Prose Draft — The Murmuration (Somerset Levels, Late October)

---

Rosa's father was called Martin. He was a broad, unhurried man with the particular quality of stillness that comes from spending a lot of time outdoors waiting for things — a farmer's stillness, or a birdwatcher's, the ability to be in a place without needing it to produce anything immediately. He drove a Land Rover that had seen considerable use and shook hands with Edmund in the car park at Shapwick Heath with the directness of someone who had nothing to prove.

"Martin Ferris," he said.

"Edmund Kelway."

That was more or less the extent of the introduction. Both of them seemed satisfied with it.

Rosa had been out of the Land Rover before it fully stopped. She was wearing a large red waterproof jacket that made her look like a very energetic fire hazard and carrying binoculars that were slightly too big for her face. She had already established, via the starling hotline she had called from the car, that the birds had roosted at Shapwick Heath the previous night, which she relayed to Ned before he had his door open.

"Good conditions tonight," she said. "Clear sky, not much wind. My dad says clear sky is better because you can see the shapes."

"Shapes?"

"What the murmuration makes. The things it looks like." She was already walking toward the reserve entrance, binoculars swinging. "Sometimes it looks like a whale. Sometimes like smoke. Once we saw one that looked exactly like a map of somewhere, except nowhere I could identify."

Ned fell in beside her. Edmund and Martin followed at a slight distance, in the natural way of adults accompanying children who have already decided where they are going.

---

The sun was sinking over the reserve on a bright and crisp October evening, the sky tinging with colour and deflecting down onto the watery glass of the rhyne below. The path ran along the South Drain — a flat, dark channel of water, perfectly still, doubling everything: the reed beds, the sky, the four of them walking in procession. The light was going slowly, gold into grey.

The Somerset Levels at this hour: flat, enormous, the sky very large. Edmund, who had lived two miles from this landscape his whole adult life, had never come here at dusk to watch the starlings. He had known about the murmurations in the way you know about things you have not made time for. Walking along the drain now with the light going and the reed beds murmuring in the small wind, he thought about this. About all the evenings at the stone and all the evenings in the library and all the evenings he had not spent here.

Martin came alongside him. "You're at Venn Court," he said.

"Yes. Two or three miles east of Somerton."

"Know it. My grandfather worked the fields there for a time, before Turley's father took them on." He looked out over the Levels. "Good land."

"Jim Turley manages it well."

Martin nodded. They walked for a moment in the comfortable silence of people who both know a piece of ground and don't need to explain it to each other.

"The stone on your land," Martin said.

Edmund looked at him.

"My grandfather mentioned it. Said there were rules about it." Martin said this without any particular inflection, the way he might say the ground was wet or the light was going. "That you didn't touch it."

"No one does," Edmund said. "It has been here rather longer than the rules."

Martin considered this and apparently found it reasonable. "Rosa knows about it," he said. "She'll ask you about it at some point."

"I rather expect she will."

"She asks about everything." Martin said it with the equanimity of a man who had made his peace with this some years ago. "She'll mean no harm by it."

"I'll look forward to it," Edmund said, which he found, on examination, was true.

---

Ahead of them Rosa had stopped and was pointing. Not at anything yet — at the horizon, at the reed beds, at the general direction of expected starlings. She was explaining something to Ned at some volume about the approach vectors, which was not a phrase she had used in the lunchtime conversation but which she appeared to have acquired since.

Ned was listening with his full attention, occasionally asking something that made Rosa stop and think before answering.

Martin watched them for a moment. "Yours?" he said, not in an unfriendly way.

"He's a ward of mine," Edmund said. "A young relative."

"He listens," Martin said, meaning it as a compliment.

"He does."

They walked on.

---

They found their spot — a slight widening in the path beside the drain, open sky on three sides, the reed beds close. A few other people were already there: a couple with professional cameras on tripods, an elderly man alone with his binoculars, a family with a child slightly younger than Rosa who was eating a cereal bar and periodically asking when it would start.

The air was cold. Breath was visible. The reed beds rustled in the small wind that came off the water. Edmund opened the bag he had brought and produced a flask, which he had filled with tea before leaving Venn Court, which was the kind of thing Edmund did.

"Tea," he said to Martin.

Martin looked briefly at the flask as if reassessing Edmund upward. "Thank you."

They stood and watched the sky. Rosa moved from foot to foot, which was her version of stillness, binoculars up and down. Ned stood quietly beside Edmund, hands in his pockets. The light continued going. The reed beds grew darker.

"There," said Martin.

---

At first it was nothing — a distant smearing of the horizon, a thickening of the air above the reed beds perhaps a quarter mile away. Then it multiplied. A dark mass appearing and growing and shifting, lifting from the reeds and rising and becoming something other than birds.

It came toward them along the drain.

Rosa made a sound — an involuntary small sound, not a word — and then went completely still. This was new. The girl who was never still, still. Her binoculars came up but she didn't look through them. She looked with her whole face, unmediated.

Martin put his hand on her shoulder without looking at her.

The shape changed continuously — elongating, compressing, dividing into two shapes and rejoining, the whole mass turning at angles that seemed to require coordination but had none. It was above them now, or partly above them, the sound of it reaching them: a low collective rushing, thousands of wingbeats in a frequency below the wind, felt more than heard.

Ned took his compass from his pocket.

He looked at it for a moment, quietly, while the murmuration built overhead. The needle was moving — not the decisive pointing of the stone in the meadow, not the hunting uncertainty of Stanton Drew. Something else. A kind of responsiveness, the needle following the edge of the mass as it moved, as if tracking a field that shifted with it. He watched this for a moment. Then he put the compass away.

Edmund had seen him do this and said nothing.

The murmuration compressed suddenly — darkening, densifying — and a peregrine came through it like a thrown stone, and the whole mass erupted sideways in a wave that moved through it at a speed that should not have been possible, three hundred thousand individual decisions made in the same instant, no one in charge, no one directing, something moving through the flock the way a thought moves through a mind.

The child with the cereal bar said *whoa.*

Rosa, still entirely still, said nothing. Her father's hand stayed on her shoulder.

---

It lasted perhaps twenty minutes from when it began. Then the starlings came down — suddenly, decisively, the whole mass dropping into the reed beds in a great collective descent that sounded like rain. The sky was ordinary again. The other watchers began to move, to talk, to retrieve their equipment. The family with the child went back along the path.

Ned stood for a moment looking at where the murmuration had been.

"The sound," he said. "When the peregrine came through."

"Susurration," Edmund said.

Ned looked at him.

"The technical term. From the Latin — a murmuring, a whisper." Edmund paused. "Rather a good word for it."

Rosa came to stand beside Ned, her binoculars around her neck, her hair doing something entirely independent under her hat. "We saw a bigger one last December," she said, in a tone that made clear this was not a criticism of tonight's murmuration but a statement of fact offered for completeness. "About half a million, my dad reckoned. It went on for forty minutes."

"I'd like to see that," Ned said.

"You can come again. We come most weekends." She said this with the straightforwardness of someone making a practical arrangement. Then she went back to her father, who was packing the flask back into Edmund's bag with the efficiency of a man who had done a lot of this kind of thing.

---

The drive home.

Edmund and Ned in the car, the dark Levels on either side of the road, the flat black water of the rhynes catching starlight. Edmund driving. Ned looking out the window.

After a while Ned said: "The compass."

"Yes."

"It was following the edge of it. The murmuration. Like it was tracking something."

"What do you think it was tracking?"

Ned was quiet for a moment. "I don't know. Whatever it is that makes them — move like that. The thing that isn't any of the birds but comes out of all of them." He looked out the window. "Like a field. An actual field. That moves with them."

Edmund drove. The Levels gave way to low hills, the road lifting slightly. A farmhouse with a light on. An oak tree standing alone in a field, its branches bare now, October finishing.

"Edmund," Ned said.

"Yes."

"Do you think that's what it was like? Before. When there were a lot of stones." He turned from the window. "Like a murmuration. Each stone responding to the nearest stones. No one in charge. But something coming out of all of them together. Something that isn't any of the stones but comes from all of them."

Edmund drove for a moment without speaking.

"I don't know," he said. "But I think that's rather a good way to think about it."

Ned turned back to the window. The darkness of the Levels, flat and enormous, sliding past.

"Rosa's father is like you," he said.

Edmund glanced at him. "In what way."

"He watches things." A pause. "He knew about the stone."

"His grandfather worked the fields near Venn Court. People remember these things without knowing quite why."

Ned nodded. He was quiet for a while, thinking about something. Edmund let him.

When they turned into the Venn Court lane Ned said: "Thank you for coming."

"It was worth coming," Edmund said. Which was entirely true and which was not quite the same as *you're welcome*, which Ned understood and did not require.

The kitchen light was on. Edmund had left it on before they went, because they would be coming back in the dark, which was the kind of thing Edmund thought of.

---

*Draft notes: The scene has four movements — the arrival and introductions, the walk along the drain, the murmuration itself, the drive home. Martin Ferris is established in his first appearance: broad, unhurried, the stillness of someone who waits for things. His mention of the stone — matter-of-fact, his grandfather's rules about it — opens a thread without dramatising it. Edmund's recognition that he has lived two miles from this landscape his whole life and never come to watch the starlings is significant: all those evenings at the stone, and this has been happening here too. The compass during the murmuration: not the stone's decisive pointing but something responsive, tracking the edge of the moving field. Edmund sees Ned do this and says nothing. The peregrine through the murmuration — the wave of response, the thought moving through a mind — is the scene's visual centrepiece. Rosa going completely still is her best moment so far: all that energy stopped by something genuinely extraordinary. The car conversation is the scene's emotional close: Ned's idea — each stone responding to the nearest stones, no one in charge, something coming from all of them together — arriving quietly and changing something in the air between them. And then: Rosa's father is like you. He watches things. The kitchen light left on because they would be coming back in the dark. Edmund thought of it. He always thinks of it.*
