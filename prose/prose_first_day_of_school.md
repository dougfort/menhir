# Prose Draft — The First Day of School

---

The alarm went off at seven fifteen.

Ned had set it himself, on his phone, the night before — three minutes before Edmund's alarm, which went off at seven eighteen regardless of what day it was, which Ned had worked out over the summer. He lay for a moment in the familiar dark of his room listening to the house, and then got up.

The grey trousers were stiff. He had known they would be — they had been stiff the two times he had tried them on — but stiff in the actual morning was different from stiff in the trying-on. He put them on anyway. The polo shirt. The school sweatshirt, navy, with the logo on the chest. The black shoes by the back door, not yet.

He looked at himself in the wardrobe mirror for a moment.

He looked like someone who went to a particular school. Which was the point.

He went downstairs.

---

Edmund was already in the kitchen, which was not surprising. He was making toast and had put Ned's plate out already — the one on the left, nearest the window, which had become Ned's place over the summer without either of them deciding it. Edmund did not say anything particular when Ned came in. He looked at him once, briefly, with the expression that meant he was noting something, and then turned back to the toast.

They ate in the comfortable silence that had established itself between them over eight weeks. Ned ate with his usual focused attention. Edmund drank his tea.

"The lane might be slippery," Edmund said. "There was dew in the night."

"I'll go slowly."

"The gate at the school is on the right side of the building, not the front."

"I know. I went past it when we got the bike."

Edmund nodded. A pause. "Do you need anything?"

Ned thought about it. "No."

"Right." Edmund picked up his mug. "The library will be open until one if you need to come back for anything."

Ned looked at him. Edmund was not the kind of person who said pointless things, which meant this was not a pointless thing. It was, Ned understood, Edmund's version of *good luck* — practical, non-sentimental, meaning something underneath the words that neither of them needed to say.

"I won't need to," Ned said. "But thank you."

He changed into his school shoes at the back door. Put his trainers on the rack beside Edmund's boots, where they had lived since the first week. Put on his helmet and wheeled the green bicycle out of the stable block.

The morning was cool and still. The orchard beyond the kitchen garden held the last of the summer in its leaves, something yellowish beginning at the edges. In six weeks it would be October.

He did not look toward the meadow. He had learned, over the summer, to know the stone was there without having to look.

He got on the bicycle and rode down the lane.

---

Edmund stood at the kitchen window and watched him go.

The boy was small in the uniform. Smaller than in his own clothes — the grey trousers and the navy sweatshirt making him look, somehow, younger than he had seemed in his faded hoodie and dark jeans. Edmund had not anticipated this and did not quite know what to do with it.

He looked, also, unmistakably like someone who belonged here. Like a child from Somerton, heading to the Somerton primary school, on a September morning. The green bicycle and the navy sweatshirt and the helmet going down the lane, and then gone around the bend where the hedges pressed in.

Edmund turned back to the kitchen. His tea was cold. He made more.

The library was quiet. It had been quiet in the mornings since mid-July only when Ned was in the garden or in Somerton, and this was a different quality of quiet — the house itself seemed to register the absence. He sat at his desk in the bay window and looked at the manuscript. The story set in the Quantock Hills. The family who found the stones under the garden.

He thought about the story. He thought about Ned on the bicycle. He thought about thirty years of early mornings in the library and how this one was different and in what precise way he was not yet able to articulate.

He wrote a sentence. It was not a very good sentence. He crossed it out.

He looked out the window at the orchard. At the meadow beyond, barely visible. At the top of the stone, dark against the pale September sky.

He wrote another sentence. This one was better. He went on.

---

The school gate was on the right side of the building as Edmund had said — not that Ned had needed reminding, he had filed the detail away when they cycled past it in July. He locked the green bicycle in the rack and stood for a moment with his helmet in his hand and looked at the building.

Small. The kind of Victorian stone school that had been here a long time and showed it. A few children were arriving. None of them looked at Ned, which was fine. He had not expected them to, particularly.

He went in.

---

The classroom was on the first floor, up a staircase with a worn wooden banister. Twenty-three pupils, all of whom had mostly known each other since Reception. And Ned, who had been here for six weeks of the summer and was known by name to a few of them and by sight to most.

His teacher's name was Mr Ashby — late thirties, slightly rumpled, the kind of teacher who had been teaching long enough to have seen most things and was therefore genuinely curious about the few things he hadn't. He had already read Ned's registration form and the notes that came with it, which told him about the living situation and the school history and not very much else. He had been mildly intrigued.

Ned sat in the seat he was directed to — third row, near the window — and put his bag under the chair and his notebooks on the desk and looked around the room with his usual system: exits, adults, atmosphere.

The atmosphere was the particular atmosphere of a classroom at the start of a new year. Organised chaos, everyone finding their place, the year not yet having its shape. Ned had been in this room before, in different schools, with different people. He knew how it worked.

He also knew — in the particular way he knew things, more felt than reasoned — that this was different. That this room had a September and an October and a November in it, and that he would be here for all of them.

He opened his notebook — the observation one, the smaller one — and wrote the date at the top of a fresh page. *September 7th, 2026.* Then he put his pen down and waited for the day to begin.

---

He came home at ten past four.

Edmund heard the back gate and looked at the clock. Then he put the kettle on.

Ned came in and put his bag on the hook and his school shoes on the rack and his helmet on top of them, which was technically incorrect in terms of the rack's organisation but Edmund did not say so. He sat down at the kitchen table in his socked feet — the colourful ones, briefly visible before he tucked his feet under the chair.

Edmund put tea in front of him. Apple juice. A biscuit he had not been asked for.

Ned wrapped his hands around the mug and looked at it for a moment.

"Well?" said Edmund.

Ned considered. "Mr Ashby has a book about fossils on his desk," he said. "A proper one, not a children's one. He had it open at crinoids."

"He sounds promising."

"Will Haddon is in the year above so he's not in my class."

"A modest relief."

Ned looked up, briefly amused. "There's a girl called Rosa. She noticed my compass."

Edmund waited.

"I had it out at lunch," Ned said. "She asked what it was for. I said navigation. She said navigation to where." A pause. "I said I didn't know yet."

"That," said Edmund, "seems like an honest answer."

Ned drank his tea. He looked out the kitchen window at the yard, the woodshed, the kitchen garden where the last of the beans were going over. He had the expression Edmund had come to think of as his settled expression — not happy exactly, because Ned did not perform happiness, but something that had to do with being in the right place.

"It was all right," Ned said, which for Ned was a substantial statement.

"Good," said Edmund, and went back to the range, where something had been going since two o'clock.

They had shepherd's pie for dinner. Ned had two helpings, which he had not done since the first lamb of the summer. Edmund noted this and said nothing.

Outside, in the meadow, the stone stood in the September evening and received the quiet in its usual way, as it received most things — without comment, without hurry, having been doing so for rather longer than either of them had been alive.

---

*Draft notes: The scene is split into three movements — Ned's morning, Edmund's morning alone, Ned's afternoon. Both viewpoints are needed. Edmund's morning alone is the emotional centre from his side — the quality of the quiet being different, the bad sentence crossed out, the better one that follows. Ned's is the day itself, handled lightly — we don't need the full school day, only what he brings home. Rosa noticing the compass is the detail that carries the scene forward: not the stone's compass, not the dramatic version, just a girl at lunch asking an innocent question that Ned deflects with an honest answer. Mr Ashby's fossil book establishes him as someone Ned will be interested in. *It was all right* from Ned is enormous. The two helpings of shepherd's pie is the scene's final note — Edmund measuring Ned's ease not in words but in appetite, which is the most reliable indicator he has found.*
