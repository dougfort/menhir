# Prose Draft — The Third Visit to the Stone (January)

---

It was a Saturday in early January, the second week of the new term just beginning, the year not yet settled into itself. Edmund was at his desk in the library when Ned appeared in the doorway.

He was already in his coat and boots.

Edmund looked up.

"Coming?" Ned said.

Edmund looked at him for a moment — at the coat, the boots, the particular quality of Ned's stillness in the doorway, which was not the stillness of someone waiting for an answer but of someone who has already decided and is extending a courtesy. He put down his pen.

"Give me a moment," he said.

---

The orchard in January was a different country from the orchard in July. The trees bare, the branches making their precise dark shapes against the pale sky, the grass beneath them long and wet and flattened by the winter. Their footsteps left marks in the frost that still lay on the shaded side of the path. The bees were gone. The fruit was gone. The light came from low and southward and made everything slightly more itself than usual — the bark of the apple trees, the moss on the ha-ha wall, the colour of the sky, which was the particular grey of an English January morning that has decided to be luminous rather than dull.

They went through the orchard without talking. Through the ha-ha. Into the meadow.

The stone.

It was different in winter. The summer stone was warm grey and lichen-orange and present in the long grass, part of the meadow's richness. The winter stone was darker, more austere, the lichen vivid against the paler background — a deep orange-rust, a grey almost silver, the black patches of something older and lower that no one had ever identified to Edmund's satisfaction. It stood in the frost-stiffened grass and was simply itself, as it always was, in whatever season, in whatever weather.

They crossed the meadow and came to the mound and stopped.

Ned went to his boulder — the right one, as you faced the stone — without looking for it. His boulder now, by six months of habit. Edmund went to his own.

They sat.

---

The stone in front of them. The meadow around them. The bare trees of the orchard behind, and beyond the orchard the house, and beyond the house the lane and Somerton and the school and the Levels and all the rest of the ordinary world that was also, now, theirs.

Ned put his hand on the stone. Not reaching — just extending, the way you extend a hand to something familiar. The warmth came through immediately, steady and even. Warm and dry in a field where the frost was still hard on the north-facing side of every grass stem.

He held his palm against it for a moment.

Edmund sat on his boulder with his hands in his pockets and looked at the stone and did not think about anything specific, which was what he did here. The quality of time at the stone — slightly different from other time, the watch on his wrist losing its two or three minutes, the morning slightly outside the ordinary measure of morning. He had been sitting here for thirty years and the quality of time was as familiar as anything he knew. More familiar, perhaps.

He was aware of Ned beside him in a way he had not been aware of the stone in July. Both present. The stone unchanged. Ned changed — not in the surface way of children who change visibly from season to season, but in the deeper way of someone who has been somewhere long enough to have become part of it, to have let it become part of them.

After a while Ned took the compass from his pocket.

He did not show it to Edmund. He looked at the needle — the familiar pointing, not north, the stone's direction — and then put it away. A private confirmation, for himself. Edmund saw this from the corner of his eye and said nothing.

A jackdaw crossed the field boundary and banked away from the stone, as birds always did. The meadow was very quiet. A wood pigeon somewhere in the orchard, its enquiry patient and unanswered.

---

"Do you think it knows we're here?" Ned said.

Edmund looked at the stone. He had been asked this before — in the second visit, July, when Ned had been working something out and offered it half-formed: *not ignoring, just something else going on, something massive.* Edmund had given a careful answer then. The question of what *knows* might mean for something that has no brain and no evident sensory apparatus. The philosophical hedge.

This was not that question. This was the same words asking something further along.

"Yes," Edmund said. "I think it does."

He said it without qualification. Not *in some sense*, not *if we allow that the word can be used loosely*. Just yes.

Ned was quiet for a moment. "How long have you thought that?"

Edmund considered. "About twenty years," he said. "I resisted it for the first ten. It seemed like the kind of thought that required more evidence than I had."

"What changed?"

"Nothing changed. I simply stopped requiring evidence for things that were sufficiently well attested by experience." He paused. "It's a particular kind of maturity. I'm not sure it's available to everyone."

Ned absorbed this. He was looking at the stone — not at Edmund, at the stone. His profile against the January sky: the light brown hair slightly longer than in July, the steady set of his face, the quality of his attention which had not changed and which Edmund now understood was not the watchfulness of a frightened child but something older and more settled than that. A sensitivity. A particular relationship with the world.

"It knew before we did," Ned said. "That we were coming. Not today — this summer. That we were going to be here."

Edmund looked at him.

"That's probably too much," Ned said.

"It may be exactly right," Edmund said. He said it carefully, as a serious possibility, not as reassurance. "It may be precisely right."

They sat with this. The stone stood in its slight lean and received the January morning, as it had received every morning for four thousand years, without hurrying any of them.

---

After a while they stood. The cold had been sitting on them — not acutely, they were properly dressed, but present. Ned stamped his feet once and looked at the meadow and then at the stone.

"Same time next week?" he said.

It was the most casual thing he could have said. The most ordinary. It assumed next week, and the week after, and all the weeks the seasons would bring.

"If the weather is reasonable," Edmund said, which was also ordinary, and also not ordinary at all.

They walked back through the meadow, into the orchard. The frost was thawing now on the south-facing side, the grass releasing it in small droplets that caught the low light. A robin appeared on the ha-ha wall and watched them pass with the absolute confidence of a bird that owns wherever it is standing. Ned noticed it. Edmund noticed Ned noticing it.

"Rosa would know what to call that," Ned said. "The way it does that."

"I imagine she'd have strong opinions about it."

"She'd have statistics," Ned said. The internal version of a laugh.

They went in through the back door. Edmund put the kettle on. Ned sat at the kitchen table and opened the field notebook — his, the birthday one, the hardcover waterproof one that had been sitting on his shelf since October with a few pages filled and the rest waiting — and found his page and picked up his pencil.

Edmund did not ask what he was writing. The kettle began its work. The range ticked in the comfortable way of the range. Outside the kitchen window the orchard stood bare and patient in the January morning.

Ned wrote in the field notebook. His handwriting small and precise, the way he did everything.

Edmund made the tea.

---

What Ned wrote — Edmund would not read it then, or for a long time. But if he had looked:

*January. Frost on the meadow, gone by ten on the south side. Stone warm and dry as always. The lichen in winter is more itself than in summer — the colours cleaner. Asked Edmund if it knows we're here. He said yes, without the usual — [a word crossed out and replaced] — without qualifying it. Said he'd thought so for twenty years. I think it knew before we came. He said that might be exactly right.*

*The compass pointed. Put it away. Didn't need to show anyone.*

*E. said same time next week if weather reasonable.*

A pause in the writing. Then, further down the page, in slightly smaller letters:

*It does know. I'm quite sure.*

---

*Draft notes: The scene is quieter than either previous stone visit — deliberately. Less explanation needed, less discovery to be narrated. Both of them simply at the stone, with six months between this visit and the last. The compass taken out privately and put away — Ned's own confirmation, for himself, no longer needing to show it to Edmund or hear Edmund's response. Just checking. The exchange about whether the stone knows: Edmund's yes, unqualified, and Ned's further step — it knew before we came. Edmund receiving this as a serious possibility, not reassurance. The ordinary close: same time next week, if the weather is reasonable. The walk back through the orchard — the robin, Rosa would have statistics. Then the kitchen: Ned writing in the field notebook, Edmund making tea. Not showing, not asking. The field notebook being used for what it was for. The closing image: Edmund making tea while Ned begins to write the book that Edmund thought might not be his own to write. We don't know this yet. We will, later.*

*The note at the end — what Ned wrote — is a structural device this story hasn't used before: the private record, shown to the reader but not to Edmund. I'm not certain it belongs in the finished draft. It may be too explicit. But it's here for now, to show what the field notebook is for, and it can be removed if the scene is stronger without it.*
