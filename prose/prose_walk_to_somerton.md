# Prose Draft — The Walk to Somerton

---

Edmund walked into Somerton most mornings when the weather permitted, which in Somerset in July it generally did. He had done this for years — a habit of the body as much as the mind, the two miles of lane a useful interval between the house and whatever the day required of him. He went early, before it was hot, and he came back with whatever small things were needed: a newspaper sometimes, bread from the baker's, something from the hardware shop that the estate had asked of him.

On Ned's fourth day he said, at breakfast, "I usually walk into town in the mornings. You're welcome to come."

He said it the way he said most things — as an offer rather than a suggestion, with no particular weight attached either way.

Ned looked up from his toast. "All right," he said.

---

The lane was at its best in the early morning, the hedges still damp on the shaded side, the light coming through gaps in the hawthorn in long angled bars. Edmund walked at his ordinary pace, which was not slow but was not hurried, and Ned walked beside him without difficulty. They were quiet for the first stretch, which suited them both.

Edmund pointed things out occasionally, not in the manner of someone conducting a tour, but in the manner of someone thinking aloud.

"Buzzard," he said at one point, not breaking stride, indicating something above the next field with a slight tilt of his head. Ned looked. A large brown shape was circling without apparent effort very high up.

"Are there a lot of them?"

"Reasonable number. They were rare once. They've come back."

Ned watched it until the lane curved and the field disappeared behind the hedge. He didn't say anything else about it, but Edmund noticed he kept an eye on the sky after that.

The lane became a proper road after a mile, with tarmac and occasionally a passing car, and then the outskirts of the town appeared — a terrace of stone cottages, a farm supplies shop with boots in the window, a garage with two elderly cars in various stages of attention outside it.

"It's smaller than I expected," Ned said.

"Most places are."

---

Edmund's first stop was the baker's on the market square, where he bought a small loaf and a conversation with the woman behind the counter, whose name was Mrs. Perrett and who had been there as long as Edmund could remember. She was a solidly built woman of sixty with a direct manner and flour on her apron, and she looked at Ned with the frank curiosity of someone who has lived in a small town long enough to notice everything.

"This is Ned," Edmund said. "He's staying with me for the summer."

"Is he," said Mrs. Perrett. Not a question, exactly. An acknowledgement, filed away. "Kelway, is it?"

"On his mother's side."

Mrs. Perrett looked at Ned for another moment — not unkindly — and then produced a small pastry from beneath the counter and put it on top of the loaf without being asked.

"For the walk back," she said.

Ned said thank you in the careful way he said things to adults he had just met, and Mrs. Perrett gave a small satisfied nod and turned to the next customer. Edmund paid and they went out into the square.

"She'll have told four people by lunchtime," Edmund said mildly. "That you exist. That you're staying at Venn Court. That's simply how it works here."

Ned considered this. "Is that all right?"

"Perfectly. It means you won't have to explain yourself quite as often."

---

They went to the hardware shop next, which was Edmund's natural habitat in Somerton — a narrow, deeply stocked place that smelled of linseed oil and old metal, presided over by a man called Derek who knew where everything was and spoke in the unhurried complete sentences of a man who had never been in a hurry in his life. He and Edmund discussed a particular kind of hinge for some time. Ned moved slowly along the shelves looking at things, picking objects up occasionally and putting them back in exactly the right place, which Derek noticed and approved of.

Afterwards they sat outside a tea room on the square with something hot and a slice of cake each, and it was here that Ned saw the children.

There were four of them — three boys and a girl, somewhere between his age and a few years older — circling the far side of the square on bicycles with the purposeless, pleasurable energy of children with nowhere particular to be. They weren't doing anything except being on bicycles in summer, which was sufficient. One of them said something and the others laughed. They circled again.

Edmund watched Ned watch them.

He said nothing for a while. He drank his tea. Then he said: "Do you ride a bicycle?"

Ned moved his gaze from the children. "I used to. At one of the — yes. I can."

Edmund nodded. Drank more tea.

"I'll ask around," he said.

---

It took less time than Edmund had expected. Derek in the hardware shop knew a family on the Langport road whose youngest had grown out of a bicycle two summers ago. A phone call from the shop's ancient landline — Derek making the call himself, unhurriedly, while Edmund and Ned waited among the hinges and the linseed oil — established that yes, it was still available, and yes, they were welcome to collect it that afternoon.

The bicycle was a reasonable shade of green and somewhat scratched, with seven gears of which five worked reliably, a bell that functioned, and tyres that needed air but were otherwise sound. It was, Edmund judged, about the right size, which meant Ned would grow into it over the summer and possibly not quite out of it before September.

"What do you think?" he said.

Ned put his hand on the handlebar. He looked at it carefully, the way he looked at most things.

"It's good," he said.

They paid a modest amount. The woman at the door watched them go with the satisfied expression of someone who has resolved a small problem that has been sitting in the garage for two years.

Edmund had already found a helmet — a sensible blue one, from Derek's shop, discovered on a hook near the back where such things apparently lived — and he produced it now from the bag he was carrying and held it out. Ned took it without comment and put it on and adjusted the strap with the brisk efficiency of someone who has worn helmets before.

Edmund walked. Ned rode.

---

The ride back was something Edmund found he had not anticipated enjoying as much as he did.

Ned rode ahead to the first bend, then stopped and waited for Edmund to catch up, then rode ahead again. After a while he stopped waiting at the bends and simply rode back, circling around Edmund at a careful distance, then riding ahead again. He did this several times — not performing, not showing off, simply unable quite to leave Edmund behind and equally unable quite to stay. There was something in it that Edmund didn't examine too closely.

At one point Ned rode up alongside him and matched his pace for thirty seconds or so, wheels turning almost too slowly to balance, before the effort became unreasonable and he gave up and rode ahead again.

The lane was quiet. The hedges went past. A rabbit considered them from the verge and thought better of it.

Edmund walked at his ordinary pace and thought about nothing in particular, which was, he reflected, becoming somewhat easier since Ned had arrived.

---

*Draft notes: This scene works through accumulation — small details building a world. Mrs. Perrett's pastry is the key gesture: unsentimental, practical, local warmth without sentimentality. Edmund's line about being told to four people is his dry humour at its best. The hardware shop is Edmund's natural habitat — Derek's unhurried complete sentences rhyme with Edmund's own manner. Ned putting objects back in exactly the right place is a small character moment. The bicycle ride home is deliberately the emotional centre of the scene — Ned circling back, unable to leave Edmund behind, doing it without knowing quite why. The final line — thinking about nothing in particular, which has been easier since Ned arrived — is Edmund registering something without naming it, as usual.*
