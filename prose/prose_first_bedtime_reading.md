# Prose Draft — The First Night (Revised)

---

The viewpoint shifts here. This scene belongs to both of them.

---

Edmund had shown Ned where the bathroom was, had pointed out the small lamp on the bedside table, had said goodnight in the way he said most things — quietly, without excess — and had got as far as the corridor before he noticed that the boy hadn't moved.

Ned was standing in the middle of the room in his pyjamas with his hands at his sides, looking at nothing in particular. Not distressed, exactly. Just very still, in the way a person is still when they are working something out and haven't finished yet.

Edmund stood in the doorway for a moment.

The house was old and it was night and it made sounds at night, as old houses do — the tick and settle of cooling stone, the occasional creak from somewhere in the Victorian wing, a sound like a door that wasn't a door. Edmund had lived with these sounds his entire life and they meant nothing to him. To a boy on his first night they would mean something rather different.

He looked at Ned. Ned looked, carefully and without expression, at the middle distance.

---

What Ned was actually doing, standing there, was listening.

Every house had its night quality and he had learned to take its measure before he could sleep. This was not simple childish fear of the dark — he had outgrown that, if he had ever had it. It was something more specific. In other houses, in the particular stillness of night, things had happened. Small things, unwitnessed. He had woken to find objects rearranged, to sense a change in the quality of the air, to lie very still and wait for whatever it was to settle. It always settled. But it was necessary to wait, and the waiting required a particular quality of attention that he did not know how to turn off.

He had learned not to tell people about this. People who were told about this made arrangements.

So he stood in his pyjamas in this new room and listened to the house. The tick of cooling stone. A creak from somewhere. A sound that was not quite a door. He catalogued these things carefully, the way he catalogued everything. None of them were wrong, exactly. They were just the sounds of a very old house settling into the night, and he did not know yet whether they were sounds he would learn to trust.

Then something else. Something he could not catalogue.

The quality of Edmund in the doorway.

He had registered this man all day — his quietness, the way he moved through his own house without effort, the particular kind of attention he paid to things. But what Ned noticed now, standing in the dark in his pyjamas with the house making its sounds around him, was that Edmund was not afraid of any of it. Not performing fearlessness. Not managing his discomfort. Simply — unafraid. The house at night was his house at night, as familiar and unthreatening as daylight, and he stood in the doorway with the settled quality of someone in exactly the right place.

Ned had not encountered this before. An adult who was genuinely comfortable in the dark.

He looked at Edmund. Edmund looked back. Neither of them said anything for a moment.

---

"The Little Prince is on your shelf," Edmund said. "Have you read it?"

Ned glanced at the shelf. "No."

"Would you like to?"

A pause. "Now?"

"If you like."

---

Edmund retrieved the chair from the small desk in the corner and set it beside the bed. He waited while Ned got in and arranged the pillows — two of them, propped against the headboard — and settled himself. Then he sat down, opened the book to the first page, and began to read.

*Once when I was six years old I saw a magnificent picture in a book called True Stories from Nature...*

He read without performance, at his usual unhurried pace. The room was warm — the house held the day's heat in its old stone walls — and the small lamp cast a comfortable circle of light. Outside, somewhere beyond the orchard, a tawny owl called once and was answered.

Ned lay against his pillows and listened. Not just to the book — to the room. The house still made its sounds but Edmund sat beside him entirely unbothered, turning a page, and in the space of that unbothered-ness something in Ned's listening began, very slightly, to change quality. Not to stop. But to loosen. The sounds of the house were simply the sounds of the house, and Edmund had always known this, and nothing was wrong.

After a page and a half Edmund stopped and held the book across.

Ned took it. Found the place. Read, in a voice slightly more careful than his speaking voice, the way children are when they read aloud and are not quite sure yet of the audience.

*In the course of this life I have had a great many encounters with a great many people who have been concerned with matters of consequence. I have lived a great deal among grown-ups. I have seen them intimately, close at hand. And this hasn't much improved my opinion of them.*

He paused on that last sentence, just fractionally, in a way that had nothing to do with the reading.

Edmund received the book back without comment.

---

They went on like this for a while. Edmund read, passed the book. Ned read, passed it back. They found a rhythm that suited them both — neither rushing, neither making anything of it. The owl called again, further away this time.

The particular vigilance Ned brought to new places at night — the watchfulness, the waiting — was still there, but it had changed its quality. It was still listening, but it was listening to different things now. To Edmund's voice, even and unhurried. To the pattern of the book. To the house, which went on making its sounds and remained entirely untroubling.

He had not felt this in any other house. This specific absence of dread.

He did not know what to do with that yet, so he held it quietly and kept reading.

Edmund was reading the passage about the baobabs — *a question of discipline*, the little prince had said — when he noticed that Ned's responses were slowing. The boy was still propped against his pillows, still holding the book when it was his turn, but his reading had grown quieter and there were longer pauses between sentences.

Edmund received the book back and read on.

When he looked up again Ned had slipped slightly sideways against the pillows, the blanket pulled up. His eyes were closed. His breathing had changed — deeper, slower, the particular quality of sleep that comes quickly to children and deeply to those who have been uncertain all day and have finally, provisionally, decided that this place is safe enough to let go.

Edmund sat for a while without moving.

He was not, in the ordinary course of things, a man given to sentiment. He had arranged his life around solitude and found it genuinely sufficient. He had not anticipated — had not thought to anticipate — the particular quality of this moment: the warm room, the sleeping boy, the old book open in his hands.

The little prince had been asking questions about sheep.

Edmund closed the book carefully and set it on the bedside table. He sat a little longer, listening to the house settle around them both. The creak from the Victorian wing. The tick of stone. Nothing that was not simply the house being itself.

Then he turned off the lamp, stood, and put the chair back where he had found it.

At the door he paused and looked back. Ned was deeply asleep, one hand still curved slightly as if holding the book.

The sleep of a boy who had, for one night at least, put his watchfulness down.

Edmund went along the tilted corridor to his own room.

He did not examine what he was feeling, particularly. But he did not dismiss it either. He simply noted it, the way he noted most things — with attention, and without hurry.

---

*Draft notes: The revision adds Ned's interior life to the first half — his practiced nighttime vigilance, the cataloguing, the specific fear not of the dark but of what happens in the dark. The key addition is Ned reading Edmund's quality: not the performance of fearlessness but genuine ease, a man at home in his own house at night. This is what Ned has never encountered before and what begins to loosen his watchfulness. The scene remains primarily Edmund's in its second half — his unspoken feeling, his careful noting. But now we understand what Ned was experiencing on his side of it. The final line before Edmund leaves — "the sleep of a boy who had, for one night at least, put his watchfulness down" — names without over-explaining what the scene has been building toward. The Victorian wing creak is still just a creak. Nothing happens in this house. That is the point.*
