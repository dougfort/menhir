# Prose Draft — The School Project (Somerset Prehistoric Monuments)

---

## Day One — Stanton Drew

The project had been set on a Tuesday. Ned had chosen his topic by Wednesday morning and started on it by Wednesday afternoon, which was faster than Mr Ashby had expected, though Mr Ashby was already developing an accurate sense of how Ned's mind worked.

*Choose something from the history of your local area and research it.* Ned had written his topic at the top of a fresh page in his observation notebook before he had even got his bicycle out of the rack: *Prehistoric monuments of Somerset.* Then, because he was Ned, he had underlined it neatly and added a question mark, because there was still the question of where to start.

He started at the Somerton library. Miss Pardew, who had been tracking Ned's reading choices since July with the quiet interest of someone who recognises an unusual reader, produced three books without being asked and said there were more if he needed them. He took the three books and sat at the table by the window and read for an hour and a half, and when he left he had four pages of notes and a question he needed to ask Edmund.

---

Edmund was in the kitchen when Ned got home, doing something to a chicken that appeared to involve concentration. Ned put his bag on the hook and his school shoes on the rack and his helmet on top of them and sat down at the table with his notebook.

"There's a place called Stanton Drew," he said.

"Yes," said Edmund.

"It's fifteen miles from here."

"Approximately."

"It's the second biggest stone circle in Britain." Ned looked at his notes. "After Avebury. The Great Circle is a hundred and thirteen metres across." He looked up. "That's bigger than a football pitch."

"Rather bigger, yes."

"And there are three circles, not just one." A pause. "And there are stones in a pub garden."

Edmund put down whatever he was doing with the chicken. "The Cove," he said. "Three stones in the garden of the Druid's Arms. They were probably the entrance to a chambered tomb, at some point. The pub was built around them."

Ned looked at him. "They built a pub around a neolithic tomb."

"They built a pub near a neolithic tomb and the tomb ended up in the garden. These things happen in Somerset." He washed his hands at the sink. "Have you been reading about Stanton Drew for your project?"

"Yes. But I want to know what you think about it."

Edmund dried his hands and came to sit at the table. This was something he had started doing over the course of the summer — sitting down when Ned asked him something, rather than answering from across the room. It had happened gradually and without discussion.

"I've been several times," he said. "It's extraordinary. Most of the stones are fallen now — you have to look carefully to see the shape of the circles. But when you do —" He paused. "When you do, the scale of it becomes real. The Great Circle is enormous. What they moved, and where they moved it from."

"The book said the geophysical surveys found postholes," Ned said. "Inside the circles. Where there used to be wooden posts."

"Enormous wooden posts. Old trees, probably sacred to whoever they were to." Edmund looked at the notebook. "What's that question mark for?"

"I'm not sure where to start. There's so much."

Edmund considered. "What is the project actually asking you to do?"

"Present your findings to the class. With a map."

"Then start with the map." He said it the way he said most things to Ned — simply, without condescension. "You know how to make maps. Make the map of Somerset first, mark everything on it, and then you'll see what you have. The shape of it will tell you what the project is about."

Ned looked at him. Then he opened his mapping notebook — a new one, started since Venn Court — and began to draw the outline of Somerset from memory, which was accurate enough to be slightly uncanny.

Edmund went back to the chicken.

"Edmund," Ned said, without looking up from the map.

"Yes."

"Have you been to all of them? All the prehistoric sites?"

"Most. Some are difficult to access. Some are not much to look at on the surface — they look like fields."

"But they don't feel like fields."

Edmund was quiet for a moment. "No," he said. "They don't feel like fields."

Ned added a small circle to his map, northwest of Somerton. Stanton Drew. He drew it carefully and labelled it in his precise, slightly architectural handwriting. Then he looked at it for a moment.

"The people who built Stanton Drew," he said. "They're the same people who built the stone here, aren't they. The same — period. The same kind of people."

"Broadly speaking. Late Neolithic to early Bronze Age. Give or take a thousand years."

"So they would have known about each other. The stone here and the circles there. They would have both been — part of the same world."

"That's exactly right." Edmund said it with the particular quality of attention he reserved for when Ned arrived somewhere he had himself taken a long time to reach. "That's precisely what I think. They weren't isolated acts. They were part of a conversation across the landscape."

Ned looked at his map. At the small circle he had drawn northwest of Somerton, and at the dot he had already marked — without thinking, automatically — for Venn Court.

"What were they saying?" he asked.

"We don't know," Edmund said. "We can't. But they were saying something. That much seems certain."

Ned went back to his map. After a moment he said, without looking up: "I want to go there. To Stanton Drew."

"We can go on Saturday," Edmund said, "if the weather is reasonable."

## Day Two — The Sweet Track

Thursday. Ned at the kitchen table with his phone and two of Miss Pardew's books and his notebook, which now had the Somerset map on one page and three pages of notes on the other. Edmund was in the library. The range was going. Outside the window the kitchen garden was past its best — the beans long finished, the last of the squash brought in, the beds looking tired in the October light.

Ned had been reading about the Somerset Levels.

"Edmund," he called.

A pause. Then Edmund appeared in the doorway. He had learned not to answer Ned's calls from the next room, on the grounds that whatever Ned wanted was generally worth a proper response.

"The Sweet Track," Ned said.

Edmund came in and sat down. "What about it?"

"It was built in 3806 BCE." Ned looked at his notes. "Exactly 3806. They can date it from the tree rings." He looked up. "That's five thousand eight hundred years ago."

"Approximately, yes."

"The Egyptian pyramids were built around 2500 BCE." Ned put his pen down. "The Sweet Track is older than the pyramids."

"Yes."

"By thirteen hundred years."

"About that."

Ned sat back in his chair and looked at the ceiling for a moment, which was what he did when something needed the full space of his attention.

"It was a wooden walkway," he said. "Across the marsh. So people could cross the Levels."

"The Levels were a shallow inland sea at that point. The walkway ran about two kilometres across the marsh. They felled specific trees — ash, oak, lime — split the planks, laid them in a herringbone pattern on poles driven into the peat. Very precise. Very well made." Edmund paused. "The extraordinary thing is the peat preserved it. If you'd built it anywhere else it would have rotted to nothing four thousand years ago."

"I saw the Levels from the train," Ned said. "When I first came."

"I know."

"It looked — very old."

"It is very old. One of the oldest intact prehistoric landscapes in Europe." Edmund got up and went to the library. He came back with an Ordnance Survey map — the 1:25000 Explorer sheet for the area — and opened it on the table between them. "Here." He pointed. "The Levels. You can see how flat it is — that's the old sea floor. These slight ridges are where the prehistoric communities lived — just high enough to be above the water."

Ned leaned over the map. His eyes moved across it with the particular focused attention he brought to all maps, reading the contour lines, the drainage patterns, the names of farms and fields that recorded the landscape's history in compressed form.

"It's all still there," he said. "Underneath."

"Underneath. Bronze Age villages, Neolithic settlements, wooden trackways, bog bodies. The peat has kept all of it." Edmund traced a line on the map. "The Sweet Track ran roughly here. You can't see it — it's six feet down in the peat — but it's there."

Ned looked at the map for a long time. Then: "Is the stone on here?"

Edmund moved the map slightly. Found Venn Court — the house marked, the lane, the orchard wall just visible as a boundary line. And then, a small distance from the house, in the field that was the meadow: a tiny Gothic-script symbol. A scheduled monument marker.

Ned looked at it.

"It's on the map," he said.

"Scheduled Ancient Monument. It's been recorded since the nineteenth century. The Ordnance Survey has marked it since the first edition."

Ned looked at the symbol for a long time. It was very small. The Gothic script was the same font used for all the ancient monuments on the map — the same as the label for a Bronze Age barrow two miles east, the same as the notation for a Roman road. Ned's stone in the same language as everything else.

"Can I —" He stopped. Started again. "Could I borrow this map?"

"You can have it," Edmund said. "I have another."

Ned looked at him. "You have two copies of the same OS map?"

"I have three," Edmund said. "I use them for different purposes."

Ned looked briefly at him with the expression that meant he was privately amused and had decided not to say so. Then he carefully took the map and folded it, exactly along its original folds, and put it beside his notebook.

He opened the notebook to the Somerset map he was making and found the location of Venn Court. Copied the monument symbol exactly — the same Gothic font, as close as he could get with his pencil. Then, beneath it, in his own handwriting: the grid reference he had memorised from the OS map.

He put the pen down and looked at what he had drawn.

The real and the private, the official and the felt, on the same page.

## Day Three — Saturday. Stanton Drew.

It rained on Friday. Saturday was cold but clear, the sky that particular shade of October blue that has some grey in it, the light coming from low to the south and making long shadows even at midday.

Edmund drove. The lanes narrowed and widened in the way of Somerset lanes, the hedges still holding their leaves though the colour was going. Ned sat in the passenger seat with his mapping notebook on his knee and watched the landscape.

"It's all the same stone," he said, after a while.

"What is?"

"The buildings. The walls. It's all the same colour — that yellowish grey." He looked out the window. "Like Venn Court."

"Local limestone. They've been quarrying it here for two thousand years. The Romans used it. Everyone since has used it." Edmund slowed for a corner. "It comes out of the ground the colour of old honey and weathers to grey. Every village in Somerset looks the same because they're all built from the same hill."

Ned turned this over. "So the stone in the meadow —"

"Local limestone, yes. Quarried somewhere in the area, probably." A pause. "That's one of the questions I've never been able to answer. Where specifically it came from. The geology suggests somewhere on the Mendips — there's a formation up there that produces stone of that character — but I haven't found a definitive match."

"Did you look for one?"

"For several years, when I was younger." Edmund said this without embarrassment. "I became somewhat expert in Somerset limestone formation. It didn't resolve the question. Which I now regard as part of the point."

Ned looked at him. "Part of the point meaning what?"

"Meaning that there are questions about the stone I will never be able to answer by research alone. The research tells you what kind of questions to ask. It doesn't always tell you the answers." He indicated and turned. "Some things have to be — approached differently."

Ned said nothing. He understood what Edmund meant, or thought he did, and either way it was not something that needed more words than Edmund had given it.

They parked in the village and walked to the field. A man in a farmyard watched them with the equanimity of someone who had watched people come to look at the stones all his life. Edmund gave him a pound for the collection box at the gate, which was the local arrangement.

And then the field.

Most of the stones were fallen — great slabs of grey limestone lying in the grass at odd angles, like something dropped and left where it had landed. But the ones that remained standing, even the stumps of them, gave the shape of the circle. Ned walked out from the gate slowly and stopped in the middle of it.

He stood very still.

The Great Circle was enormous even now, even reduced, even scattered — the diameter of it made itself felt as a spatial fact, the way the stone at Venn Court made its presence felt. Not sinister. Not frightening. Just — there, in a way that most things were not quite there. Occupied, in some quality he did not have a word for.

He took out his compass.

The needle moved. Not dramatically — not the decisive pointing he had seen at the stone in the meadow. A trembling, an uncertainty, the needle hunting for something and not quite finding it. As if the field had a question rather than an answer.

He showed it to Edmund.

Edmund looked at it for a long moment. "Interesting," he said.

"It's different from the stone at home."

Edmund looked at him when he said *home* but didn't say anything about it.

"The anomaly here is distributed," Edmund said, returning to the compass. "It's a field full of stones. At Venn Court it's concentrated — one point, one source." He looked out at the fallen circle. "Whatever they were doing here required many stones. Whatever was done with our stone required one."

Ned put the compass away. He walked the circumference of the Great Circle slowly, stepping around the fallen stones, getting the measure of it. Edmund walked with him at a slight distance, in the way he had learned to accompany Ned — close but not intrusive, present but not demanding.

When they had gone all the way around Ned stopped and looked back toward the gate.

"I want to see the pub garden ones," he said. "The Cove."

They went to the Druid's Arms. The landlord, a large comfortable man who had clearly been answering questions about the stones in his garden his whole working life, showed them through the side gate without particular ceremony. There they were: three large limestone slabs standing in a rough horseshoe, surrounded on all sides by the ordinary paraphernalia of a pub garden. Picnic tables. A parasol, furled. A stack of plastic chairs.

Ned stood and looked at them for a moment.

"Neolithic tomb," he said. "In a pub garden."

"As I said," said Edmund.

Ned looked at the stones. They were old in a way the surroundings were not. The picnic tables belonged to the last thirty years. The pub to the eighteenth century. The stones to a time so far back that the concept of a pub, or a garden, or a village, or even the language Ned was thinking in, had not yet existed.

And yet they were the same. Standing here among the plastic chairs exactly as they stood — he thought of this without quite deciding to — as the stone in the meadow stood in its field. The same quality. The same presence. Not diminished by the parasol and the stack of chairs. Not requiring the meadow, or the privacy, or Edmund's thirty years of attention. Simply what they were, regardless.

He stood with this for a moment.

"Edmund," Ned said.

"Yes."

"Do you think they knew? The people who built the pub. Did they know what the stones were?"

"Almost certainly not in any precise sense. They knew they were old. They knew they were significant. They built around them rather than removing them, which says something." He paused. "People have always known, on some level, that these things are not to be disturbed. Even when the specific reason has been forgotten."

Ned nodded slowly. He took out his notebook and made a quick sketch of the three stones — their relative positions, the angles they made, the way the tallest one leaned very slightly to the left. He was not conscious of this being like anything else. He did not notice that Edmund, standing beside him, was watching the sketch take shape with an expression he would have found difficult to name.

On the drive home Ned was quiet in the comfortable way, not the closed way — looking out the window at the limestone villages, at the long shadows of late afternoon across the fields, at the Levels visible in the distance, flat and grey and ancient.

"For the project," he said, as they turned into the Venn Court lane, "I'm going to make a proper map. All the sites. With distances and dates."

"That sounds right," Edmund said.

"And I want to write something about why people keep coming back to them. The sites. For thousands of years." He looked out the window. "Even when they've forgotten what they were for. Why they still come."

Edmund pulled the car up to the stable block. Turned the engine off. Sat for a moment.

"That," he said, "is rather a good question for a school project."

"Mr Ashby said choose something interesting." Ned unclipped his seatbelt. "So."

He got out of the car and went into the house.

Edmund sat for a moment longer in the silence of the stable block, looking at the kitchen window where the light was on, and thought about a question that had kept him interested for thirty years being posed as a school project by a nine year old boy.

Then he got out of the car and went to make tea.
