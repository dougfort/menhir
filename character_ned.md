# Character Sheet — Ned

---

## Basic Facts

- **Name:** Ned
- **Age:** Nine years old on arrival. Turns ten in mid to late October 2026, six or seven weeks into the school term.
- **School year:** Just completed Year 4 (Key Stage 2, primary school). Will enter Year 5 at Somerton primary school in September 2026.
- **Relation to Edmund Kelway:** Distant — a Kelway on his mother's side, the connection traced through women, vague enough to feel old
- **Situation:** Has been passed around various family members since he was small. Sent to Venn Court as a last stop before foster care. Arrives mid to late July 2026, at the start of the six-week summer holiday.
- **Birthday:** Mid to late October. His tenth birthday falls during his first term at Somerton primary school.

---

## Appearance

- Small for his age — not so small as to invite comment, just small enough that adults occasionally underestimate him, which he has learned to use
- Slender in the way of boys who move a lot and don't think about food much — not delicate, just spare
- Light brown hair, gets lighter in summer
- Brown eyes — his most notable feature. Steady and attentive in a way that can make adults slightly uncomfortable without knowing why
- Hands usually slightly ink-stained — he draws maps and characters from his inner world in notebooks he keeps carefully

---

## What He Wears

For the summer journey and arrival — practical and unremarkable. Not shabby enough to invite pity, not new enough to suggest anyone has thought carefully about him recently.

- Trainers with some miles on them
- Dark jeans
- A t-shirt under a hoodie — grey or dark blue, logo faded or absent
- A waterproof jacket slightly too large, bought to be grown into and not yet grown into

One private detail: colourful socks, incongruous beneath the otherwise neutral clothing. The kind of small personal thing a self-contained person allows themselves. Nobody else notices them. Ned knows they are there.

---

## What He Brought to Venn Court

Ned travels light — not because he has been told to, but because he has learned that possessions are a liability when you move frequently. You can't carry what you can't lose.

### The Backpack — Carried on His Lap the Whole Journey
The important bag. Everything that matters is here.

- **The notebooks** — two or three, different sizes. One for the world itself: maps, geography, the shape of things. One for characters and stories. Possibly a third, smaller one, for observations about the actual world. Well-used but carefully kept, pencils tucked inside the covers.
- **A book** — one paperback, currently being read. Dog-eared at the right places, no cracked spine.
- **His phone** — modest, not new. Loaded with music and a few games. The signal problem at Venn Court discovered within the first hour.
- **A spare pair of colourful socks**
- **The compass**

### The Compass
Small, brass-cased, slightly tarnished. A glass face with one very fine scratch across it. The needle still true. Old enough to have come from somewhere specific — possibly from a relative, possibly found in a drawer in one of the houses he passed through and kept because it felt like it wanted keeping. Its origin is not entirely clear even to Ned.

A compass always knows where north is. It doesn't matter which house you're in, which town, which unfamiliar room — the needle finds north and stays there. For a boy who has never had a fixed point, there is something quietly important about that. He doesn't think about this consciously. He just keeps it.

Near the stone, the compass needle will behave strangely. Ned will notice this before he mentions it to anyone.

### The Bag — Practical, Not Large
- Clothes for a season — enough but not generous. T-shirts, jeans, a spare hoodie, underwear, all folded with the neatness of someone who has packed a bag many times.
- Trainers and one pair of slightly smarter shoes.
- A washbag with the basics.

### What He Doesn't Have
No childhood toys. No photographs displayed or framed — though possibly one, folded small, tucked inside a notebook. No accumulated clutter of a settled life. Nothing that would be difficult to repack.

---

## How He Carries Himself

- Not hunched or defensive — contained. Doesn't take up more space than he needs.
- Sits still more easily than most boys his age — this is not quite the same thing as contentment, though adults tend to approve of it without realizing the difference
- Moves quietly — not sneakily, just without unnecessary noise, the way someone learns to move when they have slept in a lot of houses that weren't quite their own

---

## Character

- Bright and even tempered
- Reads people quickly and accurately — he has had to
- Knows how to make himself acceptable in a new environment without quite giving himself away
- Has a system in new places: doesn't react, watches, finds the exits, assesses the adults
- Polite and easy to like without being eager to please — there is a difference, and Ned embodies it
- Feels things deeply but doesn't lean on people before he knows if they will hold his weight
- Self contained without being shut down
- Gets along well with other children in the short term — knows how to do that — but has never had the chance for a long term friendship, the slow accumulated intimacy of a friendship with history

---

## The Reading and the Inner World

Reads a lot. Has built an elaborate private fantasy world — internally consistent, with its own geography and its own rules. This is the place where Ned is sovereign, regardless of what his actual circumstances are.

The world exists in notebooks — maps, characters, histories — carried carefully from house to house. The notebooks are his one constant possession. Everything else can be left behind.

Whether this world is something he would share with the right person remains to be seen.

### Books He Is Likely to Have Read and Loved
- Alan Garner — *The Weirdstone of Brisingamen*, *The Owl Service*: children who discover the old landscape is still alive underneath the modern one
- Susan Cooper — *The Dark is Rising* sequence: the ancient and the contemporary in direct contact
- Retellings of Norse, Greek, and Arthurian myth — old stories that feel like they are reporting something real
- Natural history, geology, old atlases — books with maps especially
- Quiet ghost stories and uncanny fiction — the unsettling kind, not the gory kind

### The Three Books on His Shelf at Venn Court
Edmund placed these from his own childhood collection — old, well-kept, personal rather than purchased for the occasion. Ned understood this immediately.

- *The Little Prince* — early English edition with Saint-Exupéry's own illustrations
- *The Wind in the Willows* — a proper edition with E.H. Shepard illustrations
- *Treasure Island* — with N.C. Wyeth's large painted illustrations

---

## Relationship With Adults

Has a finely calibrated sense of adults. Knows the difference between adults who are kind because it costs them nothing and adults who are genuinely paying attention. Has met very few of the second kind.

Edmund will confuse him initially. Edmund treats him like an interesting adult, which Ned hasn't encountered before. It will take him a while to know what to do with this.

---

## Arriving at Venn Court

The system Ned uses in new places — watch, don't react, find the exits, assess the adults — begins to fail him at Venn Court almost immediately. The house is settled in a way that resists assessment. Edmund doesn't require anything of him. The three books on the shelf were not bought for him.

The stone he will encounter during the six summer weeks before school. His inner world, with its own ancient geography, will understand instinctively that the stone is serious.

---

## His One Vulnerability

For all his self containment, Ned wants — very quietly, without quite admitting it even to himself — somewhere permanent. Not luxury. Not even happiness particularly. Just somewhere that will still be there tomorrow.

Venn Court, which has been there for centuries, may be the first place that has ever felt like it might qualify.
