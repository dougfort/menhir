# Ned's Journey to Venn Court

---

## How Edmund Is Drawn In

Edmund receives a letter — from a solicitor or social worker, or perhaps a distant family member acting as intermediary. Formal and slightly awkward in tone, written with the careful understatement of someone who doesn't want to oversell the difficulty. There is a boy. He is family, after a fashion. There is nowhere else.

Edmund's first instinct is no. He sits with the letter for several days. Perhaps visits the stone.

What changes his mind, probably in this order:

- A quiet sense of obligation to the family name and the estate — Venn Court has housed Kelways for centuries, including difficult ones
- A single detail in the letter about the boy — perhaps the notebooks are mentioned — that catches his attention in a way he doesn't entirely dismiss
- The honest recognition that he has room, both physical and circumstantial, even if he has no experience

---

## Origin

Ned comes from London — large enough that he has been just one more child in it, anonymous, which suits his recent life. He departs from **Paddington Station** — a station with its own literary history of arriving children.

---

## The Train Journey

**London Paddington to Castle Cary** — roughly two hours on a good train.

Ned has a seat reservation. Someone has thought about this practically even if not much else. He has a bag — not a large one. He doesn't own that much. The notebooks are in his backpack, which stays on his lap throughout.

He is not frightened. He has arrived in enough new places that the mechanics of it hold no terror. But he is watchful in his particular way — taking in information, not yet knowing what will be useful.

He eats his packed lunch too early in the journey and then regrets it.

### What He Sees Out the Window

The city gives way to suburbs, suburbs to the western counties. The landscape becomes visibly older and greener as Somerset arrives.

The moment the Somerset Levels appear is significant — the flat wetlands spreading out suddenly wide and strange, an old landscape that looks like somewhere things happened a long time ago. Ned doesn't know yet that he is going to a place with a stone that makes everything else feel recent.

---

## Castle Cary Station

A very small station — one of the smallest in England still served by mainline trains.

Ned steps off and it is quiet after London. Birdsong is audible — actual birdsong — which catches him slightly off guard.

Edmund is on the platform. He has thought about this moment and prepared nothing useful. He may be holding a piece of paper with Ned's name on it — both unnecessary and endearing, since there is only one unaccompanied boy on the platform.

Neither of them quite knows what to do with the other yet.

---

## The Car Journey — Castle Cary to Venn Court

Approximately twenty to twenty five minutes on country roads, growing progressively quieter and narrower. The last quarter mile to the house is unpaved lane, lined with old hedgerow growing high on both sides.

This journey is their first time alone together. Edmund will ask a careful question or two. Ned will answer accurately and without elaboration. There will be stretches of silence that are not quite comfortable and not quite uncomfortable.

Ned will be looking out the window.

---

## Arriving at Venn Court

The house presents its Georgian front first — a certain worn dignity. Then the yard, the woodshed, the back door that is almost never locked.

Edmund shows Ned his room — Georgian part of the house, higher ceilings, larger windows, looking out toward the orchard. On a clear day, from a certain angle, standing on tiptoe possibly, the top of the stone is just visible above the treeline.

Ned notices this. He says nothing about it.

---

## Notes for the Prose Draft

- The train journey is Ned's first chapter in a sense — it should carry the weight of transition, the city releasing him into something older
- The packed lunch eaten too early is a small human detail worth keeping
- The birdsong at Castle Cary station is the moment Somerset announces itself
- Edmund with his unnecessary piece of paper sets his character immediately
- The first words exchanged between Edmund and Ned on the platform will set the tone for everything — worth drafting with care when the time comes
- Ned noticing the stone from his bedroom window on the first day, and saying nothing, is the quiet beginning of the central thread
