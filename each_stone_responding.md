# Each Stone Responding to the Nearest Stones — A Developing Idea

*Note made after the murmuration scene. An idea worth holding.*

---

## The Idea as It Arrived

In the car on the way home from Shapwick Heath, Ned says:

*"Do you think that's what it was like? Before. When there were a lot of stones. Like a murmuration. Each stone responding to the nearest stones. No one in charge. But something coming out of all of them together. Something that isn't any of the stones but comes from all of them."*

Edmund's response: *"I don't know. But I think that's rather a good way to think about it."*

The idea arrives as a nine-year-old's intuition — a connection made between what he has just watched and what he knows about the prehistoric landscape of Somerset. It is not argued or theorised. It simply arrives, the way Ned's best observations arrive, in the middle of something else.

---

## What the Idea Actually Contains

The thought underneath it is: **intelligence without a centre. Pattern without a planner.**

The stones of Somerset's prehistoric landscape — Stanton Drew, Priddy, the stone at Venn Court, the dozens of others marked in Gothic script on Ordnance Survey maps — were not a system designed by a single mind working from a plan. They were responses. Each stone placed in relation to what was already there: to the landscape, to water, to the sun at particular times of year, to the other stones already standing.

What emerged from all those individual decisions, made across centuries and by many different people, was a sacred geography covering a whole county. No one built it. It accreted, stone by stone, each one in conversation with the nearest ones.

This is structurally identical to how a murmuration works. Each starling following simple rules about its seven nearest neighbours. No leader, no choreographer. Something emerging from the accumulation of local decisions that is larger and stranger than any of the parts.

---

## Why the Idea Has Legs

**It reframes the question of intention.** The standard question about prehistoric monuments is: *what did the builders mean?* Edmund has been asking this for thirty years. The murmuration model suggests the question may be wrong, or at least incomplete. The meaning wasn't designed into any individual stone. It emerged from the pattern. You can't find it by looking at one stone. You have to look at the whole landscape at once — and even then you can only see the shape, not understand it from inside.

**It applies to the stone at Venn Court specifically.** The stone is not a solitary mystery. It is a note in a chord. It was placed in conversation with other stones, responding to the nearest ones, and something came out of that conversation that is still present — not in any single stone, but in the space between them, the relationship. Edmund's thirty years at the stone have been a form of listening, but perhaps he has been listening to the wrong thing: the stone alone, rather than the stone in its landscape.

**It connects to the story's central preoccupation.** The stone at Venn Court receives what Ned carries. But maybe it has always been part of a receiving system — the whole prehistoric landscape of Somerset quietly continuing to do what it was doing five thousand years ago, each stone still in its relationship with the nearest ones, the pattern still present underneath the farms and the drainage ditches and the motorway. Ned, whose sensitivity is unusual, is the first person in a long time to feel the full weight of it. The stone is the nearest node to him. But it is not the only one.

**It is also a description of how the story itself works.** The relationship between Edmund and Ned has no single turning point. It accretes — small decisions, small acts of attention, each one responding to what was nearest. The kitchen light left on because they would be coming back in the dark. The reading diary signed each Friday. The chair beside the bed. Edmund sitting down at the kitchen table when Ned asks him something. No single gesture is decisive. The pattern emerges from all of them together. Something that isn't any of the moments but comes from all of them.

---

## What to Do With It in This Story

Not too much. The idea is doing its work already — it changes something between Edmund and Ned in the car, and the reader will feel it without needing it explained. The best ideas in this kind of story are felt rather than argued.

But it should probably be returned to, briefly, in Edmund's private thinking — perhaps in a scene where he is at the stone alone, perhaps in the library late at night. Not an explanation. Just Edmund sitting with the idea, turning it over, aware that a ten-year-old boy arrived at something in five minutes that he has been circling for thirty years without quite landing on it.

Edmund's deepest thought — the one he has not yet articulated even to himself — may be that the right book about these stones is not a book about any single stone. It is a book about the pattern. About what emerges from the conversation between them. About the sacred geography that no one designed and everyone contributed to.

And that the person best placed to write that book is someone who can feel the conversation directly, not reconstruct it from research.

---

## For Future Stories or Later Movements

The idea has applications beyond this story:

- An older Ned returning to Somerset, walking between sites, mapping not the stones themselves but the relationships between them
- Edmund writing the book that turns out to be about the pattern rather than any single stone
- The murmuration itself as a recurring image — something that happens over the Levels every winter, the same sky, the same reed beds, five thousand years of winters
- The question of what happens to a sacred landscape when no one is paying attention to it anymore — and whether Ned's presence at Venn Court, his sensitivity, his mapping, constitutes a form of renewed attention that the landscape has been waiting for

These are not notes for immediate use. They are a place to come back to.
